# Banking Fluency — Day 006

## Title

Credit I: Underwriting and Expected Repayment

## Place in the course

Day 005 showed that a profit target becomes dangerous when risk appetite, capital, controls, and incentives do not constrain it. Credit underwriting is where that discipline becomes a decision on a particular loan: before booking revenue, the institution must form and document a credible view of how the borrower will repay.

Keep this bridge brief. Do not reteach ROA, ROE, capital planning, or incentive design.

## Learning objective

Explain underwriting as a forward-looking repayment judgment rather than a checklist; distinguish willingness to pay from capacity to pay; identify the primary source of repayment; test whether purpose, amount, structure, term, and price fit that source; and explain why mitigants cannot convert a loan with no credible repayment path into sound credit.

## The coherent layer: build the loan around expected repayment

A lender advances money now in exchange for contractual payments later. The central credit question is therefore not merely whether the borrower owns assets, has a high credit score, or accepts a high interest rate. It is:

> What identifiable source is expected to produce cash for principal and interest, under what assumptions, on what schedule, and with what margin for adverse change?

**Underwriting** is the process of gathering information, analyzing the borrower and transaction, identifying material risks, testing repayment under plausible conditions, structuring the exposure, deciding whether the risk fits policy and risk appetite, and documenting the basis for approval or decline.

The word **expected** matters. Underwriting cannot guarantee repayment. It forms a reasoned ex-ante judgment from available evidence. A sound approval identifies both the base-case repayment path and what could cause that path to fail.

**Simplification label:** This lesson uses a generic commercial-loan frame because it makes the repayment mechanism visible. Consumer, residential mortgage, agricultural, real-estate, asset-based, construction, project-finance, and other lending have product-specific laws, data, definitions, and supervisory expectations. Do not apply this pack as a complete underwriting policy.

## Start with purpose and the credit need

Before measuring repayment, understand why the borrower needs the money. Purpose determines what should repay the loan and how the exposure should be structured.

Examples:

- A seasonal retailer may borrow to acquire inventory, then repay as inventory is sold and receivables become cash.
- A manufacturer may finance equipment whose useful life and operating benefit extend over several years, making an amortizing term loan more coherent than a 90-day note.
- A business facing recurring operating deficits may describe its request as “working capital,” but permanent cash burn is not the same need as financing a self-liquidating operating cycle.
- A property acquisition may depend on rental cash flow, sale proceeds, or another defined repayment source; each creates different risks and structural needs.

A rigorous lender asks:

1. Is the purpose legal, permitted by policy, and economically intelligible?
2. Is the requested amount tied to a demonstrated need rather than the maximum collateral value available?
3. Is the need temporary, seasonal, recurring, or permanent?
4. What event or operating process will generate repayment?
5. Does the requested maturity match the timing of that process?

If the lender cannot state the purpose clearly, it is difficult to identify the right repayment source, amount, tenor, covenants, monitoring information, or warning signals.

## The primary source of repayment

The **primary source of repayment** is the cash-generating source the lender reasonably expects to pay the debt in the ordinary course of the transaction. Depending on the loan, it might be:

- cash flow from normal business operations;
- conversion of financed inventory and receivables into cash;
- rental or project cash flow;
- salary or other recurring personal income for an individual borrower;
- a contractually identified payment stream; or
- sale or refinancing proceeds when those are genuinely integral to the transaction and supported by credible assumptions.

The analysis should name the source specifically. “The business,” “collateral,” or “future growth” is not yet a repayment analysis. For an amortizing business loan, the more useful statement is: “Principal and interest are expected to be paid from recurring operating cash flow after operating needs, taxes, necessary capital expenditure, and other senior or pari passu debt obligations.”

The FDIC examination manual repeatedly connects sound underwriting to loan purpose, expected repayment source, borrower financial information, and repayment capacity. It also gives a product-specific but broadly useful warning in its agricultural discussion: collateral coverage matters, yet ordinary operating cash flow—not collateral—is generally the primary repayment source for intermediate- and long-term agricultural loans.

**Simplification label:** “Cash flow is primary” is not universal. In asset-based lending, orderly conversion of controlled receivables or inventory can itself be the intended repayment mechanism. Some transactions legitimately rely on asset sale, refinancing, guarantees, or other sources. The lender must define the actual structure rather than repeat a slogan.

## Capacity and willingness are different questions

Expected repayment requires both the ability and the intention to honor the obligation.

### Capacity to repay

**Capacity** asks whether the identified repayment source is sufficient in amount, timing, and reliability to meet contractual payments while the borrower continues operating.

For a business, analysis commonly draws on:

- historical income statements, balance sheets, and cash-flow information;
- tax returns, account activity, aging reports, and other records appropriate to the borrower;
- existing and proposed debt obligations;
- working-capital needs and cash-conversion timing;
- owner distributions, taxes, and necessary capital expenditures;
- customer, supplier, product, geographic, or revenue concentrations;
- management’s projections and the assumptions behind them;
- industry, competitive, legal, and macroeconomic conditions; and
- sensitivity to lower revenue, lower margins, higher costs, higher rates, delayed collections, or another relevant stress.

Historical performance anchors the analysis, but repayment is forward-looking. Projections should reconcile with the borrower’s operating reality, historical results, contracts, capacity, and external conditions. A spreadsheet that balances because revenue was increased is not evidence.

### Willingness to repay

**Willingness** asks whether the borrower and relevant principals have demonstrated reliability and an intention to honor obligations. Evidence can include credit history, payment behavior, transparency, responsiveness, treatment of prior creditors, integrity of information supplied, and conduct when circumstances deteriorated.

Willingness is not a substitute for capacity. A highly trustworthy borrower cannot pay indefinitely from cash that does not exist. Capacity is not a substitute for willingness either: a borrower with ample resources but deceptive information or a pattern of evasion can still present serious risk.

**Simplification label:** “Character” or willingness must not become intuition, affinity, or stereotyping. Decisions should rest on relevant, documented evidence and comply with fair-lending, consumer-protection, privacy, and other applicable requirements. This lesson does not supply those legal standards.

## Turn accounting information into repayment analysis

Net income is not automatically cash available for debt service. Income statements include noncash items; balance-sheet growth can consume cash; loan principal payments are generally not an income-statement expense; and a borrower must fund taxes, inventory, receivables, maintenance expenditure, and other obligations.

A simplified analytical bridge is:

> Cash available for debt service ≈ recurring operating cash generation − taxes − owner distributions − necessary capital expenditure − other required cash uses

A simplified debt-service coverage ratio is:

> DSCR = cash available for debt service ÷ required principal and interest payments

If simplified annual cash available for debt service is $600,000 and annual required principal and interest is $500,000, DSCR is 1.20 times. That represents a $100,000 modeled cushion—not a 20 percent probability of safety and not a guarantee.

The ratio is only as good as its definitions and inputs:

- Was a one-time gain treated as recurring cash flow?
- Were owner compensation and distributions normalized credibly?
- Were necessary maintenance expenditures deducted?
- Does debt service include all relevant existing and proposed obligations?
- Is a floating-rate loan tested at a higher rate?
- Does a seasonal annual total conceal a month when cash is insufficient?
- Are projected margins, collection periods, and growth assumptions plausible?

**Simplification label:** There is no universal DSCR numerator. Institutions and products define cash flow and debt service differently. EBITDA is not free cash flow, and a single threshold is not a complete credit decision. Use the institution’s approved policy and a definition appropriate to the borrower and facility.

## Worked case: a plausible business can still have an incoherent loan

A distributor requests a $1 million, five-year term loan. Management says the funds are for inventory ahead of a seasonal sales peak. The company has been profitable and owns a warehouse with substantial equity.

The request initially sounds strong, but purpose and structure do not align.

1. **Stated need:** seasonal inventory, expected to convert to receivables and then cash within six months.
2. **Requested structure:** a five-year loan with no required reduction tied to that conversion cycle.
3. **Repayment claim:** “Sales growth and the warehouse will cover it.”
4. **Missing evidence:** inventory aging, receivables aging, borrowing cycle, peak cash need, customer concentration, historical cleanup, and downside sensitivity.

A lender should not jump directly from valuable real estate to approval. It should determine the amount and duration of the seasonal need, verify the operating conversion cycle, and assess whether the line can reduce when cash arrives. A revolving facility with controls and an expected cleanup may fit the need better than a long amortizing loan—but only if verified cash conversion supports it.

Now change the facts. Suppose analysis shows that inventory remains elevated all year, receivables are lengthening, and operations have consumed cash for three consecutive years. The request may be financing permanent working capital or an operating deficit, not a temporary seasonal need. Renaming the loan does not change the economics. The lender must decide whether there is a credible long-term repayment path and an appropriate permanent-capital or term structure, or whether the risk should be declined.

The warehouse may be a secondary source or mitigant. It does not answer why ordinary repayment will occur.

## Structure should follow the repayment mechanism

Loan structure converts the credit thesis into enforceable timing and controls. Relevant elements include:

- **Amount:** limited to the supported financing need.
- **Maturity:** aligned with the expected life or conversion of the financed need.
- **Amortization:** principal reduction matched to expected cash generation or asset use.
- **Payment frequency:** fitted to monthly, seasonal, milestone, or other cash-flow timing.
- **Rate type:** fixed or floating, with repayment tested under relevant rate conditions.
- **Covenants:** selected to preserve assumptions, produce information, or trigger action—not added as decoration.
- **Reporting:** timely financial, collateral, compliance, or operating information needed to monitor the thesis.
- **Conditions and controls:** such as borrowing-base limits, advance rates, account controls, insurance, or other product-appropriate protections.

A mismatch creates risk even when the borrower appears profitable. A short maturity can manufacture refinancing dependence. A long interest-only period can postpone recognition that cash flow cannot amortize principal. A payment date before the seasonal cash inflow can create avoidable delinquency. A facility that can grow while the borrowing base deteriorates can turn a temporary need into permanent exposure.

Covenants and reporting do not create repayment capacity. They help preserve, observe, and respond to the conditions on which the approval depended.

## Pricing compensates for accepted risk; it does not repair repayment

Pricing should reflect the institution’s full economics: funding cost, operating and servicing cost, expected credit loss, capital usage, liquidity, option or commitment features, and a return consistent with strategy and competition.

But a higher interest rate has two limits:

1. It cannot make an unidentifiable repayment source credible.
2. It increases the borrower’s required debt service and can therefore weaken capacity.

A bank should not approve a structurally unsound loan merely because the yield is high. The sequence is: establish a credible repayment path; structure and mitigate the risk; determine whether the residual risk fits policy and appetite; then price the risk and resources consumed.

**Simplification label:** This is an economic sequence, not a complete loan-pricing model. It omits product-specific transfer pricing, expected-loss estimation, capital allocation, fees, optionality, taxes, competitive constraints, and legal limits.

## Approval is a documented hypothesis, not the end of underwriting

A useful approval memorandum should make the reasoning inspectable:

1. borrower, ownership, management, and relevant obligors;
2. purpose and supported amount;
3. primary and secondary repayment sources;
4. historical and projected repayment capacity;
5. material assumptions and sensitivities;
6. structure, collateral, guarantees, covenants, and reporting;
7. exceptions to policy and who approved them;
8. key risks and mitigants;
9. internal risk rating and rationale; and
10. monitoring triggers and actions if the thesis weakens.

The Federal Reserve’s SR 98-25 emphasizes that internal credit-risk ratings should meaningfully distinguish gradations of risk and support monitoring and management. The rating is not merely an origination label. New information—missed projections, declining liquidity, covenant breaches, adverse payment behavior, customer loss, management turnover, or weaker collateral—should cause reassessment.

This creates a disciplined lifecycle:

> Underwrite → approve or decline → document → monitor → re-rate → intervene, restructure, collect, or exit when warranted

**Simplification label:** A risk-rating system, adverse-classification framework, allowance methodology, and regulatory reporting each have distinct rules. “Expected repayment” in this lesson means the underwriting judgment about contractual repayment; it is not the accounting term “expected credit losses” under CECL.

## Institutional operator scenario

A relationship manager proposes a $2 million loan to a fast-growing services company. Revenue grew 40 percent last year, but customers take longer to pay. The founder wants the proceeds for hiring and a distribution. The proposal cites a strong personal credit score, a guarantee, and a rate 300 basis points above ordinary pricing. Forecast debt-service coverage is 1.35 times if revenue grows another 35 percent.

A rigorous operator should ask:

- What portion funds growth, and what portion leaves the business as a distribution?
- Which cash flow is expected to repay principal and interest?
- Do historical operations generate cash after working-capital growth, taxes, distributions, and necessary expenditure?
- What evidence supports 35 percent growth, stable margins, and timely collections?
- What happens to coverage if revenue growth is 10 percent, margins compress, receivables stretch, or rates rise?
- Is the maturity and amortization coherent with the use of proceeds and cash-flow timing?
- What does the guarantee add, and is guarantor liquidity independently verified?
- Does the higher rate improve compensation while simultaneously weakening debt-service capacity?
- What information and triggers will reveal early that the thesis is failing?
- Does the residual risk fit policy, risk appetite, concentration limits, and control capacity?

The correct outcome is not automatically approve or decline. The disciplined outcome is a decision whose repayment mechanism, assumptions, structure, mitigants, pricing, and monitoring all agree.

No Bitcoin or Galoy application is needed in this lesson. The institutional credit mechanism stands on its own.

## What must not be confused

1. **Underwriting versus a checklist:** documents are inputs; underwriting is the reasoned judgment connecting evidence to expected repayment.
2. **Purpose versus source of repayment:** purpose explains why funds are advanced; source explains what cash is expected to pay them back.
3. **Capacity versus willingness:** one concerns economic ability; the other concerns evidenced intent and reliability. Sound credit generally needs both.
4. **Revenue or net income versus cash available for debt service:** growth and accounting profit can coexist with cash consumption.
5. **Primary repayment versus collateral:** collateral or guarantees may reduce loss or provide another repayment path, but they do not automatically establish ordinary-course capacity.
6. **Structure versus mitigation:** good structure aligns payments and controls with the thesis; it cannot rescue a transaction whose base economics do not support repayment.
7. **Price versus credit quality:** extra yield may compensate for accepted risk, but it cannot create repayment and may raise payment burden.
8. **Expected repayment versus expected credit loss:** the first is today’s underwriting thesis; the second belongs to an accounting measurement framework covered later.
