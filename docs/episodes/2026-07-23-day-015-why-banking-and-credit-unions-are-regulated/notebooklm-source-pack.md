# Banking Fluency — Day 015

## Title

Reading the balance sheet I: assets, liabilities, and equity

## Place in the course

Day 014 showed why a product must be mapped to the exact legal entity that holds the authority, customer obligation, asset, record, and control duty. This lesson now opens the financial statements of that legal entity. The task is to read a bank balance sheet as a connected system of resources, claims, and residual ownership—not as a loose list of account names.

## Learning objective

Explain the accounting equation **assets = liabilities + equity** from a bank's perspective; classify the major balance-sheet accounts of an ordinary U.S. commercial bank; trace how a deposit, loan, payment, loss estimate, and retained profit change the statement; and use the balance sheet to ask better questions without confusing accounting value, market value, liquidity, solvency, or regulatory capital.

## The coherent layer: every asset is financed by a liability or by equity

A balance sheet is a financial position **at a point in time**. It reports:

- **Assets:** resources and rights controlled by the bank from which economic benefit is expected—cash, balances due from other institutions, securities, loans, premises, and other assets.
- **Liabilities:** present obligations and creditor claims against the bank—deposits, borrowings, amounts owed in transactions, and other liabilities.
- **Equity:** the residual accounting interest after liabilities are subtracted from assets.

The governing identity is:

> **Assets = Liabilities + Equity**

Equivalently:

> **Equity = Assets − Liabilities**

The two sides must balance because the right side explains how the resources on the left were financed. If a bank acquires an asset, it must ordinarily:

1. incur or increase a liability;
2. use or reduce another asset;
3. receive equity from owners;
4. retain earnings that increase equity; or
5. combine those effects.

This identity is mechanical, but its interpretation requires judgment. It does not say that all assets are safe, liquid, or worth their recorded amounts. It does not say that all liabilities mature at once. It does not say that accounting equity equals cash, sale value, or regulatory capital. It says that a complete set of recognized balances must reconcile.

**Simplification label:** This lesson uses a simplified U.S. commercial-bank balance sheet and ordinary double-entry examples. It is educational, not accounting, audit, tax, legal, valuation, supervisory, capital, liquidity, or investment advice. Actual reporting follows U.S. generally accepted accounting principles, regulatory reporting instructions, consolidation rules, institution-specific elections, and detailed classifications. Credit unions, trust institutions, foreign banking offices, broker-dealers, bank holding companies, and Canadian institutions use related but not identical statements and terminology. Verify the reporting entity, reporting date, accounting basis, Call Report form, schedules, footnotes, and current official instructions.

## First discipline: choose the reporting entity and date

Before reading any number, identify two boundaries.

### 1. Which entity does the statement cover?

A banking group can contain a parent holding company, one or more banks, broker-dealers, trust companies, insurance entities, technology subsidiaries, and unconsolidated affiliates. Their statements are not interchangeable.

A parent-company debt obligation may not be a liability of the subsidiary bank. Cash at the bank may not be freely available to the parent. A customer deposit belongs on the books of the depository institution that owes it, not automatically on the books of the app or holding company whose brand the customer sees.

Ask:

- Is this the bank-only Call Report or the consolidated holding-company statement?
- Are subsidiaries consolidated?
- Are intercompany balances eliminated?
- Does the legal entity match the entity that holds the deposit, loan, or contract being analyzed?

### 2. At what date was the position measured?

A balance sheet is a snapshot. A December 31 balance cannot by itself reveal every inflow, outflow, peak exposure, funding strain, or loss that occurred during the year. Window dressing, seasonal deposits, settlement timing, acquisitions, sales, and quarter-end transactions can change the snapshot.

The income statement covers activity **over a period**. The cash-flow statement explains changes in cash over a period. The balance sheet shows the resulting stocks at a particular date. Read all three together when making a serious assessment.

## The asset side: where the bank has deployed resources

A bank asset is an economic resource or claim of the bank. In an ordinary commercial bank, the largest categories often include cash assets, securities, and loans. The Federal Reserve's H.8 release groups commercial-bank assets into categories including bank credit, securities, loans and leases, cash assets, federal funds sold and reverse repurchase agreements, loans to commercial banks, and other assets.

### 1. Cash and balances due from banks

This category can include vault cash, reserve balances or other balances at Federal Reserve Banks, balances due from depository institutions, and cash items in process of collection, depending on the statement and instructions.

Cash assets support:

- customer withdrawals and payments;
- interbank settlement;
- reserve and clearing needs;
- collateral and margin calls;
- operating expenses; and
- unexpected liquidity demands.

Cash is usually more liquid than a long-term loan, but the label still requires inspection. Some balances can be restricted, pledged, operationally trapped, in transit, or exposed to another institution.

**Do not confuse:** “cash asset” with “profit.” A bank can hold abundant cash while losing money, or report profit while facing a cash-timing problem.

### 2. Securities

Banks hold securities for liquidity, income, risk management, collateral, and other authorized purposes. Common holdings include U.S. Treasury securities, agency securities, mortgage-backed securities, municipal securities, and other debt instruments.

The recorded amount can depend on classification and accounting rules. Some debt securities are carried at fair value with unrealized changes recognized through earnings or other comprehensive income; others can be carried at amortized cost subject to applicable credit-loss rules. Maturity, duration, optionality, pledging, market depth, and unrealized gains or losses affect what the same headline amount means economically.

A $100 security balance is therefore not automatically equivalent to $100 of immediately spendable cash. Selling it may crystallize a gain or loss; using it as collateral may be possible; holding it may produce future cash flows; and its value can change as interest rates, credit conditions, and market liquidity change.

**Simplification label:** Security classification, fair-value measurement, accumulated other comprehensive income, credit losses, hedge accounting, and regulatory-capital treatment are deliberately compressed here. The OCC's Bank Accounting Advisory Series discusses these topics in much more detail.

### 3. Loans and leases

A loan is an asset of the bank because the borrower owes principal and interest under a contract. The same instrument is a liability of the borrower. This is the mirror image of a deposit: the deposit is an asset of the customer and a liability of the bank.

Loan categories can include:

- commercial and industrial loans;
- commercial real-estate loans;
- residential mortgages;
- construction and land-development loans;
- credit-card and other consumer loans;
- agricultural loans;
- loans to financial institutions; and
- lease-financing receivables.

The face amount is not the full economic story. A reader must consider:

- contractual balance and maturity;
- accrued interest;
- unearned income, deferred fees, and costs;
- nonaccrual status;
- collateral and guarantees;
- expected prepayment;
- delinquency and modification;
- interest-rate structure;
- concentration; and
- the allowance for credit losses.

Regulatory and public data often present loans **net of an allowance for credit losses**, or show gross loans and the allowance separately. The allowance is a contra-asset: it reduces the net carrying amount of the loan portfolio to reflect estimated credit losses under the applicable accounting framework.

**Do not confuse:** a loan asset with cash already received. The bank owns a contractual claim whose future cash flows depend on borrower performance, collateral, legal enforceability, servicing, and economic conditions.

### 4. Trading assets, reverse repurchase agreements, and other financial claims

A larger or more complex bank can have trading assets, derivatives with positive fair values, securities purchased under agreements to resell, receivables, and other market-related claims. Their economic meaning depends heavily on netting, collateral, counterparty exposure, market value, settlement, and contract terms.

A reverse repurchase agreement is economically a secured financing asset: the bank advances cash and receives securities under an agreement that the counterparty will repurchase them. Legal form, accounting, collateral margin, and counterparty risk still matter.

### 5. Premises, equipment, goodwill, servicing assets, deferred tax assets, and other assets

Not every asset is a loan or tradable instrument. A bank may recognize:

- land, buildings, leasehold improvements, and equipment;
- accrued interest and fees receivable;
- repossessed real estate;
- mortgage-servicing rights;
- goodwill and other intangible assets from acquisitions;
- bank-owned life-insurance assets;
- deferred tax assets; and
- prepaid expenses and other receivables.

These items differ sharply in liquidity and loss behavior. A branch building supports operations but cannot settle a withdrawal without sale or financing. Goodwill can be an accounting asset but does not generate a contractual payment and can be impaired. A deferred tax asset depends on tax rules and expected realization. “Other assets” deserves explanation precisely because heterogeneous exposures can accumulate there.

## The liability side: who finances the bank and what the bank owes

A liability is an obligation of the bank. In the Federal Reserve's H.8 presentation, major commercial-bank liabilities include deposits, borrowings, amounts due to related foreign offices, and other liabilities including trading liabilities.

### 1. Deposits

Deposits are usually the central funding liability of an ordinary commercial bank. The bank owes the customer under the account agreement. Deposit categories differ by:

- transaction versus savings purpose;
- demand versus contractual maturity;
- interest-bearing versus noninterest-bearing status;
- retail, commercial, brokered, reciprocal, municipal, or other source;
- insured versus uninsured amount;
- operational versus rate-sensitive behavior; and
- concentration by depositor, industry, geography, or channel.

Two banks can report the same total deposits but have very different economics and liquidity risk. Stable operating accounts from many customers are not behaviorally identical to a few large uninsured deposits gathered through a digital channel or rate-sensitive time deposits approaching maturity.

The balance sheet reports the obligation. It does not by itself reveal when customers will exercise withdrawal rights or how expensive replacement funding would be.

### 2. Borrowings

Borrowings can include federal funds purchased, repurchase agreements, Federal Home Loan Bank advances, central-bank credit, subordinated debt, secured facilities, unsecured notes, and other wholesale funding, depending on the institution and reporting classification.

For every borrowing, ask:

- Who is the creditor?
- When does it mature or reprice?
- Is it secured, and by what collateral?
- Can the creditor demand more collateral or decline renewal?
- What covenants, triggers, or haircuts apply?
- Is the instrument debt for accounting purposes but eligible for some form of regulatory-capital recognition?

“Borrowing” is not one homogeneous source of funds. A short-term secured borrowing that can disappear tomorrow creates a different risk from long-dated subordinated debt.

### 3. Trading, derivative, settlement, and other liabilities

Banks can owe counterparties for derivatives with negative fair values, securities sold under agreements to repurchase, trading positions, unsettled transactions, taxes, employee benefits, accrued expenses, commitments recognized under accounting rules, and many other obligations.

As on the asset side, “other liabilities” should prompt decomposition. Gross and net presentation, collateral, close-out rights, legal enforceability, and settlement timing can materially alter the exposure.

### 4. What is not automatically a liability of the bank

Client assets held in a fiduciary, custody, or agency capacity are not automatically proprietary assets of the bank, and the full client position is not automatically a deposit liability. The governing legal capacity, asset title, cash arrangement, accounting rules, and reporting instructions determine recognition.

Likewise, an undrawn loan commitment is not ordinarily reported as if the full future loan had already become an on-balance-sheet asset. It can still create credit, liquidity, operational, and capital exposure and may require an allowance for expected credit losses. Off-balance-sheet does not mean outside risk management.

## The equity section: the residual, not a pile of cash

Equity is the residual accounting interest after recognized liabilities are subtracted from recognized assets. It can include common stock, additional paid-in capital or surplus, retained earnings, accumulated other comprehensive income, and other components under the applicable reporting framework.

### Common stock and contributed capital

When owners contribute capital, the bank receives an asset—often cash—and records an increase in equity. The exact legal and accounting accounts depend on the instrument and structure.

### Retained earnings

Retained earnings accumulate net income that the institution has not distributed, subject to accounting adjustments. If the bank earns income greater than expenses and dividends, retained earnings generally rise. If it reports a net loss or pays dividends, retained earnings generally fall.

Retained earnings are not a separate vault of retained cash. The economic resources generated by prior earnings may now be embodied in loans, securities, cash, premises, or other assets. Equity identifies the residual claim; it does not identify where a particular dollar is physically held.

### Accumulated other comprehensive income

Some gains and losses bypass current net income and enter equity through other comprehensive income under the applicable accounting rules—for example, certain unrealized changes associated with available-for-sale securities. This can make equity move even when no security has been sold and the change is not part of current net income.

### Why residual does not mean unimportant

If a bank has assets of 110 and liabilities of 100, its accounting equity is 10. If recognized asset value falls by 4 with no offsetting liability reduction, equity falls to 6. The liability claimholders still have claims totaling 100; the residual absorbs the accounting reduction.

That loss-absorption logic is important, but the next lesson will distinguish accounting equity from regulatory capital and show why capital adequacy cannot be judged from the simple equation alone.

**Simplification label:** Accounting equity, tangible common equity, book value, market capitalization, regulatory capital, and loss-absorbing capacity are not synonyms. Regulatory deductions, risk weights, capital instruments, minority interests, accumulated other comprehensive income rules, prompt-corrective-action categories, and holding-company structures are outside this lesson.

## Five transactions that make the equation visible

Use a stripped-down bank with this opening balance sheet:

| Assets | Amount | Liabilities and equity | Amount |
|---|---:|---|---:|
| Cash and reserve balances | 20 | Customer deposits | 85 |
| Securities | 30 | Borrowings | 5 |
| Loans, net | 60 | Equity | 20 |
| **Total assets** | **110** | **Total liabilities + equity** | **110** |

These examples omit taxes, fees, accrued interest, required allowances, settlement lags, and other real-world entries unless stated.

### Transaction 1: a customer deposits 10 in external cash

The bank receives cash and owes the customer the same amount.

- Cash asset: **+10**
- Deposit liability: **+10**

New totals:

- Assets: 120
- Liabilities: 100
- Equity: 20

The bank did not earn 10. It exchanged one form of claim: it acquired cash and issued a deposit liability.

If the “deposit” arrives by transfer from another bank rather than physical currency, the receiving bank's settlement balance rises and its deposit liability rises. The sending bank experiences the opposite settlement and deposit movements.

### Transaction 2: the bank makes a 10 loan and credits the borrower's deposit account

At origination in this simplified example:

- Loan asset: **+10**
- Deposit liability: **+10**

New totals:

- Assets: 130
- Liabilities: 110
- Equity: 20

The loan creates an asset for the bank and a liability for the borrower. Crediting the deposit creates a liability for the bank and an asset for the borrower. The bank's balance sheet expands on both sides.

This does **not** mean lending is unconstrained or costless. The bank must satisfy underwriting, pricing, concentration, liquidity, capital, legal, operational, and supervisory constraints. If the borrower transfers the proceeds to another bank, the originating bank generally loses a settlement asset and the associated deposit liability leaves; the loan remains. The bank must fund and manage the resulting balance sheet.

**Simplification label:** Actual loan accounting can include fees, costs, discounts, commitments, collateral, participations, fair-value elections, purchase accounting, and an allowance for credit losses. The example isolates the initial balance-sheet logic.

### Transaction 3: a depositor sends 8 to another bank

Assume final interbank settlement occurs.

- Cash or reserve asset: **−8**
- Deposit liability: **−8**

Both sides contract by 8. Equity does not change merely because a valid withdrawal or payment is honored.

This demonstrates why deposit funding and liquid assets must be read together. The liability tells you what the bank owes; the asset mix helps show how the bank can meet outflows.

### Transaction 4: expected credit losses rise by 3

Assume the bank records a 3 provision expense and increases its allowance by 3.

- Net loans: **−3** through the contra-asset allowance
- Current-period expense: **+3**, reducing net income
- Retained earnings/equity: **−3**, after the simplified income-statement close

Assets and equity both fall by 3. Liabilities do not change merely because management's estimate of expected loan losses changes.

A later charge-off normally reduces the gross loan balance and the allowance. If the loss was already provided for, the charge-off itself is not a second identical hit to expense. Recoveries and subsequent estimate changes follow the applicable accounting framework.

**Simplification label:** The allowance for credit losses under ASC Topic 326 requires portfolio-specific estimation, reasonable and supportable forecasts, historical experience, qualitative adjustments, governance, and disclosures. Timing and entries can differ for debt securities and off-balance-sheet credit exposures. Day 007 introduced reserves and charge-offs; this example only locates their balance-sheet effect.

### Transaction 5: the bank earns 2 after all expenses and retains it

Assume net income ultimately produces 2 of additional cash or other net assets and no dividend is paid.

- Assets: **+2**
- Retained earnings/equity: **+2**

If the bank instead pays a dividend of 1 in cash:

- Cash asset: **−1**
- Retained earnings/equity: **−1**

Profit can strengthen the residual claim, while losses and distributions reduce it. But profit and cash are not identical: accrual accounting can recognize income or expense before cash is received or paid.

## A practical method for reading a real bank balance sheet

### Step 1: prove the equation

Confirm that total assets equal total liabilities plus total equity. If a presentation uses “liabilities and stockholders' equity” as one total, reconcile the components. A failure to balance usually means a transcription, unit, consolidation, or classification error—not a novel bank.

### Step 2: mark the unit and scale

Determine whether amounts are dollars, thousands, millions, or billions. Separate absolute size from mix. A 5 billion loan portfolio means something different at a 6 billion bank than at a 100 billion bank.

### Step 3: convert major accounts to percentages of total assets

For example:

- cash / total assets;
- securities / total assets;
- loans / total assets;
- deposits / total assets;
- borrowings / total assets; and
- equity / total assets.

These are orientation ratios, not conclusions. They reveal the balance-sheet shape and where to investigate.

### Step 4: compare multiple dates

Measure both dollar and percentage changes. Ask what financed asset growth. If loans rose by 20, did deposits, wholesale borrowings, asset sales, or equity also change? If deposits fell, did cash fall, securities get sold, borrowings rise, or the whole balance sheet contract?

A coherent explanation must connect both sides.

### Step 5: distinguish gross, allowance, and net values

For loans and some other assets, identify:

- gross contractual or amortized-cost amount;
- allowance or valuation adjustment; and
- net carrying amount.

Do not compare one bank's gross number with another bank's net number.

### Step 6: separate liquidity from accounting value

Classify assets by how quickly and reliably they can generate cash under ordinary and stressed conditions. Classify liabilities by withdrawal rights, maturity, repricing, concentration, collateral, and renewal dependence.

A balanced statement can still contain a severe timing mismatch.

### Step 7: identify valuation bases

Ask which amounts are carried at amortized cost, fair value, lower of cost or fair value, net realizable value, or another basis. Review unrealized gains and losses and the footnotes. Book value is produced by accounting rules, not by pretending every asset can be sold at that amount today.

### Step 8: inspect “other,” intangible, pledged, and off-balance-sheet exposures

Large residual categories require decomposition. Also ask which assets are pledged, encumbered, restricted, or operationally unavailable. Review commitments, guarantees, letters of credit, derivatives, fiduciary assets, securitizations, and servicing obligations that do not appear as simple funded assets or liabilities.

### Step 9: connect the snapshot to earnings and risk

The balance sheet supplies the stock of earning assets and funding liabilities. The income statement shows interest income, interest expense, noninterest income, noninterest expense, provisions, taxes, and net income over time. The mix, price, maturity, quality, and optionality of balance-sheet accounts drive future earnings and risk.

### Step 10: write one causal sentence

A useful first-pass conclusion has this structure:

> The bank increased **[asset category]**, financed primarily by **[liability or equity category]**, which may improve **[income or strategic outcome]** but increases exposure to **[credit, liquidity, interest-rate, market, operational, or concentration risk]**; the decisive follow-up evidence is **[schedule, footnote, maturity table, deposit data, valuation data, or control report]**.

If you cannot name the financing source, you have read only half the balance sheet.

## Worked operator scenario

A bank announces that its balance sheet grew 25 percent in one year and that its digital-account program was a major contributor. Its summary data show:

- loans grew rapidly;
- cash declined as a percentage of assets;
- securities were roughly flat in dollars;
- deposits grew, concentrated in large technology-company operating accounts;
- short-term borrowings also rose;
- the allowance increased, but more slowly than loans;
- accounting equity rose modestly through retained earnings; and
- the bank reports undrawn commitments to many of the same deposit customers.

A weak analysis says: “Growth is strong because both loans and deposits increased.”

A disciplined balance-sheet analysis asks:

1. **Entity:** Are the deposits and loans on the same insured bank's books, or is the announcement mixing the holding company, fintech interface, and bank?
2. **Asset financing:** How much loan growth was financed by deposits, borrowings, reduced cash, security sales, or equity?
3. **Deposit behavior:** How many customers account for the increase? How much is uninsured, rate-sensitive, or contractually withdrawable on demand?
4. **Double exposure:** Are the large depositors also borrowers or holders of undrawn commitments, creating correlated drawdown and withdrawal risk?
5. **Liquidity:** If deposits leave and borrowers draw commitments together, which unencumbered assets can generate cash, at what speed and loss?
6. **Credit measurement:** Did underwriting, portfolio mix, loss forecasts, or charge-offs change? Why did the allowance grow more slowly than loans?
7. **Equity pace:** Did retained earnings keep pace with asset growth, before making any conclusion about regulatory capital?
8. **Valuation:** Do securities or other assets contain unrealized losses or valuation uncertainty not obvious from the headline totals?
9. **Borrowing dependence:** What are the maturity, collateral, covenant, and renewal terms of the new short-term funding?
10. **Off-balance-sheet claims:** How large are commitments, guarantees, derivatives, and operational obligations relative to available liquidity and equity?

The balance sheet does not answer all ten questions. It tells you which questions the institution's structure makes urgent.

No Bitcoin or Galoy application is needed. Reading the regulated institution's own books is the foundation.

## What must not be confused

1. **Bank asset versus customer asset:** A deposit is the customer's asset but the bank's liability; a loan is the bank's asset but the borrower's liability.
2. **Asset versus something good:** An asset can be illiquid, impaired, volatile, encumbered, operationally inaccessible, or wrongly valued.
3. **Liability versus something bad:** Deposits are liabilities and can be valuable, stable funding relationships.
4. **Equity versus cash:** Equity is a residual accounting interest, not a segregated cash reserve.
5. **Balanced equation versus safe bank:** Every complete balance sheet balances, including one with poor credit, unstable funding, or inadequate capital.
6. **Book value versus market value:** Recorded amount depends on accounting rules and may differ from sale value.
7. **Balance sheet versus income statement:** The former is a point-in-time stock; the latter measures flows over a period.
8. **Deposit inflow versus revenue:** Receiving a deposit increases an asset and a liability; it is not itself income.
9. **Loan origination versus immediate profit:** Origination creates a loan asset and related funding effects; income recognition follows separate rules.
10. **Loan balance versus expected collection:** Allowances, delinquency, collateral, and borrower performance affect net value.
11. **Allowance versus cash reserve:** The allowance is a contra-asset accounting estimate, not cash set aside in a vault.
12. **Provision versus charge-off:** Provision expense adjusts the allowance; a charge-off uses the allowance against a loan balance.
13. **Cash versus liquidity:** Cash is highly liquid, but liquidity also depends on outflows, market access, collateral, encumbrance, and timing.
14. **Deposits versus one funding type:** Deposit stability, cost, insurance status, concentration, and withdrawal rights vary.
15. **Borrowing versus permanent funding:** Maturity, collateral, repricing, and renewal determine its behavior.
16. **Off-balance-sheet versus no exposure:** Commitments, guarantees, derivatives, servicing, and fiduciary activities can create material risk.
17. **Bank statement versus holding-company statement:** Entity boundaries can change assets, liabilities, creditors, capital, and available cash.
18. **Accounting equity versus regulatory capital:** They overlap but are not identical; the next lesson develops the distinction.
19. **Asset growth versus strength:** Growth can increase earnings capacity while stretching funding, liquidity, controls, credit quality, and capital.
20. **One date versus a causal story:** Trends, transactions, notes, and related statements are needed to explain the snapshot.

These sources anchor the lesson. The accounting equation, simplified entries, reading method, and operator scenario above are educational synthesis—not agency accounting instructions, examination conclusions, valuation determinations, or capital calculations.

1. **Federal Financial Institutions Examination Council — FFIEC 041, current information and reporting materials.** Official reporting page for the Consolidated Reports of Condition and Income used by eligible institutions. Schedule RC is the balance sheet; supporting schedules decompose cash, securities, loans, deposits, borrowings, equity, and other accounts.  
   https://www.ffiec.gov/resources/reporting-forms/ffiec041

2. **Federal Financial Institutions Examination Council — Instructions for Preparation of Consolidated Reports of Condition and Income, FFIEC 031 and FFIEC 041.** Primary line-item and general instructions for regulatory reporting, including consolidation, reporting date, Schedule RC classifications, loan and deposit reporting, allowances, liabilities, and bank equity capital.  
   https://www.ffiec.gov/sites/default/files/data/reporting-forms/ffiec031-ffiec041-call-report-preparation-intructions.pdf

3. **Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System — H.8, “Assets and Liabilities of Commercial Banks in the United States.”** Official weekly aggregate presentation. It shows the practical asset structure—bank credit, securities, loans and leases net of allowance, cash assets, and other assets—and the liability structure—deposits, borrowings, related-office balances, and other liabilities—with a residual equal to assets less liabilities.  
   https://www.federalreserve.gov/releases/h8/current/default.htm

4. **Office of the Comptroller of the Currency — Bank Accounting Advisory Series, August 2025.** Official OCC staff interpretations of accounting topics relevant to national banks and federal savings associations. Covers investment securities, loans, leases, other assets, liabilities, capital, transfers of financial assets, fair value, derivatives, and credit losses. The OCC states that the series interprets accounting topics but is not itself an OCC rule or regulation.  
   https://www.occ.treas.gov/publications-and-resources/publications/banker-education/files/pub-bank-accounting-advisory-series.pdf

5. **Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation — BankFind Suite, Financial & Regulatory Reporting.** Official institution-level tool for reviewing balance sheets, income statements, performance and condition ratios, demographic data, and reports across dates. Use it to test the reading method against the exact insured bank rather than a brand or parent-company summary.  
   https://banks.data.fdic.gov/bankfind-suite/financialreporting

6. **Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation — Quarterly Banking Profile.** Official aggregate reporting on FDIC-insured institutions, with tables and analysis connecting changes in loans, securities, deposits, borrowings, equity, income, credit quality, and other balance-sheet measures.  
   https://www.fdic.gov/quarterly-banking-profile
