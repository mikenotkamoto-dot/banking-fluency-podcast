# Banking Fluency — Day 011

## Title

Liquidity and solvency III: bank runs and contagion

## Place in the course

Day 010 explained how a bank builds a resilient funding profile and converts cash, unencumbered liquid assets, collateral, and borrowing facilities into usable liquidity. This lesson adds the next layer only: what happens when withdrawal demand accelerates faster than those sources can be mobilized, and how stress at one institution can change behavior and prices across other institutions.

## Learning objective

Explain a bank run as a dynamic interaction among runnable liabilities, depositor incentives, information, confidence, and the bank’s finite capacity to produce cash; trace how a run can damage an initially viable bank; distinguish several contagion channels; and identify the ex ante controls and crisis decisions that can slow the feedback loop without confusing communication, liquidity support, deposit insurance, and loss absorption.

## The coherent layer: a bank run is a race between withdrawals and cash-producing capacity

A bank funds long-lived assets partly with liabilities that customers can withdraw now or soon. That maturity transformation is economically useful, but it creates a timing vulnerability. No ordinary bank keeps immediately available cash equal to every potentially runnable claim while also performing its lending and investment functions.

A **bank run** occurs when withdrawal or non-renewal demand accelerates so sharply that the institution cannot meet it through ordinary cash flows and funding operations. Management must then mobilize contingency liquidity, sell or pledge assets, replace funding at higher cost, seek capital, pursue a transaction, or enter an applicable supervisory and resolution process.

The decisive variable is not merely the total amount withdrawn. It is the relationship among:

- the amount and timing of outflows;
- the concentration and behavior of the funding base;
- immediately available cash;
- assets that can be sold or pledged by the relevant deadline;
- collateral eligibility, haircuts, encumbrance, and operational readiness;
- the bank’s condition and counterparties’ willingness to provide funds;
- realized losses and funding costs created by the response; and
- whether credible public and private information restores or further damages confidence.

A slow runoff can sometimes be managed through normal balance-sheet adjustment. The same total outflow compressed into hours can overwhelm payment operations, collateral movement, approvals, and borrowing facilities. Speed changes the problem.

**Simplification label:** This pack uses a generic U.S. commercial-bank frame. It is educational, not legal, accounting, supervisory, deposit-insurance, monetary-policy, communications, recovery, or resolution advice. “Bank run,” “contagion,” “solvent,” “systemic,” and “insured” can have context-specific legal, regulatory, and analytical meanings. Actual outcomes depend on the institution, jurisdiction, depositor, account ownership, applicable insurance rules, charter, collateral, market conditions, and authorities available at the time.

## Why withdrawal can become individually rational and collectively destructive

A depositor’s decision is not made in isolation. It depends partly on what the depositor expects other claimants to do.

Consider an uninsured or otherwise exposed depositor who believes the bank may have enough long-run asset value to repay creditors, but not enough same-day cash to satisfy everyone if many depositors leave at once. Waiting has a possible cost: earlier claimants may receive immediate payment at par while later claimants face delay, uncertainty, or loss in a failure process. Moving first can therefore appear prudent even when the depositor is unsure that the bank is insolvent.

This creates a feedback mechanism:

> Concern about the bank → expectation that others may withdraw → incentive to withdraw before them → actual outflows → visible liquidity strain → stronger concern

The mechanism does not require every depositor to possess the same facts or reach the same valuation. Depositors can react to:

- credible evidence of asset losses, weak capital, poor governance, or funding concentration;
- uncertainty about information the bank has not explained;
- observed withdrawals by informed or influential customers;
- a failed capital raise, asset sale, rating action, earnings disclosure, or supervisory event;
- news about another institution that appears similar;
- inaccurate information or rumors; or
- the belief that other depositors will react to any of the above.

The FDIC’s 2023 report on deposit-insurance reform states that deposit withdrawals can force asset liquidation and make failure a self-fulfilling prophecy. That does **not** mean every run lacks fundamentals. A run can reveal genuine weaknesses, accelerate a failure that losses already made likely, destroy an institution that could have remained viable with time, or combine all three.

A rigorous diagnosis therefore separates two questions:

1. **Why did depositors begin to question the bank?** This can involve real asset, capital, governance, concentration, or operational weaknesses.
2. **Why did the concern become a rapid collective withdrawal?** This involves depositor exposure, incentives, common information channels, ease of transfer, and expectations about other depositors.

Calling a run “pure panic” can conceal real weaknesses. Calling every run proof of insolvency confuses an immediate collective-action problem with a completed valuation analysis.

**Simplification label:** The depositor-incentive explanation is a teaching model, not a claim that all deposits have equal legal priority, all withdrawals settle instantly, or all depositors are financially sophisticated. Payment cutoffs, account controls, insurance coverage, sweep arrangements, collateralization, resolution rules, and operational frictions differ.

## Runnable funding is behavioral, not just contractual

A liability is more runnable when its holder can and is motivated to demand payment quickly. Four dimensions matter.

### 1. Exposure to loss or delay

Deposit insurance can reduce the insured depositor’s incentive to run because covered funds are protected subject to applicable limits and rules. Uninsured balances have more direct exposure to the bank’s failure and can react faster to signs of distress.

Insurance status is not a complete behavioral forecast. Insured customers can move for service, convenience, access, or rate reasons. Uninsured customers may retain operational balances because moving payroll, payments, credit, custody, or treasury relationships is costly. The operator needs account-level facts and customer purpose.

### 2. Concentration

A bank can have thousands of accounts but only a small number of independent withdrawal decisions. Balances may be influenced by the same corporate treasurer, investment firm, venture network, wealth adviser, platform, broker, municipality, industry, or social group.

Concentration amplifies both size and speed. One decision can move a large balance; one recommendation can coordinate many nominally separate accounts.

### 3. Information network

Depositors connected through shared investors, advisers, executives, online communities, or industry channels can receive and act on the same signal nearly simultaneously. Social media does not create the bank’s balance-sheet weakness, but it can compress the time between a concern, its interpretation, and coordinated action.

### 4. Transfer technology and operational access

Online and mobile banking, faster payments, remote approvals, and always-available information reduce the physical and administrative friction once associated with moving funds. The FDIC observed that information or misinformation can spread rapidly and that funds can be transferred overnight and on weekends. The Basel Committee similarly noted that digital access and faster payment and settlement services facilitate deposit movement.

Technology therefore changes stress assumptions. Historical runoff observed when transfers required branch visits, telephone instructions, or slower settlement may understate current speed. A bank should not solve this by assuming that every dollar leaves instantly; it should segment the funding base and test severe, plausible speeds for its actual customers and channels.

## The run sequence: how cash stress becomes balance-sheet damage

A run unfolds through connected stages. The stages can overlap or occur in a different order, but the causal structure is useful.

### Stage 1: a vulnerability already exists

Common vulnerabilities include:

- concentrated uninsured or rate-sensitive deposits;
- rapid growth without matching controls;
- long-duration or difficult-to-sell assets;
- material unrealized or unrecognized economic losses;
- weak interest-rate, credit, or concentration-risk management;
- heavy collateral encumbrance;
- dependence on short-term wholesale renewal;
- contingency facilities that have not been documented or tested; and
- governance that delays recognition and escalation.

A trigger is not the underlying vulnerability. A disclosure, rumor, rating action, failed financing, peer-bank failure, or large withdrawal may ignite the run, but the severity depends on the structure in place before the trigger.

### Stage 2: withdrawals exceed ordinary inflows

The bank first uses immediately available cash and incoming payments. Treasury and operations teams then mobilize securities, collateral, and borrowing facilities. If outflows arrive faster than usable sources, the bank’s survival horizon contracts.

Management needs a live view of cleared balances, pending withdrawals, payment deadlines, collateral location, settlement status, available borrowing after haircut, legal-entity constraints, and customer concentration. A daily report may become obsolete within hours.

### Stage 3: the bank takes increasingly costly actions

The bank may:

- sell marketable securities;
- pledge unencumbered assets;
- replace deposits with wholesale borrowing;
- raise deposit rates;
- slow asset growth or new lending;
- seek an equity injection, merger, or asset sale; or
- request liquidity from an eligible official facility under its rules.

Each action changes the next decision. Asset sales create cash but can realize losses. Secured borrowing creates cash but encumbers collateral. Expensive replacement funding weakens earnings. Using the best collateral first can leave fewer options later. A public capital raise can strengthen the bank if completed, but an unsuccessful attempt can intensify uncertainty.

### Stage 4: liquidity stress can impair solvency

The feedback chain is:

> Withdrawals → asset sales and emergency funding → realized losses and higher interest expense → weaker earnings and capital → lower collateral and market confidence → reduced funding access → more withdrawals

An initially solvent bank can become impaired if forced-sale losses and funding costs consume its equity. Conversely, if the bank was already economically insolvent, liquidity merely determines how quickly the loss becomes impossible to defer. The operator must continue running liquidity and solvency analysis in parallel.

### Stage 5: operational limits become binding

A bank can have theoretical collateral and still fail to produce cash before the deadline. Obstacles include:

- assets not legally pledged or pre-positioned;
- incomplete loan files or rejected collateral;
- untested settlement accounts;
- stale valuations;
- missing approvals or unclear authority;
- incompatible systems and data;
- cutoffs, weekends, or time zones;
- staff capacity; and
- duplicate use of the same collateral across projected sources.

The 2023 Basel Committee report describes Signature Bank attempting during its failure weekend to pledge loans that had not been accepted as eligible collateral, even though management had continued including the portfolio in collateral calculations. The lesson is narrow and operational: potential collateral is not emergency cash until eligibility and execution are established.

**Simplification label:** This sequence is not a prediction that every run follows five discrete stages. It omits many accounting, legal, intraday-payment, supervisory, monetary-policy, resolution, and cross-border details. It also does not imply that official liquidity is automatic or that liquidity lending protects shareholders or creditors from underlying losses.

## Case anchor: Silicon Valley Bank and the compression of time

The Federal Reserve’s April 2023 review identified a combination of fundamental weaknesses and exceptionally rapid outflows at Silicon Valley Bank. It concluded that the bank’s management failed to manage basic interest-rate and liquidity risk, its board failed to provide effective oversight, and supervisors did not act with sufficient force and urgency.

The bank had a highly concentrated business model, substantial reliance on uninsured deposits, and material interest-rate exposure. On March 8, 2023, it announced that it had sold securities at a loss and intended to raise capital. It then faced a depositor run on March 9. The Federal Reserve review reports deposit outflows above $40 billion that day and management’s expectation of another $100 billion the following day. California closed the bank on March 10.

The report highlights three connected accelerants:

1. a concentrated and highly networked depositor base;
2. social media that spread concern quickly; and
3. technology that enabled immediate withdrawal instructions.

The case should not be reduced to “social media caused a bank failure.” The official review identifies failures in asset-liability management, liquidity risk, governance, regulation, and supervision. Nor should the role of speed be minimized: a contingency plan built for slower runoff can fail even if its source totals look adequate on a static report.

The correct operator question is:

> Which structural vulnerabilities made depositors sensitive to the trigger, which network and technology features coordinated their behavior, and which usable liquidity and operational controls failed to match the resulting speed?

**Simplification label:** This is a compressed teaching summary of the Federal Reserve review, not a complete history or an allocation of legal liability. The review itself should be used for its detailed findings, chronology, scope, and limitations.

## Contagion: how one institution changes conditions for others

A run is institution-specific. **Contagion** occurs when distress at one institution materially changes beliefs, behavior, funding conditions, or asset prices affecting others. Direct financial exposure is only one channel.

### Channel 1: information and similarity

Depositors and investors use one failure to update beliefs about institutions with similar features:

- high uninsured-deposit shares;
- concentrated customers or industries;
- large interest-rate exposures;
- comparable securities losses;
- rapid prior growth;
- weak disclosed liquidity; or
- a business model newly perceived as fragile.

This can be rational information processing even when the comparison is incomplete. If outsiders cannot quickly distinguish strong banks from weak ones, they may withdraw from the entire perceived category.

The practical defense is not generic reassurance. It is credible, decision-useful evidence about capital, asset quality, funding concentration, liquidity, collateral readiness, and governance—subject to legal and disclosure constraints.

### Channel 2: shared depositor and adviser networks

The same corporate treasurer, fund, adviser, platform, broker, or community may influence balances at multiple banks. A recommendation to diversify or withdraw after one failure can produce simultaneous outflows elsewhere.

This is why depositor concentration should be mapped by common decision maker and information network, not only by legal account name.

### Channel 3: common funding markets

When one bank fails, wholesale lenders and large depositors may demand higher rates, shorter terms, more collateral, or lower limits from other banks. Funding can remain technically available while becoming economically destructive. Institutions needing to refinance at the same time become correlated through the market even without direct exposure to the failed bank.

### Channel 4: common assets and fire-sale prices

A distressed institution may sell securities or loans quickly. If sales are large or markets are thin, transaction prices can fall. Those prices can affect:

- market valuations at other institutions;
- collateral haircuts and margin requirements;
- investor estimates of unrealized losses;
- the cash other banks could raise through sale or pledge; and
- confidence in institutions holding similar assets.

This can produce a loop:

> Forced sales → lower observed prices → weaker valuations and collateral capacity elsewhere → more funding pressure → additional forced sales

A lower market price does not prove identical ultimate credit loss at every holder. But it can still create immediate liquidity, accounting, collateral, and confidence consequences.

### Channel 5: counterparty and operational exposure

Banks, payment providers, clearing arrangements, custodians, correspondents, and service providers can hold claims on or depend operationally on one another. Failure can delay payments, trap balances, interrupt services, or create replacement needs. These are direct channels, but their importance varies by institution.

### Channel 6: resolution expectations and policy uncertainty

How authorities resolve one failure can change expectations at other banks. If uninsured depositors or other creditors experience or expect losses, similarly situated claimants elsewhere may move preemptively. If authorities extend protection or liquidity support, immediate run incentives may decline, but questions of scope, precedent, cost, and moral hazard arise.

The FDIC’s deposit-insurance report emphasizes both sides: insurance promotes stability by reducing incentives for insured depositors to run, but broader protection can weaken market discipline and increase risk-taking unless paired with pricing, capital, supervision, and other constraints.

### Direct exposure is not required

The Federal Reserve’s SVB review states that contagion can create systemic consequences even when the original bank is not exceptionally large, deeply connected through financial counterparties, or the provider of a critical service. The Basel Committee’s 2023 review likewise notes indirect and cross-border contagion channels.

“Systemic” therefore does not mean merely “big.” It concerns whether distress disrupts confidence, markets, payments, credit supply, or other institutions strongly enough to threaten wider financial stability.

**Simplification label:** These channels are an analytical taxonomy, not an official legal test for systemic risk or a complete contagion model. Their direction and strength are scenario-dependent. Diversification, hedging, accounting treatment, policy response, market depth, disclosure, and time can interrupt or amplify them.

## What can break the feedback loop—and what each tool actually does

No single control makes a bank run-proof. Resilience comes from reducing both the probability that concern becomes a run and the damage if it does.

### Ex ante balance-sheet resilience

Before stress, the bank can:

- limit dangerous concentrations in deposits, wholesale funding, assets, industries, and counterparties;
- maintain capital proportionate to its loss exposure;
- preserve a usable cushion of cash and unencumbered liquid assets;
- manage duration and interest-rate risk;
- fund long-lived or illiquid assets with appropriately stable sources;
- maintain and test diverse borrowing channels;
- pre-position eligible collateral;
- verify legal, operational, currency, and entity access; and
- price products and growth so resilience is not sacrificed for short-term earnings.

Capital absorbs losses. Liquidity meets payments. Stable funding reduces the volume likely to flee. They reinforce one another but are not interchangeable.

### Run-specific stress testing

A severe run scenario should vary more than a runoff percentage. It should test:

- outflow speed by hour and day;
- common control and depositor-network effects;
- insured, uninsured, operational, and non-operational behavior;
- simultaneous credit-line draws by withdrawing customers;
- loss of unsecured and non-renewing wholesale funding;
- collateral haircuts and ineligibility;
- asset-sale discounts and market depth;
- operational cutoffs and staff capacity;
- adverse media or peer-bank failure;
- combined institution-specific and market-wide stress; and
- second-round effects on earnings, capital, and confidence.

Historical experience is an input, not a ceiling. The Federal Reserve review and FDIC report both indicate that information networks and transfer technology can make past speeds unreliable.

### Early-warning indicators and decision authority

Useful indicators include:

- deposit outflows by customer, channel, insurance status, and hour;
- unusual online-transfer attempts or limit changes;
- large customer inquiries and account-opening activity elsewhere;
- changes in deposit pricing and retention requests;
- wholesale non-renewal or widening spreads;
- collateral calls, falling borrowing capacity, or higher haircuts;
- equity, debt, and credit-protection prices where relevant;
- social and news narratives, verified rather than treated as fact;
- failed or delayed capital and funding transactions;
- facility-test failures; and
- breaches of survival-horizon or encumbrance limits.

Indicators require pre-agreed triggers, named owners, escalation deadlines, and authority. A dashboard without a decision protocol only documents the run more elegantly.

### Contingency funding execution

A credible plan identifies each source’s amount after haircut, collateral, owner, counterparty, conditions, cutoff, settlement time, and failure mode. It should be tested in normal conditions, including weekends and after-hours where relevant.

The plan must sequence actions by side effect. Selling the best securities first may provide speed but shrink the remaining buffer. Pledging the best collateral first may limit later options. Paying any price for deposits may preserve cash while damaging future earnings and attracting unstable funds.

### Communication

Communication can reduce an information vacuum, but it cannot replace cash, capital, or truthful disclosure. Effective crisis communication is:

- factually accurate and consistent across management, the board, operations, counterparties, and authorities;
- specific enough to address the actual concern;
- aligned with current liquidity and solvency evidence;
- legally and operationally reviewed; and
- paired with executable action.

Unsupported reassurance can backfire when depositors interpret it as evidence that management is avoiding the underlying issue. Silence can also allow inaccurate narratives to dominate. The correct response is not “say more” or “say less” in the abstract; it is coordinated, truthful, decision-useful communication within the applicable legal framework.

### Deposit insurance

Deposit insurance protects covered depositors under applicable rules and reduces their incentive to withdraw solely from fear of loss in a bank failure. It also supports broader financial stability.

Insurance is not free of trade-offs. If depositors expect protection regardless of bank risk, they may exert less discipline, and banks may have stronger incentives to take risk. That moral-hazard concern is why insurance design interacts with risk-based assessments, capital, supervision, liquidity standards, resolution, and governance.

### Central-bank liquidity

Eligible official liquidity can convert suitable collateral into cash and help a viable institution bridge a timing mismatch. It is not common equity and does not erase underlying losses. Access, collateral, haircuts, documentation, condition standards, authority, and operational readiness apply.

### Resolution

When an institution is failing or cannot restore viability, resolution uses the applicable legal framework to protect insured depositors, preserve critical functions where required, allocate losses, and manage the failure. Resolution and liquidity support solve different problems. Keeping payments moving temporarily is not the same as restoring positive economic net worth.

## Institutional operator scenario

A regional bank has grown rapidly by serving professional-services firms and technology companies. Forty large corporate groups control most of its uninsured commercial balances. Many use the same treasury advisers and communicate in two industry channels. The bank holds a large portfolio of long-duration securities and relationship loans.

After another bank with a superficially similar customer base fails:

- several customers ask for current insurance and collateral information;
- advisers recommend moving excess operating cash across multiple institutions;
- online transfer instructions rise sharply before market open;
- one wholesale lender declines to renew a maturing position;
- the market value of the bank’s securities falls;
- the bank’s collateral estimate still includes assets pledged for public deposits;
- a loan portfolio shown as borrowing capacity has never completed eligibility testing;
- commercial borrowers begin drawing commitments while moving deposits; and
- management proposes a broad statement that the bank is “well capitalized and fully liquid.”

A disciplined operator should not start with the statement. The sequence is:

1. **Establish the live cash clock.** Reconcile cleared cash, queued withdrawals, settlement obligations, commitment draws, and deadlines by entity and currency.
2. **Map common decision makers.** Aggregate deposits by beneficial owner, adviser, platform, industry network, insurance status, purpose, and transfer capability.
3. **Recalculate usable liquidity.** Remove encumbered assets, reject untested collateral, apply current values and stressed haircuts, and include settlement time.
4. **Run multiple speeds.** Test ordinary runoff, a concentrated network withdrawal, loss of wholesale renewal, and combined market-wide stress by hour and day.
5. **Connect liquidity to solvency.** Estimate sale losses, replacement-funding cost, earnings effects, and capital after each contingency action.
6. **Activate tested authorities.** Assign collateral movement, facility draws, customer operations, legal review, board escalation, and supervisor contact to named owners.
7. **Prepare truthful communication.** Address verified concerns using current evidence; do not claim that gross assets or facility limits equal available cash.
8. **Monitor contagion channels.** Track peer-bank news, common-asset prices, depositor networks, wholesale terms, and counterparty behavior.
9. **Define the next decision point.** State the remaining survival horizon after each action and the trigger for capital, transaction, recovery, or resolution measures.

The first board question should be:

> After removing encumbrance, untested collateral, and duplicated sources, how much cash can this bank deliver by each deadline under a coordinated depositor-network run, and what losses and funding costs does that response impose on capital?

No Bitcoin or Galoy application is needed. Bank-run dynamics and contagion should be understood on their own institutional terms.

## What must not be confused

1. **Trigger versus vulnerability:** a rumor, disclosure, or peer failure can ignite stress, but balance-sheet, funding, governance, and operational weaknesses determine susceptibility.
2. **Run versus proof of insolvency:** withdrawal pressure can strike a viable bank; it can also expose real losses. Solvency still requires valuation and capital analysis.
3. **Confidence versus fiction:** confidence matters, but unsupported reassurance cannot create cash or net worth.
4. **Uninsured versus certain to flee:** loss exposure raises incentives, but customer purpose, relationships, alternatives, and common decision makers shape behavior.
5. **Many accounts versus many independent decisions:** common owners, advisers, platforms, and networks can coordinate ostensibly diverse deposits.
6. **Total runoff versus runoff speed:** operational and settlement capacity can survive a slow decline and fail against the same amount compressed into hours.
7. **Information versus truth:** accurate information, incomplete signals, and misinformation can all change expectations and behavior.
8. **Social media versus root cause:** communication technology accelerates coordination; it does not by itself create interest-rate losses, weak capital, concentrated funding, or poor governance.
9. **Asset value versus same-day cash:** loans and securities can have value without being saleable or pledgeable before withdrawals settle.
10. **Potential collateral versus executable capacity:** eligibility, documentation, pre-positioning, valuation, haircut, authority, and cutoff determine cash.
11. **Liquidity support versus capital:** borrowing adds cash and an obligation; it does not absorb pre-existing losses.
12. **Asset sale versus harmless conversion:** a sale provides cash but may realize losses, depress prices, and weaken capital.
13. **Institution-specific run versus contagion:** the first concerns one bank; contagion transmits changed beliefs, outflows, funding terms, prices, or operations to others.
14. **Contagion versus direct exposure only:** similarity, depositor networks, common assets, and policy expectations can transmit stress without an interbank loan.
15. **Large bank versus systemic event:** size matters, but speed, similarity, confidence, markets, and indirect channels can make a smaller failure consequential.
16. **Deposit insurance versus complete risk control:** insurance reduces covered depositor run incentives but introduces design, pricing, fund, discipline, and moral-hazard questions.
17. **Regulatory minimum versus run survival:** standardized ratios cannot capture every institution’s depositor network, intraday speed, collateral defect, or combined scenario.
18. **Contingency document versus tested response:** names, amounts, collateral, cutoffs, procedures, decision rights, and execution tests make the plan real.
