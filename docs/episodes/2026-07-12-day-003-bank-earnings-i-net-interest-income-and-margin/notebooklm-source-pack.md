## Title

Bank Earnings I: Net Interest Income and Net Interest Margin

## Place in the course

Day 001 established that a bank is a regulated balance-sheet institution. Day 002 established that customer deposits are liabilities to the bank and an important funding source. Today adds the first earnings layer: how the institution earns interest on assets, pays interest on funding, and measures the difference without confusing revenue with profit.

Do not recap the entire definition of a bank or deposits. Bridge from Day 002 in under 45 seconds.

## Learning objective

Explain interest income, interest expense, net interest income, average earning assets, and net interest margin; understand why net interest margin is not the same thing as profit.

## Core mechanism

A simplified bank balance sheet contains interest-earning assets—such as loans and securities—and interest-bearing liabilities—such as many deposits and wholesale borrowings.

- **Interest income** is interest earned on loans, securities, and other interest-earning assets.
- **Interest expense** is interest paid on deposits, borrowings, and other interest-bearing liabilities.
- **Net interest income** is interest income minus interest expense.
- **Net interest margin**, commonly abbreviated NIM in written banking analysis, expresses annualized net interest income relative to average earning assets.

A simplified expression is:

> Net interest margin = annualized net interest income ÷ average earning assets

This is a margin on earning assets—not the simple difference between one loan rate and one deposit rate, and not the institution’s final profit margin.

## Worked example

Suppose a bank has $100 million of average earning assets. During a year it earns $6 million of interest income and incurs $2 million of interest expense.

- Net interest income: $6 million − $2 million = $4 million.
- Simplified net interest margin: $4 million ÷ $100 million = 4%.

That does not mean the bank earned $4 million of net profit. It still has non-interest revenue and substantial expenses and losses to account for: salaries, technology, branches, fraud, compliance, deposit insurance assessments, credit-loss provisions, taxes, and other operating costs.

## Why the margin moves

Net interest margin changes when asset yields and funding costs change, but not necessarily at the same speed.

- Many loans and securities have fixed rates or reprice slowly.
- Some funding costs reprice quickly when market rates change.
- Depositors may demand higher rates or move balances.
- The mix of loans, securities, cash, deposits, and wholesale funding changes.
- Credit and liquidity strategy influence which yields and funding costs the institution accepts.

The repricing speed of deposits is sometimes discussed through **deposit beta**: how much deposit rates change relative to a change in a reference market rate. Do not turn this into a universal constant; behavior differs by account type, customer segment, competition, institution, and rate cycle.

## What must not be confused

1. **Spread versus net interest margin:** a quoted loan rate minus a quoted deposit rate is a narrow spread. Net interest margin is an institution-wide measure using net interest income and average earning assets.
2. **Revenue versus profit:** net interest income is a major revenue component, not bottom-line income.
3. **High margin versus good bank:** a higher margin may reflect better pricing or cheaper funding, but it may also accompany more credit risk, liquidity risk, concentration, or unstable funding.
4. **Deposits versus free money:** deposits are liabilities with servicing, operational, liquidity, insurance, and sometimes explicit interest costs.

## Institutional operator scenario

A bank’s net interest margin rises sharply. One executive calls this an obvious improvement. Before agreeing, ask:

- Did loan or securities yields rise?
- Did funding costs lag temporarily?
- Did the bank shift toward riskier or longer-duration assets?
- Did deposit composition become less stable?
- Are credit-loss provisions rising?
- Is the change sustainable across the rate cycle?

The operator’s job is not to celebrate a single ratio. It is to understand the balance-sheet mechanism and the risks producing it.
