# Banking Fluency Daily Lesson — Day 001

## Topic

What is a bank?

## Why this matters

If Leo may operate inside Galoy's marketing / business-development motion, the first discipline is to stop treating banks as apps, wallets, or generic financial companies. A bank is a regulated balance-sheet institution. It takes liabilities, makes assets, manages risk, connects into payment systems, and lives under supervision because its failure can damage customers and the broader financial system.

That mental model comes before Bitcoin.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this lesson, Leo should be able to explain:

1. Why a customer deposit is a liability for the bank.
2. Why a loan is an asset for the bank.
3. Why banks are regulated differently from normal companies.
4. Why banking software buyers care about risk, controls, auditability, uptime, and supervision.
5. Why Bitcoin banking products must fit into existing banking categories before they can be sold credibly.

## Core concepts

- **Bank** — a regulated financial institution that accepts deposits, makes loans, provides payments/services, and manages risk under legal/supervisory constraints.
- **Deposit** — money a customer places with a bank; economically a claim against the bank, so it appears as a bank liability.
- **Loan** — money the bank lends out; economically a claim the bank has against a borrower, so it appears as a bank asset.
- **Balance-sheet business** — banking is built around assets, liabilities, equity/capital, and risk transformation.
- **Intermediation** — banks connect savers/depositors and borrowers, while transforming maturity, liquidity, and credit risk.
- **Public trust function** — banks hold customer money and operate inside payment systems, so their failure creates public and systemic consequences.

## Plain-English lesson

A bank is not mainly a website, a card, a vault, or an app. Those are interfaces. The bank itself is a regulated institution built around a balance sheet.

When a customer deposits $1,000, the bank does not simply put that exact money in a labeled box. The customer now has a claim on the bank. From the customer's perspective, it feels like an asset: “I have $1,000 in my account.” From the bank's perspective, it is a liability: “We owe this customer $1,000 on demand or according to the account terms.”

The bank then uses its balance sheet to hold reserves, buy securities, make loans, provide payments access, and earn revenue. A loan is different from a deposit. If the bank lends $10,000 to a borrower, that loan is an asset for the bank because the borrower owes the bank principal and interest. This is the first unlock: deposits fund liabilities; loans produce assets; banking is the management of that structure under uncertainty.

Banks make money largely by earning more on assets than they pay on liabilities, plus fees and services. But this simple model creates several risks:

- borrowers may not repay;
- depositors may withdraw faster than expected;
- interest rates may move against the bank;
- securities may lose market value;
- operations may fail;
- fraud, cyber, vendor, or compliance failures may damage the institution;
- public confidence may collapse.

This is why banks are supervised. Regulators are not only checking paperwork. They are trying to preserve depositor confidence, payment-system stability, consumer protection, anti-money-laundering controls, and the safety and soundness of the institution.

For Galoy-style work, this matters because banks do not buy “cool Bitcoin software” the way consumers buy an app. They ask institutional questions:

- Does this create new balance-sheet risk?
- Does it touch deposits, payments, lending, custody, or customer assets?
- What controls prove that transactions are accurate?
- Can examiners understand the system?
- How does this integrate with existing core banking, compliance, audit, and reporting systems?
- What happens when something breaks?

Before Bitcoin becomes interesting to a bank, it has to be translated into banking categories: custody, payments, collateral, lending, treasury, settlement, operational controls, compliance obligations, and customer risk.

That is the point of this course.

## Institutional buyer lens

A bank buyer does not start from “Is Bitcoin interesting?” They start from:

- Will this help us grow revenue or defend relevance?
- Will this create regulatory heat?
- Can our compliance team live with it?
- Can our core systems integrate with it?
- Can we explain it to our board and examiners?
- Can we operate it safely during stress?

This means marketing and BD in this market must speak risk, controls, supervision, and institutional workflow — not just innovation.
