# Banking Fluency — Day 013

## Title

Institutional forms I: banks and credit unions

## Place in the course

Day 012 separated central-bank liquidity, deposit insurance, and resolution for a failing bank. This lesson changes the lens from crisis tools to institutional identity: before asking which authority, insurance fund, or resolution process applies, determine what legal institution you are dealing with. The new layer is the structural difference between a bank and a credit union—and the further distinction between federal and state charters inside each category.

## Learning objective

Explain how charter, ownership, membership, powers, insurance, and supervision fit together for U.S. banks and credit unions; identify the principal authority map for a national bank, state member bank, state nonmember bank, federal credit union, and state-chartered credit union; and show why similar products do not make two institutions legally or economically interchangeable.

## The coherent layer: institutional form is an operating system, not a brand label

A bank and a credit union can both accept funds, make loans, issue cards, provide online accounts, and move payments. Those surface similarities do not answer five deeper questions:

1. **Who created the institution and under which charter?**
2. **Who owns or governs it?**
3. **Whom may it serve?**
4. **Which activities may it conduct, and under what limits?**
5. **Which agencies charter, supervise, insure, and—if necessary—resolve it?**

The answers come from the institutional form. A charter is not merely permission to put “bank” or “credit union” on a website. It creates a legal entity under a body of law, defines its basic powers and governance, and places it within a supervisory and resolution architecture.

The central lesson is:

> Product similarity does not erase institutional structure. Start with charter and ownership; then map powers, eligible customers or members, insurer, and supervisors.

This prevents a common analytical error: seeing a checking-like account or consumer loan and assuming the provider has the same authority, ownership incentives, insurance, and regulator as every other institution offering a similar interface.

**Simplification label:** This pack uses a high-level U.S. framework and focuses on ordinary insured depository institutions. It is educational, not legal, tax, accounting, chartering, deposit- or share-insurance, supervisory, corporate-governance, consumer-compliance, or resolution advice. State law varies. Savings associations, mutual banks, bankers’ banks, corporate credit unions, industrial banks, limited-purpose institutions, uninsured institutions, private deposit or share insurance, bank and savings-and-loan holding companies, foreign banking offices, and special-purpose charters add important exceptions. Always verify the legal entity, charter, insurer, state, current law, and agency records.

## Build the identity stack in the right order

When evaluating an institution, use this sequence.

### Layer 1: legal entity

Identify the exact entity—not the parent company, product brand, app, program manager, or technology vendor. A financial group can contain several regulated and nonregulated entities. The name on a website may differ from the legal entity that holds a customer’s funds or makes a loan.

Useful evidence includes:

- the legal name in account agreements and disclosures;
- the chartering authority’s institution record;
- FDIC BankFind Suite for an FDIC-insured bank;
- NCUA’s Credit Union Locator and share-insurance records for a credit union;
- the institution’s FDIC certificate or NCUA charter number;
- the named creditor, custodian, and account-holding entity in the contract; and
- the entity shown on regulatory reports and official agency databases.

### Layer 2: charter

The charter answers which sovereign created the institution and supplies its foundational authority.

For the categories in this lesson:

- a **national bank** receives a federal charter from the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency;
- a **state bank** receives its charter from a state banking authority;
- a **federal credit union** receives a federal charter from the National Credit Union Administration; and
- a **state-chartered credit union** receives its charter from the applicable state authority.

“Federal” and “state” describe the charter source. They do not, by themselves, provide a complete regulator or insurer map.

### Layer 3: ownership and governance

Determine who holds the residual economic and voting rights and how directors are selected.

- Most ordinary commercial banks are stock institutions owned by shareholders. Customers are creditors of the bank for their deposits but do not become owners merely by opening accounts. Shareholder voting and economic rights generally depend on shares held, subject to corporate, banking, and securities law.
- A credit union is a cooperative owned and controlled by its members. An eligible person generally becomes a member by acquiring and maintaining the required membership share under the credit union’s rules. Members elect the board; the cooperative principle is one member, one vote rather than votes proportional to account balance.

This difference changes the constituency to which governance is formally accountable. It does not imply that every bank ignores customers or that every credit union always delivers better prices. Competition, scale, risk, capital, management, technology, product mix, and local market conditions still matter.

### Layer 4: powers and customer boundary

A charter authorizes a defined set of activities. It does not grant unlimited permission to conduct anything that resembles finance.

Banks generally exercise deposit-taking, lending, payment, fiduciary, investment, and incidental powers under the statutes and regulations applicable to their charter, approvals, subsidiaries, and activities. The details differ by national or state charter and by institution type.

Federal credit unions exercise the powers Congress granted in the Federal Credit Union Act and that NCUA rules implement. They serve members within an approved **field of membership**, subject to statutory and regulatory rules. A federal credit union’s charter may be based on:

- a single occupational or associational common bond;
- multiple occupational or associational common bonds; or
- a well-defined local community, neighborhood, or rural district.

State credit-union powers and membership rules arise principally under state law and can differ from the federal framework.

### Layer 5: insurer and supervisors

Only after identifying entity and charter should you map:

- the chartering authority;
- the primary federal supervisor;
- the state supervisor, where applicable;
- the deposit or share insurer;
- any additional functional or consumer regulator; and
- the authority that would act in failure or liquidation.

Chartering, supervising, insuring, and resolving are distinct functions. One agency may perform several of them, but the words are not synonyms.

## Banks: stock ownership, deposits, and the dual banking system

### What “bank” means for this lesson

This pack uses **bank** as shorthand for an ordinary U.S. commercial bank that accepts deposits and is FDIC-insured. The exact legal definitions in federal and state statutes are more technical. A company can facilitate payments, extend credit, or provide an app without being a bank. Conversely, a chartered bank can use a brand that does not make its legal status obvious.

For a depositor, the balance-sheet relationship remains the Day 002 principle:

- the customer’s deposit is an asset to the customer; and
- the same deposit is a liability of the bank.

In a stock bank, that creditor relationship is separate from ownership. A depositor with no bank stock has a claim against the bank under the deposit agreement but no shareholder vote merely because the deposit is large.

### The dual banking system

The United States has both federal and state bank charters. The FDIC’s official “What We Do” page summarizes the basic map: banks may be chartered by states or by the OCC, and a state-chartered bank may choose whether to become a member of the Federal Reserve System.

That produces three core categories.

#### 1. National bank

- **Charter:** OCC.
- **Primary federal supervisor:** OCC.
- **Federal Reserve membership:** National banks are members of the Federal Reserve System under federal law.
- **Deposit insurer:** Ordinarily FDIC for the insured national banks covered by this lesson.
- **State role:** State laws can still apply in important areas, but the charter and federal framework determine the national bank’s powers and supervision; questions of federal preemption are activity- and law-specific.

The OCC states that it charters, regulates, and supervises all national banks, federal savings associations, and federal branches and agencies of foreign banks. It examines the institutions it supervises for safe and sound operation, fair access, fair treatment of customers, and compliance with applicable law.

#### 2. State member bank

- **Charter:** State banking authority.
- **Primary federal supervisor:** Federal Reserve, working with the state authority.
- **Federal Reserve membership:** Yes.
- **Deposit insurer:** FDIC for an insured state member bank.

The state charter remains foundational even though the institution has elected Federal Reserve membership. State and federal oversight coexist rather than one erasing the other.

#### 3. State nonmember bank

- **Charter:** State banking authority.
- **Primary federal supervisor:** FDIC for an insured state bank that is not a Federal Reserve member, alongside the state authority.
- **Federal Reserve membership:** No.
- **Deposit insurer:** FDIC.

The FDIC says it is the primary federal regulator for state-chartered banks that do not join the Federal Reserve System. It also has insurance-related and backup examination authority affecting other insured institutions.

### Ownership and capital in a stock bank

A stock bank’s common equity belongs to shareholders. It absorbs losses after the institution recognizes lower asset values or other expenses. Shareholders elect directors under the applicable governance rules and receive residual value only after liabilities are satisfied.

Depositors and shareholders therefore occupy different positions:

- **Depositor:** creditor holding a contractual deposit claim, with FDIC protection if the deposit and amount meet the insurance rules.
- **Shareholder:** residual owner whose equity absorbs loss and is not protected by deposit insurance.

A person can be both, but the rights arise from separate instruments. A $100,000 depositor who owns no shares does not receive ownership control. A shareholder with no deposit is still an owner.

Most commercial banks are organized this way, but not every insured banking institution is stock-owned. Mutual savings institutions are an important exception. The reliable operator habit is to inspect the actual charter and ownership form rather than treating “bank equals stock corporation” as universal.

### Bank powers are chartered powers

A bank may conduct only activities authorized by its governing law, regulation, charter, approvals, and applicable conditions. National banks derive core corporate powers from the National Bank Act, including receiving deposits, lending money, and exercising incidental powers necessary to conduct the business of banking. State banks derive powers from state law, with federal restrictions and conditions applying when they are federally insured or Federal Reserve members.

The comparison is not simply “federal banks have more powers” or “state banks are more flexible.” The real analysis asks:

1. Which entity will conduct the activity?
2. Which charter and statute authorize it?
3. Is the power express, incidental, or exercised through a subsidiary?
4. Is notice, approval, or a particular supervisory condition required?
5. Do federal insurance, safety-and-soundness, consumer, securities, commodities, payments, or state rules impose additional limits?

A charter is a bundle of authority and constraints, not an all-purpose financial license.

## Credit unions: cooperative ownership, membership, and shares

### The cooperative identity

NCUA’s consumer education site describes a credit union as a not-for-profit financial institution that accepts deposits, makes loans, and provides other financial services. Its defining institutional feature is not the product list. It is member ownership and control.

A federal credit union is organized to promote thrift among its members and create a source of credit for provident or productive purposes under the Federal Credit Union Act. Members pool savings, borrow, use services, and elect the board that governs the institution.

This creates three connected roles:

- a member may be a saver whose shares fund the institution;
- a member may be a borrower using the pooled funds; and
- the same member holds a governance vote under the cooperative structure.

The slogan “one member’s savings becomes another member’s loan” is a useful intuition, but the actual balance sheet is more complex. Credit unions hold cash, investments, loans, allowances, fixed assets, borrowings, and other accounts; they manage liquidity, capital, interest-rate risk, credit risk, and operations like other depository institutions.

### One member, one vote

In the federal credit-union model, each member has one vote regardless of the number of shares held. Members elect the board. NCUA’s public explanation describes credit unions as member-owned and controlled, with a member-elected volunteer board.

Contrast two simplified cases:

- A bank shareholder owning 10 percent of voting stock may carry materially more voting power than a small shareholder.
- A credit-union member with a $500,000 balance ordinarily does not receive more member votes than a qualifying member who maintains only the required membership share.

The governance principle changes formal voting power. It does not guarantee high election turnout, contested board races, perfect representation, or a particular management decision. Cooperative governance still depends on bylaws, nominations, elections, board competence, member participation, and regulatory duties.

### Shares are both funding claims and the gateway to membership

Credit unions commonly call customer funding claims **shares** or **share accounts** rather than deposits. On the credit union’s balance sheet, member shares are liabilities: the institution owes the member funds under the account terms. They are not common equity merely because the word “share” is used.

This distinction is essential:

- **Membership share/share account:** generally a liability of the credit union and potentially insured by the National Credit Union Share Insurance Fund under applicable rules.
- **Retained earnings and other regulatory capital accounts:** absorb losses.

Calling a share account an ownership interest can be directionally useful for explaining membership, but it can mislead if it implies that the account balance is loss-absorbing common stock. The member’s governance status and the accounting classification of the account are separate questions.

### Field of membership

Credit unions do not ordinarily serve an unlimited public merely because anyone can reach the website. A person must fall within the institution’s approved field of membership and satisfy its membership process.

For federal credit unions, NCUA’s Chartering and Field of Membership framework implements three broad charter types:

1. **Single common bond:** one occupational or associational bond.
2. **Multiple common bond:** more than one qualifying occupational or associational group, subject to the statutory and regulatory framework.
3. **Community:** persons who live, work, worship, attend school, or participate in qualifying associations within an approved well-defined local community, neighborhood, or rural district, as applicable under current rules.

Eligibility can also arise through family or household relationships and other provisions permitted by the charter and rules. State-chartered credit unions follow state law, which may define fields of membership differently.

Field of membership is not a marketing segment. It is a charter boundary. Growth plans, mergers, new groups, digital acquisition, branches, and partnerships must be tested against who may lawfully become a member and how eligibility is documented.

### Credit-union capital has a different path

A stock bank can potentially raise common equity from investors, subject to law, market access, approvals, and governance. A natural-person credit union generally builds its primary loss-absorbing net worth through retained earnings rather than selling conventional voting common stock to outside investors.

That affects growth economics. Rapid asset growth can dilute a credit union’s net-worth ratio if retained earnings do not keep pace. Management may need to balance:

- loan and membership growth;
- pricing and member returns;
- operating investment;
- credit and interest-rate risk;
- liquidity; and
- the pace at which earnings replenish or increase net worth.

“Not-for-profit” does not mean “does not need profit.” A credit union needs earnings to cover operating costs, build loss-absorbing net worth, invest in systems, and remain safe and sound. The structural question is what happens to the economic surplus: it is retained for resilience and institutional capacity or returned to members through pricing and services rather than distributed to outside common shareholders.

**Simplification label:** “Profit,” “surplus,” “net income,” “retained earnings,” “net worth,” and regulatory capital have distinct accounting and legal meanings. The comparison above omits subordinated debt rules, secondary capital, mutual forms, mergers, conversions, tax rules, and institution-specific capital standards. It explains the incentive and funding architecture, not a regulatory capital calculation.

## The credit-union charter, supervision, and insurance map

### Federal credit union

- **Charter:** NCUA.
- **Primary federal supervisor:** NCUA.
- **State supervisor:** None as chartering supervisor, although generally applicable state laws may still matter.
- **Share insurer:** The National Credit Union Share Insurance Fund, administered by NCUA.
- **Membership boundary:** The field of membership approved under the Federal Credit Union Act and NCUA rules.

NCUA states that it charters and regulates federal credit unions, insures deposits at federally insured credit unions, protects the members who own credit unions, and acts to protect the safety and soundness of the credit-union system.

### Federally insured state-chartered credit union

- **Charter:** State credit-union authority.
- **Primary chartering supervisor:** State authority.
- **Federal insurer:** National Credit Union Share Insurance Fund, administered by NCUA.
- **NCUA role:** Insurance oversight and statutory examination, enforcement, and rulemaking authority in applicable areas, coordinated with state supervisors.
- **Membership boundary and powers:** Primarily state law and the state charter, plus applicable federal requirements.

This is the credit-union counterpart to an important lesson from the banking map: the federal insurer need not be the chartering authority. State-chartered credit unions can be federally insured without becoming federal credit unions.

### Privately insured state-chartered credit union

Some state-chartered credit unions may use qualifying private share insurance rather than the federal fund where state law permits. They are outside the ordinary federally insured frame used in most of this lesson. Do not infer NCUSIF coverage from the words “credit union” alone. Verify the official insurance status.

### What the Share Insurance Fund covers

Congress created the National Credit Union Share Insurance Fund in 1970. NCUA administers it. NCUA’s official overview states that individual accounts are insured up to $250,000 and that separate coverage can apply to joint accounts, certain retirement accounts, and trust accounts under the governing rules. The fund has the full faith and credit of the United States.

Use the correct language:

- FDIC protects qualifying **deposits at an FDIC-insured bank**.
- NCUSIF protects qualifying **shares at a federally insured credit union**.

The familiar $250,000 figure does not make the regimes identical or turn every account into full coverage. Coverage depends on the insured institution, member or depositor, ownership category, account records, aggregation, and current rules.

**Simplification label:** The insurance descriptions are general educational summaries, not account-specific determinations. The FDIC and NCUA—not the institution, an app, a partner, or this pack—determine coverage under their respective laws and records. Pass-through arrangements, trusts, beneficiaries, joint ownership, retirement accounts, government accounts, mergers, and non-deposit or non-share products require additional analysis.

| Institution | Chartering authority | Core primary supervision | Federal insurance in the ordinary case | Ownership/governance anchor | Customer boundary |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| National bank | OCC | OCC | FDIC | Usually stock shareholders; board governance | Customers under authorized products and law |
| State member bank | State banking authority | Federal Reserve plus state authority | FDIC | Usually stock shareholders; board governance | Customers under authorized products and law |
| State nonmember bank | State banking authority | FDIC plus state authority | FDIC | Usually stock shareholders; board governance | Customers under authorized products and law |

Depending on institution and activity, the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, Financial Crimes Enforcement Network, state attorneys general, securities or commodities regulators, Federal Trade Commission, Department of Justice, payment-system authorities, and other bodies can have roles. A holding company can also introduce Federal Reserve oversight even when a subsidiary bank has a different primary federal regulator.

## Why ownership changes incentives—but does not decide outcomes

Ownership architecture establishes formal claims and governance. It does not mechanically determine every commercial choice.

### Pricing and surplus

A stock bank’s board and management are accountable within a structure whose residual owners are shareholders. The bank must serve customers competitively, comply with law, maintain safety and soundness, and produce an acceptable risk-adjusted return on shareholder capital.

A credit union’s residual constituency is its membership. Surplus can support higher savings rates, lower borrowing rates, lower fees, expanded services, technology, reserves, and retained net worth.

But a real comparison must control for:

- product and credit risk;
- term and optionality;
- branch and service model;
- insurance and other benefits;
- member or customer segment;
- funding mix;
- capital strength;
- operating efficiency;
- taxes and other legal differences;
- competitive intensity; and
- short-term promotions.

“Credit unions always pay more” and “banks are always more efficient” are slogans, not institutional analysis.

### Growth and capital

A stock bank may seek outside equity when growth or loss absorption requires more capital. A credit union’s cooperative structure makes retained earnings especially important. Therefore, a credit union offering the maximum immediate pricing benefit to members can weaken its ability to build net worth and fund future services if the pricing is not sustainable.

The governance tension is not “members versus profit.” It is:

> How much current member benefit can the institution deliver while retaining enough earnings, liquidity, systems capacity, and loss-absorbing net worth to serve members safely over time?

Banks face an analogous tension among customer value, employee and operational investment, resilience, and shareholder return, but the residual claimant and capital-raising mechanisms differ.

### Voting power and participation

One-member-one-vote limits the formal voting dominance of the largest account holders. Yet member control becomes weak in practice if elections are uncontested, turnout is low, information is poor, or the board lacks challenge and competence. Stock ownership can concentrate voting power, yet market, disclosure, fiduciary, supervisory, and governance mechanisms also constrain management.

Institutional form shapes incentives. Execution and governance determine whether the promised model is realized.

## Products can look identical while the legal relationships differ

Consider five interfaces.

### 1. Transaction account

A bank may offer a checking account; a credit union may offer a share draft account. Both can support cards, checks, bill pay, and electronic transfers. Behind the interface:

- the legal account holder is a depositor at the bank or a member/account holder at the credit union;
- the balance is a liability of the relevant institution;
- FDIC or NCUSIF rules apply depending on the institution; and
- credit-union membership eligibility may be required.

### 2. Loan

Both can make auto, mortgage, consumer, and business loans within their powers. The loan is an asset of the institution and a liability of the borrower. Underwriting, pricing, concentration, fair-lending, consumer-protection, collateral, capital, and allowance requirements still apply. Cooperative ownership does not remove credit risk; stock ownership does not authorize predatory treatment.

Federal credit unions also have charter-specific lending and maturity rules, and state credit unions can have different powers under state law. Never transfer a product conclusion from one charter to another without checking authority.

### 3. Digital app

The app may belong to the bank or credit union, a parent, a fintech partner, or a vendor. The user interface does not identify who holds the funds. Read the account agreement and official institution records.

### 4. Insurance badge

An FDIC or NCUA sign concerns qualifying products at the named insured institution under the relevant rules. It does not insure every product shown nearby, every affiliate, or the solvency of a nonbank technology provider.

### 5. “Member” marketing language

A retailer, fintech, or subscription business may call customers “members.” That does not make it a chartered credit union or create cooperative voting rights. Legal membership in a credit union comes from the institution’s charter, field of membership, bylaws, and account-opening requirements—not vocabulary.

## Failure and resolution follow the institution

The immediate bridge from Day 012 is now precise.

For an FDIC-insured bank:

- the appropriate chartering authority closes the bank under the applicable framework;
- the FDIC acts as insurer and normally as receiver; and
- insured deposits and resolution claims are handled under the bank-resolution regime.

For a federally insured credit union:

- NCUA administers the NCUSIF;
- NCUA has conservatorship and liquidation roles under the Federal Credit Union Act; and
- insured shares and creditor claims are handled through the credit-union framework.

A state authority can have a central role for a state-chartered institution. The exact sequence depends on charter and law.

The analytical correction is simple:

> Before applying a crisis playbook, identify the charter and insurer. “Depository institution” is not one universal resolution regime.

This also explains why calling every protected customer balance an “FDIC-insured deposit” is wrong. A federally insured credit-union member usually relies on NCUSIF share insurance, not FDIC deposit insurance.

## Institutional operator scenario

A software vendor says it has “two regulated financial-institution prospects” for the same account, lending, and payment product.

### Prospect A

- The brand belongs to a publicly traded financial holding company.
- The contracting entity is “River National Bank, N.A.”
- Official records identify an OCC charter and FDIC insurance.
- The bank wants to offer the product to commercial customers nationwide.
- The proposal would rely on the bank’s account, lending, and payment powers.

### Prospect B

- The contracting entity is “Harbor Community Federal Credit Union.”
- NCUA records identify a federal credit-union charter and federal share insurance.
- Members elect the board.
- The product team wants any U.S. consumer who downloads the app to open an account.
- The business case assumes outside investor capital can be injected through ordinary voting common stock if growth is rapid.

A weak analysis says both are insured institutions and therefore the deployment is identical.

A disciplined analysis proceeds as follows.

### Step 1: verify the legal entities

Do not contract with or write controls for the parent brand. Confirm which chartered entity opens accounts, holds funds, originates loans, moves payments, and retains customer data.

### Step 2: map each charter

- River is a national bank: OCC charter and primary supervision; FDIC deposit insurance.
- Harbor is a federal credit union: NCUA charter and primary supervision; NCUSIF share insurance.

### Step 3: identify ownership and decision rights

- River’s residual owners are shareholders through the corporate structure.
- Harbor’s owners are eligible members, with cooperative voting and an elected board.

The sales process, board framing, growth objective, and capital assumptions cannot be copied mechanically.

### Step 4: test customer eligibility

River’s customer scope depends on its authorized business, law, risk appetite, and product controls. Harbor cannot assume that every downloader can become a member. It must map each applicant to its approved field of membership and maintain the evidence required by its charter and rules.

### Step 5: test powers and approvals

For each institution, legal and compliance teams must identify the precise authority for the account, credit, payment, vendor, data, and geographic model. “The other prospect can do it” is not legal authority.

### Step 6: correct the insurance language

- River’s qualifying customer deposits may receive FDIC coverage under FDIC rules.
- Harbor’s qualifying member shares may receive NCUSIF coverage under NCUA rules.

Marketing, disclosures, pass-through structures, and non-deposit products require institution-specific review.

### Step 7: correct the capital plan

River may be able to raise stock capital through its ownership chain, subject to law, approval, and market conditions. Harbor cannot assume the same conventional outside voting-equity mechanism. It must model retained earnings, growth, net-worth requirements, and any legally available supplemental capital instruments under the credit-union framework.

### Step 8: build separate supervisory maps

The product’s risk assessment, board materials, regulatory engagement, examination evidence, incident escalation, and exit planning must use the correct agency relationships. Shared technical controls may exist, but governance and legal accountability differ.

The first diligence question should be:

> What exact chartered legal entity will perform each regulated function, and how do its ownership model, field of membership or customer authority, powers, insurer, capital path, and supervisors change the product design?

No Bitcoin or Galoy application is needed. The institutional-form map is foundational on its own terms.

## What must not be confused

1. **Product versus institution:** Offering accounts, loans, cards, or payments does not prove an entity is a bank or credit union.
2. **Brand versus legal entity:** The app or parent name may not be the entity holding funds or making loans.
3. **Charter versus license for anything:** A charter grants bounded powers and carries conditions, supervision, and governance.
4. **Federal charter versus federal insurance:** They often coexist, but chartering and insurance are separate functions.
5. **State charter versus state-only oversight:** A state-chartered insured institution also has a federal insurer and primary federal supervisory relationship.
6. **National bank versus every bank:** “National” identifies an OCC-chartered bank, not merely a bank with customers in many states.
7. **State member versus customer member:** A state member bank is a state-chartered member of the Federal Reserve System; its depositors do not thereby own it.
8. **State nonmember versus unregulated:** It is not a Federal Reserve member, but its state authority and FDIC supervise it in the ordinary insured case.
9. **Depositor versus shareholder:** A deposit is a creditor claim; stock is residual ownership.
10. **Credit-union member versus large balance:** Governance ordinarily follows one member, one vote, not dollars deposited.
11. **Credit-union share versus common equity:** A member share account is generally a liability and can be insured; retained earnings and regulatory capital absorb loss.
12. **Not-for-profit versus no earnings needed:** A safe credit union needs earnings for costs, systems, reserves, and net worth.
13. **Member-owned versus automatically well governed:** Participation, information, board competence, and controls remain necessary.
14. **Field of membership versus target market:** It is a charter eligibility boundary, not merely a preferred audience.
15. **Federal credit union versus every credit union:** State-chartered credit unions have state-law powers and membership rules.
16. **NCUA charter versus NCUSIF coverage:** NCUA charters federal credit unions; the NCUSIF can also insure qualifying state-chartered credit unions.
17. **FDIC limit versus NCUSIF limit:** The headline amount is similar, but the funds, institutions, terminology, and coverage rules are distinct.
18. **Insurance badge versus every product covered:** Non-deposit investments and other products are not insured merely because an insured institution offers them.
19. **Primary supervisor versus only regulator:** Consumer, financial-crime, holding-company, functional, and state authorities can also apply.
20. **Ownership structure versus guaranteed pricing:** Institutional incentives matter, but actual rates and fees depend on risk, scale, costs, competition, and strategy.
21. **Same control objective versus identical implementation:** Both forms manage safety, liquidity, credit, cyber, consumer, and vendor risk, but authority and governance routes differ.
22. **Bank resolution versus credit-union liquidation:** The FDIC and NCUA frameworks protect different insured claims and follow different statutory regimes.
