## Place in the course

Day 007 followed one weakening loan from collateral and expected recovery through allowance, provision, charge-off, and recovery. This lesson changes the unit of analysis: even when each loan looked defensible on its own, many loans can deteriorate together because they depend on the same economic driver.

Keep this bridge brief. Do not reteach collateral-dependent accounting, the allowance roll-forward, or charge-off mechanics.

## Learning objective

Explain why sound individual underwriting does not eliminate portfolio risk; identify concentrations by common source of repayment and common sensitivity rather than by product label alone; trace how concentrations build and surface through the credit cycle; and use segmentation, limits, monitoring, stress testing, capital, and exposure reduction to manage correlated loss.

## The coherent layer: from one credit to a portfolio that can fail together

A bank does not own a collection of isolated loans. It owns a portfolio of contractual cash flows whose borrowers may depend on the same customers, employers, property market, commodity price, interest-rate environment, geography, collateral type, or refinancing channel.

The central portfolio question is:

> Which exposures could weaken at the same time, for the same underlying reason, and how large would the combined effect be on earnings, the allowance, capital, liquidity, and the bank’s ability to keep lending?

The OCC states that credit-risk management does not end with individual transactions. A pool can threaten a bank even when every loan in it was soundly underwritten, because a shared characteristic or sensitivity can cause the loans to perform similarly. The Basel Committee makes the same distinction: banks must manage both individual credits and the portfolio as a whole.

A useful causal chain is:

> Common economic driver → correlated borrower stress → simultaneous risk-rating migration and defaults → higher provisions and charge-offs → pressure on earnings and capital → reduced flexibility to fund, lend, or absorb further loss

This lesson is about that chain. It is not an argument that every concentration is reckless or that diversification makes credit risk disappear.

**Simplification label:** This pack uses a generic U.S. commercial-bank frame. It is educational, not a complete statement of U.S. lending-limit law, regulatory capital rules, CECL, supervisory ratings, concentration-reporting instructions, stress-testing requirements, securities law, or portfolio-model methodology. The OCC’s supervisory definition and thresholds do not automatically apply to every institution, jurisdiction, exposure, or internal risk decision.

## Individual credit risk and portfolio credit risk are different

Individual underwriting asks whether a particular borrower has a credible repayment path, suitable structure, adequate capacity, evidenced willingness, and acceptable residual risk.

Portfolio management asks what happens when the assumptions beneath many individually acceptable loans fail together.

Suppose a bank makes 100 loans to well-run local businesses. Each borrower has positive cash flow and each loan fits policy. The portfolio may still be highly concentrated if most borrowers depend on one regional employer. A plant closure could reduce employee income, local retail sales, apartment occupancy, home values, contractor revenue, and municipal receipts at the same time. The loan files have different names and industries; the repayment sources share one economic engine.

This produces a crucial distinction:

- **Idiosyncratic risk** is specific to one borrower or transaction: fraud by one owner, the loss of one contract, a fire at one facility, or one bad acquisition.
- **Systematic or common-factor risk** affects many borrowers together: recession, higher rates, a commodity-price collapse, broad property-value decline, drought, regulatory change, war, supply-chain disruption, or the failure of a dominant local employer.

Diversification can reduce the impact of idiosyncratic failures because unrelated good outcomes can coexist with individual bad outcomes. It is much less effective when apparently different exposures share the same common factor.

**Simplification label:** “Systematic” has precise meanings in some finance and risk models. Here it means a shared driver that can affect multiple borrowers, not a formal asset-pricing or regulatory-capital parameter.

## What a concentration is

The FDIC describes a concentration generally as a significantly large volume of economically related assets advanced or committed to one person, entity, affiliated group, or related set of exposures that may in aggregate create substantial risk. The OCC’s supervisory booklet defines a concentration for its reporting framework as direct, indirect, or contingent obligations exceeding 25 percent of tier 1 capital plus the applicable allowance.

The number is useful, but the mechanism matters more than the label.

The OCC explicitly warns that:

- the same-sized pools can present very different risks;
- some pools below a reporting threshold may deserve intense scrutiny;
- some large, diversified, predictably performing pools may pose less danger than smaller, volatile pools;
- one exposure can belong to several concentration pools; and
- size must be considered with volatility, credit quality, growth, underwriting, correlation, and potential loss relative to earnings and capital.

Therefore, “below 25 percent” does not mean safe, and “above 25 percent” does not by itself mean unsound. The threshold identifies a supervisory concentration; it is not a universal risk appetite, a legal lending limit, or a substitute for analysis.

**Simplification label:** The OCC denominator and treatment of the allowance have technical details, including CECL transition provisions. Use current regulatory instructions for an actual calculation. Internal limits are often more granular and may be set below supervisory reporting thresholds.

## Find the common dependency, not merely the common label

Concentration analysis begins by grouping exposures that may react similarly. Useful lenses include:

### 1. Borrower and connected counterparties

A bank can face multiple legal entities whose repayment ultimately depends on one controlling owner, corporate group, sponsor, guarantor, or common cash source. Separate loan agreements do not create economic independence.

### 2. Industry and supply chain

Direct loans to automakers and loans to parts suppliers may share sensitivity to vehicle demand. Hotels, airlines, restaurants, and event businesses may share sensitivity to travel. An industry code helps, but it can miss upstream and downstream dependency.

### 3. Geography

Borrowers in one city, agricultural region, flood zone, wildfire corridor, or resource-dependent province may share local employment, property values, weather, infrastructure, and demand.

### 4. Product and underwriting vintage

Loans originated through the same channel, under the same loosened policy, with the same promotional terms, or during the same period can behave alike. A vintage created during rapid growth may conceal weaker borrower selection even if the product category is broad.

### 5. Source of repayment

Different products may depend on the same repayment source: rental income, home sale, refinancing, one government program, one commodity, one major customer, or one platform’s transaction volume.

### 6. Collateral and valuation driver

Loans to different borrowers may all be protected by the same collateral type. If the collateral price falls at the same time borrower cash flow weakens, default frequency and loss severity can rise together.

### 7. Interest-rate, maturity, and refinancing sensitivity

Borrowers with floating-rate debt, short maturities, balloon payments, or thin coverage may weaken together when rates rise or refinancing markets close.

### 8. Counterparty, originator, or protection provider

Purchased loans from one originator can share underwriting defects. Guarantees across many loans can share one protection provider. Selling participation interests does not remove risk if the buyer has recourse or if the bank retains operational, reputational, or contingent exposure.

### 9. On- and off-balance-sheet exposure

Unused commitments, letters of credit, guarantees, derivatives exposure, warehouse lines, investment holdings, and loans can all depend on the same counterparty or market. Stress may cause borrowers to draw commitments precisely when their credit quality and the bank’s liquidity are weakening.

The operator’s test is:

> If one adverse fact occurred, which exposures would appear on the same exception report—even if they sit in different systems, products, branches, or legal entities?

## Hidden correlation: calm periods can mislead

**Correlation** here means the tendency of credit outcomes to move together. It is not fixed.

The OCC notes that pools showing little performance correlation in a benign environment can become strongly correlated in deterioration. Geographic mortgage pools once assumed to be distinct became highly correlated when home prices declined broadly. The same logic applies whenever a common factor is quiet during the observation period and dominant during stress.

Three traps follow.

### Trap 1: low recent losses are treated as proof of independence

During expansion, delinquencies and charge-offs may be low across many segments. That does not show how the segments will behave when the shared driver reverses.

### Trap 2: product labels are mistaken for economic diversification

A commercial mortgage, a contractor line, a hotel loan, and a restaurant loan look like four products. If all depend on the same downtown occupancy and visitor economy, the bank may hold one economic bet in four wrappers.

### Trap 3: collateral is assumed to diversify the repayment source

If borrower cash flow and collateral value both depend on the same market, collateral can fail when it is most needed. A property downturn can reduce rent, impair refinancing, increase defaults, and depress liquidation proceeds together.

**Simplification label:** Formal correlation estimates require definitions, data, time horizons, and model validation. Small institutions may use structured scenarios and expert judgment rather than complex models. The objective is not a decorative coefficient; it is identifying shared loss drivers and decision-relevant exposure.

## How concentrations build through the credit cycle

The **credit cycle** is the recurring, uneven movement from easier credit and strong performance toward tightening, deterioration, loss recognition, and eventual repair. It is not a mechanical calendar and does not move every borrower or sector at once.

### Phase 1: expansion and apparent validation

Economic activity is strong. Borrower revenue rises, collateral values appreciate, defaults are low, and refinancing is available. Loans originated earlier look safe. Competition for growth and earnings intensifies.

The dangerous inference is: “Losses are low because our current production is high quality.” Low losses may primarily reflect favorable conditions and the seasoning of older loans; newly originated loans may not yet have met stress.

### Phase 2: concentration and underwriting drift

A profitable segment attracts more lenders. To maintain growth, institutions may accept thinner debt-service coverage, higher leverage, longer interest-only periods, weaker covenants, optimistic appraisals, policy exceptions, less experienced sponsors, or a greater reliance on sale and refinancing.

Rapid growth can hide deterioration:

- new balances enlarge the denominator, keeping delinquency ratios deceptively low;
- young loans have not had time to default;
- rising collateral values conceal weak amortization or cash flow;
- refinancing pays off weak loans before their underlying economics are tested; and
- staff, systems, credit review, and collateral controls may not scale with volume.

The OCC identifies concentrations booked during rapid expansion—often alongside weakening underwriting—as a recurring precursor to correlated distress.

### Phase 3: turning point

The common driver changes. Rates rise, demand falls, costs increase, vacancy grows, a commodity price drops, home values weaken, or refinancing becomes scarce. Borrowers with the thinnest margins fail first.

Early signals may include:

- rising utilization of revolving commitments;
- slower receivable collection or inventory turnover;
- covenant breaches and waiver requests;
- repeated maturity extensions;
- weaker debt-service coverage;
- more policy exceptions;
- risk-rating migration within pass grades;
- higher delinquencies, nonaccruals, or criticized assets;
- falling collateral values; and
- concentration-limit exceptions or unexpected overlap between pools.

### Phase 4: correlated deterioration and loss

The shock reaches multiple borrowers. Default probability rises. If collateral values decline and liquidation takes longer, loss severity can rise too. The institution may need higher allowance through provision expense while charge-offs also increase. Earnings absorb the provision; capital absorbs losses that exceed current earnings and available allowance treatment.

Funding and liquidity can become part of the same problem. Depositors, counterparties, or markets may react to visible asset-quality and capital pressure. Borrowers may draw commitments as other funding disappears. A credit concentration can therefore create liquidity and strategic constraints even before every expected loss is realized.

### Phase 5: tightening and repair

The bank tightens underwriting, raises monitoring intensity, curtails originations, sells or participates exposures, obtains capital, restructures viable borrowers, recognizes losses, and exits weak relationships. Economy-wide tightening can deepen stress for borrowers that depended on continual refinancing.

Eventually losses season through, balance sheets repair, pricing improves, and lending resumes. The next cycle may not resemble the last one; yesterday’s safe segment can become tomorrow’s concentrated risk.

**Simplification label:** This five-phase description is a teaching model, not a forecasting rule. Policy, inflation, technology, fiscal support, market structure, borrower hedging, deposit behavior, and institution-specific controls can alter the sequence. Credit conditions can also differ sharply across sectors and regions.

## Growth, vintage, and lagging indicators

A concentration report that shows only today’s outstanding balance is incomplete. The bank should understand how the pool was built.

Useful views include:

- commitments and outstanding balances by quarter or year of origination;
- growth rates compared with capital, staffing, and the institution’s market;
- underwriting policy in force for each vintage;
- exceptions, overrides, loan-to-value, coverage, leverage, maturity, and pricing by vintage;
- risk-rating migration before delinquency;
- renewals, extensions, and refinancings that may postpone visible default;
- utilization of undrawn commitments;
- collateral revaluation dates and assumptions; and
- realized default and loss severity as each vintage seasons.

Delinquency and charge-off rates are important, but they are late evidence. A portfolio can become riskier before a borrower misses a payment. Growth, structural weakness, exceptions, deteriorating pass grades, and weakening borrower cash flow can reveal the change earlier.

A useful discipline is to ask two questions separately:

1. **What is the portfolio reporting now?**
2. **What would it report if recent production had seasoned through a downturn?**

## Stress testing is a decision tool, not a prediction contest

The OCC describes stress testing as a way to alter economic or financial assumptions, quantify potential portfolio effects, identify correlated pools, and evaluate consequences for earnings and capital. Relevant variables can include unemployment, interest rates, commodity prices, rents, vacancy, real-estate values, and other drivers of a specific portfolio.

The Basel Committee says banks should consider future economic change, assess credit exposure under stressful conditions, and act when results exceed tolerance. It explicitly allows methods ranging from simple assumption changes to sophisticated models.

A useful concentration stress test has five parts:

1. **Define the common driver.** Example: office vacancy rises, rents fall, refinancing rates increase, and property values decline.
2. **Map exposure.** Include loans, commitments, guarantees, investments, counterparties, sponsors, tenants, and related sectors affected by the driver.
3. **Translate driver to credit outcomes.** Recalculate borrower coverage, refinancing capacity, default likelihood, collateral value, and loss severity.
4. **Aggregate consequences.** Estimate effects on risk grades, criticized assets, allowance, provision, charge-offs, earnings, capital, and liquidity.
5. **Pre-commit management actions.** Identify limits, monitoring, exposure reductions, capital or liquidity buffers, underwriting changes, and escalation triggers before stress arrives.

A stress test that produces a loss estimate but changes no limit, capital decision, contingency plan, or monitoring priority is unfinished.

## Worked portfolio case: different loans, one downtown economy

A simplified bank has **$50 million of capital** and **$500 million of credit exposure** across five pools:

| Pool | Exposure | Base PD | Base LGD | Stress PD | Stress LGD |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Downtown office | $120m | 2.0% | 30% | 12% | 45% |
| Construction | $100m | 3.0% | 40% | 15% | 50% |
| Hotels | $90m | 2.5% | 30% | 14% | 45% |
| Restaurants | $70m | 3.0% | 35% | 12% | 45% |
| Other diversified | $120m | 1.0% | 20% | 3% | 25% |

Management originally treated the first four as separate products. In reality, they share downtown employment, travel, visitor spending, property values, rent, and refinancing conditions.

Using the deliberately simplified relationship:

> Scenario loss = exposure × scenario probability of default × scenario loss given default

Base-case losses are:

- office: $120m × 2.0% × 30% = **$0.720m**;
- construction: $100m × 3.0% × 40% = **$1.200m**;
- hotels: $90m × 2.5% × 30% = **$0.675m**;
- restaurants: $70m × 3.0% × 35% = **$0.735m**;
- other: $120m × 1.0% × 20% = **$0.240m**; and
- total simplified base loss: **$3.570m**.

Now assume a downtown stress: occupancy and travel fall, refinancing rates rise, and property values weaken. Under the stated stress assumptions:

- office: $120m × 12% × 45% = **$6.480m**;
- construction: $100m × 15% × 50% = **$7.500m**;
- hotels: $90m × 14% × 45% = **$5.670m**;
- restaurants: $70m × 12% × 45% = **$3.780m**;
- other: $120m × 3% × 25% = **$0.900m**; and
- total simplified stress loss: **$24.330m**, or **48.66 percent of the illustrative $50 million capital base**.

The lesson is not that these assumptions are realistic for every bank. It is that four product categories can act like one correlated exposure, and both default frequency and loss severity can worsen under the same scenario.

Before using this analysis for a decision, management would need to test at least:

- whether exposures overlap or double-count the same obligor;
- whether commitments are measured by outstanding or potential draw;
- sponsor, guarantor, tenant, and counterparty links;
- maturity and refinancing dates;
- fixed versus floating rates and borrower hedges;
- current debt-service coverage and risk ratings;
- collateral type, lien priority, valuation date, and selling costs;
- migration, default, recovery, and correlation assumptions;
- the applicable allowance and capital frameworks; and
- second-order liquidity effects and feasible management actions.

**Simplification label:** The arithmetic is a scenario illustration, not a CECL estimate, regulatory stress test, capital requirement, forecast, or valuation. PD × exposure × LGD omits timing, prepayment, utilization, discounting, transitions, dependence structure, recoveries, taxes, earnings generation, allowance balances, regulatory adjustments, and management actions.

## What management can do

Concentration risk cannot be managed by one limit alone. A coherent control system combines information, governance, underwriting, capital, and exposure decisions.

### Identify and aggregate

- assign consistent borrower, connected-party, industry, geography, collateral, sponsor, tenant, product, vintage, and repayment-source fields;
- include off-balance-sheet commitments and contingent exposures;
- aggregate across branches, business lines, legal entities, and purchased portfolios; and
- validate data quality independently.

The OCC warns that inaccurate or delayed management information can create false comfort and direct attention toward the wrong pool.

### Establish risk appetite, limits, and sublimits

A broad commercial-real-estate limit may need narrower sublimits for construction, property type, geography, sponsor, tenant, maturity, or structure. Limits should reflect potential volatility and loss relative to earnings and capital—not only balance size.

Approaching or breaching a limit should trigger defined escalation and action. Repeated waivers can silently replace the stated risk appetite with the exception process.

### Strengthen underwriting and monitoring

Management can tighten leverage, coverage, amortization, advance rates, covenants, collateral controls, sponsor support, pricing, or approval authority. It can increase review frequency and reduce exposure to weak credits.

However, stricter terms on new loans do not repair the embedded risk of the existing portfolio. The stock and the flow require separate decisions.

### Diversify intelligently

True diversification adds exposures unlikely to fail for the same reason. Entering an unfamiliar market merely to change a concentration ratio can import underwriting and operational risk. The Basel Committee cautions banks not to book borrowers they do not know or activities they do not understand solely for diversification.

### Reduce or transfer exposure

The bank may allow runoff, curtail commitments where contractually permitted, sell whole loans, sell participations, use guarantees or insurance, hedge defined risk, or exit relationships. Each tool has basis, counterparty, legal, operational, and reputational risks. A participation with recourse may not transfer the risk management thinks it transferred.

### Hold more capital and liquidity

Additional capital can increase loss-absorbing capacity. Liquidity planning can prepare for commitment draws, deposit reactions, reduced market access, and slower asset sales during deterioration. Buffers mitigate consequences; they do not make the underlying credits independent.

### Connect stress results to decisions

Stress results should inform risk appetite, limits, underwriting, allowance analysis, capital planning, liquidity planning, portfolio strategy, and contingency actions. If modeled losses exceed tolerance and no feasible action restores resilience, reducing the concentration may be the only credible response.

## Institutional operator scenario

A regional bank reports that no individual loan breaches its single-borrower limit. Its board package also shows that each named industry remains within policy. Loan growth is strong, delinquency is low, and the commercial portfolio has produced excellent yields.

A deeper review finds:

- 35 percent of total commitments depend directly on one metropolitan property market;
- office landlords, construction firms, hotels, restaurants, and equipment lessors are reported as different industries;
- many borrowers share the same three sponsors and two major tenants;
- floating-rate loans were underwritten when rates were lower;
- 40 percent of the affected exposure matures or reprices within 18 months;
- recent vintages contain more interest-only structures and policy exceptions;
- appraisals largely predate higher vacancy;
- unused commitments could add material exposure under stress; and
- the concentration dashboard reports outstanding balances but not commitments, tenants, sponsors, vintage, or repayment-source dependency.

A disciplined operator should proceed in this order:

1. **Correct the map:** aggregate direct, indirect, and contingent exposure by sponsor, tenant, geography, repayment source, collateral, product, vintage, rate structure, and maturity.
2. **Validate the data:** reconcile exposure to source systems and test connected-party and off-balance-sheet fields.
3. **Find common drivers:** identify the assumptions—occupancy, rent, visitor demand, rates, refinancing, and property value—that connect the pools.
4. **Re-underwrite the stress:** recalculate coverage and refinance capacity, refresh valuations, and migrate risk ratings where current facts require it.
5. **Aggregate loss and liquidity effects:** estimate provision, charge-off, earnings, capital, commitment-draw, and funding consequences under coherent scenarios.
6. **Compare with tolerance:** test results against board limits, capital and liquidity buffers, and feasible contingency actions.
7. **Act:** impose sublimits, escalate exceptions, intensify monitoring, restrict weak new production, reduce or transfer exposure, and strengthen buffers as needed.

The low current delinquency rate is evidence about current contractual performance. It is not proof that the portfolio is diversified or that recent vintages have survived a cycle.

No Bitcoin or Galoy application is needed. The institutional portfolio mechanism stands on its own.

## What must not be confused

1. **Sound individual loans versus a sound portfolio:** each loan can be defensible while the collection is dangerously dependent on one driver.
2. **Different products versus different risk:** product names do not establish economic independence.
3. **Concentration versus automatic unsoundness:** concentrations can reflect expertise or local market structure; they require proportionate identification, control, capital, and monitoring.
4. **A supervisory threshold versus a safe harbor:** a pool below 25 percent can be risky, and the OCC threshold is not a universal internal limit.
5. **Single-borrower limits versus portfolio concentration:** complying with a legal or policy limit on one obligor does not control correlated exposure across many obligors.
6. **Recent low losses versus low forward risk:** benign conditions and young vintages can hide deterioration.
7. **Outstanding balances versus total exposure:** commitments, guarantees, derivatives, and contingent obligations can matter, especially during stress.
8. **Collateral diversification versus repayment diversification:** many loans secured by the same asset type can share both cash-flow and recovery risk.
9. **Delinquency versus early warning:** risk ratings, coverage, utilization, exceptions, extensions, collateral values, and common-driver data can worsen before missed payments.
10. **Stress testing versus forecasting:** a stress test asks whether the bank can withstand a coherent adverse condition; it does not claim that condition will occur with the stated probability.
11. **Diversification versus unfamiliar expansion:** adding unknown borrowers or products can exchange concentration risk for underwriting and operational risk.
12. **Capital versus prevention:** capital absorbs loss; it does not stop correlated defaults.
13. **Allowance versus capital:** expected-loss estimation and loss-absorbing capital interact but remain distinct mechanisms.
14. **Exposure transfer versus disappearance:** participations, guarantees, insurance, hedges, and sales can leave counterparty, recourse, basis, operational, or reputational risk.
