# Banking Fluency — Day 010

## Title

Liquidity and solvency II: funding and liquid assets

## Place in the course

Day 009 separated liquidity—the ability to meet cash and collateral obligations when due without unacceptable loss—from solvency—the adequacy of asset value and loss-absorbing equity relative to liabilities. This lesson takes the next layer only: how a bank constructs a resilient funding profile and a usable liquid-asset buffer before stress arrives.

## Learning objective

Explain how deposit behavior, wholesale funding, asset liquidity, collateral capacity, and time horizon combine into a bank’s funding-liquidity position; distinguish nominal balance-sheet amounts from cash that is actually usable under stress; and evaluate whether diversification, a liquidity cushion, stress testing, and contingency preparation make the institution more resilient.

## The coherent layer: fund the balance sheet so cash is available when obligations arrive

A bank’s assets and liabilities mature, reprice, and produce cash on different schedules. Many loans return cash slowly. Some deposits can leave immediately. Wholesale borrowings mature on contractual dates. Borrowers may draw unused commitments precisely when markets are strained.

Liquidity management therefore has two connected jobs:

1. **Construct a funding profile** whose providers, maturities, prices, currencies, legal entities, and behavioral characteristics do not all fail together.
2. **Maintain usable liquidity**—cash, marketable unencumbered assets, collateralized borrowing capacity, and credible inflows—that can cover stressed outflows for long enough to execute a contingency plan.

The central question is not “How much funding does the bank have?” It is:

> Which cash sources remain available, in the right entity and currency, by each deadline, after deposit runoff, non-renewal, commitment draws, collateral haircuts, market discounts, operational delays, and deterioration in the bank’s own condition?

A funding source can be cheap but fragile. An asset can be valuable but not readily monetizable. A committed line can exist on paper but fail operationally or contractually. Resilience comes from connecting liability behavior, asset usability, collateral readiness, and stress-specific timing.

**Simplification label:** This pack uses a generic U.S. commercial-bank frame. It is educational, not legal, accounting, investment, supervisory, liquidity, or contingency-planning advice. Deposit categories, regulatory ratios, runoff assumptions, collateral eligibility, insurance treatment, and borrowing access vary by institution, jurisdiction, rule, product, counterparty, and scenario.

## Funding is the liability-and-equity side’s support for assets

Banks fund assets with deposits, secured and unsecured borrowings, other liabilities, and equity. Those sources do not behave alike.

- **Deposits** are obligations to customers. Their contractual maturity may be immediate or fixed, but actual behavior depends on customer purpose, insurance status, rate sensitivity, concentration, service relationships, and confidence.
- **Wholesale funding** is generally obtained from financial markets, institutional counterparties, brokered channels, secured lenders, or other non-retail sources. It can provide scale and term, but may be price-sensitive, collateral-intensive, concentrated, and unwilling to renew in stress.
- **Equity capital** supports the balance sheet and absorbs loss. It can also supply cash when raised, but it is not interchangeable with ordinary funding. A deposit or borrowing adds a repayment obligation; common equity represents a residual claim.

Funding liquidity risk arises when the bank may be unable to obtain funds or retain funding at a reasonable cost in the required amount and time. That risk can surface through withdrawals, non-renewals, higher rates, collateral demands, shorter maturities, reduced market access, or simultaneous draws on commitments.

The FDIC describes funds management as estimating liquidity needs and meeting them cost-effectively across time and adverse scenarios. The OCC emphasizes a diverse mix of existing and potential sources, marketable assets, stress testing, and contingency plans. Neither source treats liquidity as a single cash ratio.

## Deposits are behaviorally different even when the accounting label is the same

“Deposits” is too broad a category for liquidity analysis. Two deposits with identical balances and legal withdrawal rights can have very different observed behavior.

### Retail and small-business relationship deposits

A household’s insured transaction account used for payroll, bills, and daily banking may be less rate-sensitive and operationally harder to move than a deposit placed solely for yield. A small business using the bank for payments, payroll, cards, credit, and treasury services may also have reasons to preserve the relationship.

That does not make any deposit permanently stable. Service failures, fraud, rate gaps, news, financial distress, or easier transfer technology can change behavior rapidly.

### Insured and uninsured balances

Deposit insurance can reduce a depositor’s exposure to bank failure up to applicable limits and conditions. Uninsured depositors bear more direct credit exposure and may have a stronger incentive to move early when they doubt the bank.

Insurance status is not the entire behavioral model. An insured depositor can still leave for price or service. An uninsured operational customer may retain some balance because moving payment, custody, payroll, or credit relationships is costly. Management must examine the actual customer and account purpose rather than use insurance status as a complete answer.

### Operational and non-operational deposits

An operational balance supports clearing, custody, cash management, payroll, settlement, or other services embedded in the customer’s activity. A non-operational balance may be excess cash seeking yield or temporary placement. Operational ties can create friction against withdrawal, but the bank must substantiate the relationship and understand what portion of the balance is genuinely needed for operations.

### Rate-sensitive, brokered, listing-service, and third-party deposits

Funding gathered primarily through price competition or third-party placement can arrive quickly and diversify the names on an account list. It may nevertheless react to the same rate, insurance, placement-agent, or confidence signal. A thousand accounts sourced by one channel are not necessarily a thousand independent funding decisions.

### Public funds, fiduciary funds, and collateralized deposits

Some depositors require the bank to pledge assets or satisfy statutory and policy constraints. The deposit provides cash, but the pledged collateral becomes encumbered and unavailable for other liquidity needs. The net liquidity value can therefore be lower than the gross deposit balance suggests.

### Concentrated commercial deposits

Large accounts from one industry, investor network, platform, geographic market, corporate group, or common adviser may move together. The bank should evaluate concentration by common decision driver—not merely count account holders.

A useful behavioral map asks:

- Who controls the withdrawal decision?
- What portion is insured under applicable rules?
- Why is the balance at this bank: operations, relationship, convenience, yield, collateral arrangement, or temporary placement?
- How quickly can it move?
- Which accounts share an owner, adviser, industry, platform, geography, or information network?
- What happened to comparable balances when rates changed, service failed, or confidence weakened?
- Would the customer’s credit lines be drawn at the same time its deposits leave?

**Simplification label:** Terms such as “stable,” “operational,” “retail,” and “wholesale” can have precise regulatory definitions and prescribed treatment. Here they are used first as behavioral concepts. An actual Liquidity Coverage Ratio, deposit-insurance determination, brokered-deposit classification, call-report treatment, or supervisory assessment requires the applicable current rules and account-level facts.

## Wholesale funding is useful—but its failure modes must be explicit

Wholesale funding is not inherently bad. It can diversify funding beyond local deposits, extend maturities, match particular assets, and provide efficient secured borrowing capacity. The risk is reliance without understanding the conditions under which the source reprices, demands collateral, or disappears.

Common forms include:

- secured advances and repurchase agreements;
- unsecured interbank or capital-markets borrowing;
- large certificates of deposit and institutional placements;
- brokered or third-party-arranged deposits;
- commercial paper or other short-term obligations; and
- longer-term debt.

Five characteristics determine resilience.

### 1. Maturity

A one-year borrowing and an overnight borrowing may have the same balance today but radically different rollover risk. A maturity ladder should show contractual cash needs by day, week, month, and longer horizon. Large maturity cliffs deserve explicit limits and contingency actions.

### 2. Security and collateral

Secured funding may remain available when unsecured credit weakens, but it consumes collateral capacity. Lenders apply eligibility rules, valuations, haircuts, concentration limits, and operational requirements. If collateral value falls, the bank may need to pledge more assets or repay part of the borrowing.

### 3. Counterparty discretion and condition sensitivity

A counterparty may decline renewal, shorten term, widen the spread, demand different collateral, or invoke contractual protections as the bank weakens. “Historically available” is not equivalent to “available in the scenario that creates the need.”

### 4. Price

Funding can remain technically available but become economically damaging. Replacing low-cost deposits with expensive emergency funding compresses net interest income. If expensive funding persists, it can weaken earnings and capital, making future funding still harder.

### 5. Concentration and common failure mode

Ten counterparties exposed to the same market, collateral, credit signal, or intermediary may withdraw together. Diversification requires independent failure modes—not merely more names.

The OCC warns that wholesale providers are often more sensitive to the bank’s credit condition and interest rates, may require the bank’s liquid assets as collateral, and may refuse to extend or roll funding if collateral becomes less liquid or uncertain. This is why wholesale capacity should be stressed rather than counted at its normal-times maximum.

## Funding diversification is more than a source count

A funding dashboard can look diversified while remaining structurally fragile.

Suppose a bank reports:

- retail deposits;
- business deposits;
- brokered deposits;
- secured advances;
- repurchase agreements; and
- unsecured institutional borrowing.

Those are six labels. They may still share one failure mode if they all depend on the bank maintaining strong credit quality, the same collateral pool, the same rate advantage, or the same depositor network.

A rigorous diversification test spans at least:

1. **Provider:** Who makes the funding decision?
2. **Channel:** Is funding sourced directly, through a broker, platform, listing service, or market intermediary?
3. **Instrument:** Deposit, secured borrowing, unsecured borrowing, or capital-market debt?
4. **Maturity:** When can the source leave or demand repayment?
5. **Security:** Which collateral supports it, and what happens if that collateral is downgraded or repriced?
6. **Currency and legal entity:** Can cash be transferred to the entity and currency that needs it without legal, tax, regulatory, or operational blockage?
7. **Behavioral driver:** Is the source motivated by operations, relationship, insurance, yield, credit quality, or market arbitrage?
8. **Stress sensitivity:** Does it survive an institution-specific problem, a market-wide closure, or both?

True diversification means that no plausible common shock removes too much funding before replacement sources can become usable.

**Simplification label:** Diversification does not mean every source receives equal weight, and it does not guarantee survival. Correlations can increase in stress, providers can react to the same public information, and a market-wide event can close otherwise independent channels.

## Asset liquidity: value is not the same as monetization capacity

The asset side can produce cash in three principal ways:

1. **Contractual inflow:** borrowers and issuers pay interest and principal.
2. **Sale:** the bank sells the asset for cash.
3. **Pledge:** the bank borrows against the asset as collateral.

Each route has different timing, value, and operational constraints.

### Cash and central-bank balances

Immediately available cash is the most direct source, subject to legal-entity, currency, reserve-account, settlement, and operational constraints. Cash has little monetization uncertainty but carries an opportunity cost compared with higher-yielding assets.

### Marketable securities

Some government and high-quality securities trade in deep markets and can be sold quickly with relatively modest price impact in many conditions. Other securities may be thinly traded, structurally complex, downgraded, or exposed to large interest-rate and credit changes.

“Marketable” is scenario-dependent. Market depth can evaporate, bid-ask spreads can widen, settlement takes time, and a sale can realize an accounting or economic loss.

### Loans

Loans can generate scheduled inflows and may be pledgeable under eligible programs or private facilities. They are usually slower and harder to sell than standardized securities because buyers need credit data, documentation, valuation, servicing arrangements, and legal transfer capacity. A loan’s expected long-run collection value does not make it same-day cash.

### Committed facilities and borrowing capacity

Borrowing capacity is an asset-monetization channel, not a pile of cash. Capacity depends on eligible collateral, current valuation, haircut, prior liens, documentation, counterparty rules, limits, operational testing, and the institution’s condition. The relevant amount is drawable cash after those constraints—not the face value of assets or the headline facility limit.

## The usable-liquidity test

An asset or facility belongs in the usable buffer only to the extent it passes all of these tests:

- **Owned or controlled:** Does the correct legal entity have the right to use it?
- **Unencumbered:** Is it free of a prior pledge, lien, segregation requirement, or contractual restriction?
- **Eligible:** Will the intended buyer or lender accept it under current rules?
- **Valued:** Is there a current defensible price or collateral value?
- **Haircut-adjusted:** How much cash remains after market discount or lender haircut?
- **Operationally ready:** Are legal documents, account setup, data, collateral files, transfer instructions, approvals, and staff procedures complete?
- **Timely:** Will cash settle before the obligation is due?
- **Currency-matched:** Is cash available in the required currency without relying on a stressed foreign-exchange market?
- **Entity-accessible:** Can it be transferred without violating legal, regulatory, tax, or internal restrictions?
- **Stress-surviving:** Does the source remain usable in the scenario being tested?

This produces a critical distinction:

> Nominal liquidity is the amount visible on reports. Usable liquidity is the amount that can actually become cash, where and when needed, after constraints and stress adjustments.

An asset pledged to secure an existing borrowing cannot also support a second borrowing unless the arrangement legally permits excess collateral release or reuse. Counting both the first borrowing and the full collateral value double-counts liquidity.

## Haircuts, encumbrance, and operational readiness

### Haircut

A haircut reduces the cash a lender advances relative to collateral value. If eligible collateral is valued at $40 million and the applicable stressed haircut is 25 percent, the illustrative borrowing proceeds are $30 million. The haircut protects the lender against valuation and liquidation risk; it also means the bank needs more collateral than the cash it receives.

Haircuts can change with asset type, maturity, credit quality, volatility, concentration, counterparty, and stress. A normal-times assumption should not be carried unchanged into a severe scenario.

### Encumbrance

An encumbered asset is pledged, restricted, or otherwise unavailable for unrestricted monetization. Encumbrance can arise from secured borrowing, public deposits, derivatives, payment systems, fiduciary arrangements, legal restrictions, and other obligations.

The bank needs an enterprise-wide collateral inventory showing location, owner, currency, eligibility, market value, haircut, lien, existing pledge, excess collateral, settlement system, and time to mobilize. Without that inventory, management can count the same asset twice or discover too late that collateral sits in the wrong place.

### Operational readiness

Potential borrowing capacity becomes credible only when the bank can execute. Readiness includes:

- completed agreements and authorizations;
- tested access and current contacts;
- eligible collateral identified and valued;
- required loan files and data accepted;
- collateral transferred or capable of timely transfer;
- staff trained for weekend and after-hours execution;
- payment and settlement paths tested; and
- board and management escalation authority clear.

The Federal Reserve’s SR 10-6 page notes the 2023 interagency addendum reinforcing actionable contingency funding plans. “Actionable” means the plan can be performed under pressure, not merely approved and shelved.

**Simplification label:** The haircut example is teaching arithmetic, not a quote, collateral schedule, or forecast. Actual borrowing value can reflect accrued interest, margins, caps, eligibility exclusions, overcollateralization, concentration limits, legal opinions, valuation frequency, and lender discretion.

## The liquidity cushion: self-insurance with an opportunity cost

A liquidity cushion is a stock of cash and unencumbered liquid assets available to cover stressed needs. It buys time for management to respond without immediately selling illiquid assets or accepting destructive funding terms.

A sound cushion should be:

- sized from institution-specific stress outflows rather than a peer average alone;
- diversified by asset and monetization channel;
- free of legal and operational barriers;
- valued after realistic discounts and haircuts;
- available across relevant entities and currencies;
- tested through actual sale, transfer, and borrowing procedures; and
- governed by limits on encumbrance and minimum survival horizons.

Holding more liquid assets generally lowers immediate liquidity risk but can reduce earnings because cash and highly liquid securities may yield less than loans or less-liquid investments. This trade-off is real, but treating the cushion as “idle money” ignores the option value of surviving stress without forced action.

The buffer is not meant to remain cosmetically untouched during a genuine crisis. The Basel Committee’s Liquidity Coverage Ratio standard says high-quality liquid assets are intended as a defense against stress and may be used during stress, even if the ratio falls below its normal minimum. That principle matters: a buffer that management refuses to deploy is not functioning as a buffer.

## Short-horizon survival and structural funding are different questions

Liquidity has multiple clocks.

### The short horizon

The Basel Liquidity Coverage Ratio, or LCR, is designed to promote resilience over a 30-calendar-day stress period. In simplified conceptual form:

> Stock of unencumbered high-quality liquid assets ÷ total net cash outflows over 30 days

The international standard normally requires at least 100 percent outside stress, while recognizing that the stock may be drawn down in stress. Its scenario specifies categories, runoff rates, inflow treatment, asset eligibility, caps, and haircuts.

The conceptual lesson is not “30 days solves liquidity.” It is that near-term stressed outflows should be covered by assets that can become cash easily and immediately in private markets, subject to the standard’s rules.

### The structural horizon

The Basel Net Stable Funding Ratio, or NSFR, addresses a different problem: whether the bank maintains sufficiently stable funding relative to the liquidity characteristics and maturities of its assets and off-balance-sheet activities over a longer horizon. In simplified conceptual form:

> Available stable funding ÷ required stable funding

The international standard requires at least 100 percent. Longer-term, illiquid assets require more stable funding; funding judged more reliable over the horizon receives more available-stable-funding recognition.

The conceptual lesson is that a bank should not finance a large stock of long-lived, illiquid assets primarily with funding likely to vanish quickly. A large 30-day buffer cannot by itself repair a structurally fragile business model, and stable long-term funding does not eliminate tomorrow morning’s payment needs.

**Simplification label:** These two formulas are conceptual summaries of Basel standards, not calculations for this hypothetical bank and not statements of which U.S. institutions are legally subject to which domestic liquidity rules. The standards contain detailed definitions, weights, caps, consolidation rules, transition provisions, and jurisdictional implementation choices. Internal liquidity management must also cover risks and horizons that regulatory minima do not capture.

## Stress testing converts categories into behavior

Contractual maturity is the starting point, not the final cash-flow forecast. A useful liquidity stress converts accounts, instruments, commitments, and collateral into scenario-specific cash flows.

### Outflow assumptions can include

- withdrawal or runoff by depositor segment;
- non-renewal of maturing wholesale funding;
- loss of unsecured borrowing;
- higher secured-funding haircuts and collateral calls;
- draws on unused credit commitments;
- derivative, payment, and settlement needs;
- operational expenses and legal-entity transfers; and
- cash consequences of rating, covenant, or market changes.

### Inflow and capacity assumptions can include

- scheduled loan and securities payments, reduced for delay or default;
- proceeds from asset sales after price discounts and settlement time;
- secured borrowing after eligibility, encumbrance, and haircut;
- deposit inflows or retention supported by actual behavior;
- access to committed facilities after contractual and condition-sensitive constraints; and
- feasible management actions, with realistic execution time.

### Scenarios should be distinct

1. **Institution-specific stress:** concerns about this bank cause deposit outflows, lost unsecured access, wider spreads, and collateral pressure.
2. **Market-wide stress:** funding markets tighten, asset liquidity weakens, and many institutions seek cash simultaneously.
3. **Combined stress:** the bank weakens during a broad market disruption, so neither market access nor franchise confidence can be assumed.

Stress severity should be severe enough to inform action, not calibrated merely to produce a passing result. Historical observations matter, but they may understate faster digital transfers, concentrated depositor networks, or a shock not present in the historical window.

## Worked case: a diversified-looking bank with a $36 million stressed gap

Consider a deliberately simplified bank forecasting one stressed horizon. Its modeled outflows are:

| Outflow | Balance or exposure | Stress assumption | Cash outflow |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Insured retail deposits | $120m | 5% runoff | $6m |
| Uninsured operational commercial deposits | $80m | 15% runoff | $12m |
| Concentrated uninsured non-operational deposits | $100m | 45% runoff | $45m |
| Maturing wholesale funding | $40m | 75% not renewed | $30m |
| Unused credit commitments | $20m | 50% drawn | $10m |
| Other contractual and contingent needs | — | scenario amount | $7m |
| **Total stressed outflows** |  |  | **$110m** |

Its available sources are:

| Source | Nominal amount | Stress adjustment | Usable cash |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Immediately available cash | $8m | none in this illustration | $8m |
| Unencumbered marketable securities | $30m | 10% sale discount/haircut | $27m |
| Eligible, unencumbered collateral | $40m | 25% borrowing haircut | $30m |
| Contractual inflows | $12m | 75% arrives on time | $9m |
| **Total usable sources** |  |  | **$74m** |

The stressed gap is:

> $110 million outflows − $74 million usable sources = $36 million shortfall

The diagnostic value lies in the adjustments, not the precision of the assumed percentages.

Management should ask:

1. Does “insured retail” hide households controlled by one wealth manager or platform?
2. Is the operational portion of commercial deposits evidenced, or is management labeling excess cash as sticky?
3. Are securities truly unencumbered, and can they settle within the horizon?
4. Is the $40 million collateral pool eligible, documented, valued, and transferable before cash is due?
5. Does the collateral haircut increase in the same scenario that causes the outflows?
6. Will commitment draws exceed 50 percent because borrowers lose other funding?
7. Are inflows from the same borrowers whose conditions are deteriorating?
8. Which contingency actions close the $36 million gap, who authorizes them, and by what deadline?

If management responds by adding $36 million of overnight funding, it may close the immediate arithmetic while worsening rollover concentration and funding cost. If it sells assets rapidly, it may create cash while realizing losses. If it raises durable term funding or equity before stress, reduces concentrated deposits and commitments, improves collateral readiness, or changes the asset mix, it may improve the underlying structure. Each response has different liquidity, earnings, capital, and execution consequences.

**Simplification label:** This illustration is not an LCR, NSFR, regulatory report, contingency forecast, or recommendation. It omits inflow caps, regulatory classifications, taxes, accrued interest, intraday needs, collateral calls, entity and currency restrictions, deposit-insurance nuances, market-price feedback, capital effects, behavioral interaction, and uncertainty ranges. The assumed runoff rates and haircuts are invented solely to teach the mechanism.

## Contingency funding planning: pre-decide before the clock accelerates

A contingency funding plan connects early-warning signals, decision authority, funding actions, collateral operations, and communication. It should answer:

- What triggers each escalation stage?
- Who declares a liquidity event?
- Which committee, executives, and board members have authority at each stage?
- Which sources are used first, and what are their cash amounts after haircut?
- Which assets are saleable or pledgeable, where are they held, and how long does mobilization take?
- Which deposit and wholesale concentrations receive real-time monitoring?
- What actions preserve liquidity, and which actions could worsen confidence or capital?
- How are legal entities, currencies, payment obligations, and collateral movements coordinated?
- Which supervisors, counterparties, service providers, and internal teams must be contacted?
- How are public and customer communications kept accurate and consistent with the facts?
- How often is the plan operationally tested, and how are failures remediated?

A list of theoretical sources is not a plan. Each source needs an owner, current contact, legal authority, expected capacity, collateral requirement, lead time, operational procedure, and scenario in which it remains credible.

Contingency actions should also be sequenced by side effect. Selling the most liquid assets first creates cash quickly but shrinks the future cushion. Pledging the best collateral first can leave weaker assets for later. Raising deposit rates may retain funds but increase costs and attract highly rate-sensitive money. Curtailing lending preserves cash but can damage customers, franchise value, and future earnings. The plan must show not only what can be done, but what the balance sheet looks like after it is done.

## Management dashboard: the minimum decision view

A useful daily or more frequent stress dashboard should connect:

1. **Cash-flow gaps:** discrete and cumulative gaps by intraday, overnight, seven-day, 30-day, and longer horizons.
2. **Deposit behavior:** insured status, customer purpose, concentration, channel, rate sensitivity, geography, common control, and recent movement.
3. **Wholesale maturity ladder:** counterparty, instrument, secured status, collateral, maturity, renewal assumption, and spread.
4. **Liquid-asset inventory:** market value, accounting classification, price sensitivity, expected sale discount, settlement time, and legal owner.
5. **Collateral inventory:** eligibility, encumbrance, haircut, location, currency, excess collateral, and time to mobilize.
6. **Borrowing capacity:** tested cash capacity by facility after all constraints—not gross line size.
7. **Contingent outflows:** commitments, derivatives, guarantees, payment-system needs, and collateral calls.
8. **Stress results:** institution-specific, market-wide, and combined scenarios with survival horizon.
9. **Limits and triggers:** breaches, trends, responsible owner, escalation deadline, and required action.
10. **Contingency status:** actions completed, remaining capacity, operational defects, and next decision point.

Reconciliation matters. Deposit, treasury, collateral, loan, securities, derivatives, and payment data may sit in different systems. A polished dashboard built on stale balances, duplicate collateral, or missing commitments can be more dangerous than an acknowledged data gap.

## Institutional operator scenario

A regional bank reports strong liquidity because it has $600 million of “available sources” against $400 million of uninsured deposits. The source total includes:

- $150 million of cash and securities;
- $200 million of borrowing capacity against loans;
- $100 million of unused secured facilities;
- $100 million of brokered-deposit capacity; and
- $50 million of expected loan inflows.

Review reveals:

- $60 million of the securities are pledged for public deposits and derivatives;
- the loan-based borrowing estimate uses face value, not collateral value after haircut;
- many loan files have not been delivered or tested with the intended lender;
- the unused secured facilities depend on the same collateral pool as the loan-based capacity;
- brokered-deposit capacity assumes the bank can continue paying the highest market rate and remains eligible under applicable restrictions;
- expected inflows come partly from commercial borrowers likely to draw their unused lines during stress;
- uninsured deposits are concentrated among firms funded by the same venture investors and connected through one communications network; and
- most wholesale funding matures within 45 days.

A disciplined operator should:

1. remove encumbered assets from the unrestricted pool;
2. eliminate double-counting between facilities that rely on the same collateral;
3. apply current collateral values, eligibility rules, concentration limits, and stressed haircuts;
4. distinguish tested, same-day borrowing capacity from potential future capacity;
5. segment deposits by insurance, purpose, common control, network, rate sensitivity, and observed behavior;
6. model deposit runoff, wholesale non-renewal, commitment draws, delayed inflows, and collateral calls together;
7. calculate gaps by deadline, legal entity, and currency;
8. identify actions that remain executable in institution-specific, market-wide, and combined stress;
9. operationally test collateral transfer, borrowing, settlement, and escalation; and
10. report the resulting usable liquidity and survival horizon—not the $600 million headline.

No Bitcoin or Galoy application is needed. This institutional funding-and-buffer mechanism should stand on its own.

## What must not be confused

1. **Deposit balance versus deposit stability:** accounting amount does not predict behavior.
2. **Contractual maturity versus behavioral life:** an on-demand deposit may remain for years, while a term source may still create a sharp maturity cliff.
3. **Insured versus immobile:** insurance can reduce failure exposure but does not prevent withdrawal for rate, service, or convenience.
4. **Operational label versus proven operational balance:** the bank must evidence customer purpose and the amount genuinely tied to services.
5. **More account names versus diversification:** one broker, adviser, platform, industry, or social network can coordinate many accounts.
6. **Wholesale funding versus bad funding:** wholesale sources can add term and diversity, but their price, collateral, rollover, and condition sensitivity must be controlled.
7. **Facility limit versus drawable cash:** eligibility, collateral, haircut, documentation, conditions, and lead time determine usable capacity.
8. **Asset value versus liquid value:** a valuable asset may be slow to sell or pledge.
9. **Unencumbered versus unidentified:** if the bank cannot locate, value, and transfer an asset, practical availability is doubtful.
10. **Gross collateral versus borrowing proceeds:** haircuts and caps reduce cash received.
11. **Pledged asset versus remaining cushion:** existing encumbrance consumes flexibility and can create double-counting.
12. **Normal-market liquidity versus stressed liquidity:** prices, haircuts, settlement, and counterparties can change together.
13. **LCR versus complete liquidity management:** a 30-day regulatory metric does not capture every horizon, entity, currency, intraday need, or institution-specific risk.
14. **NSFR versus immediate cash:** structural stable funding does not pay an obligation due this afternoon.
15. **Passing a model versus being ready:** assumptions, data, authorities, documents, people, and operational testing determine execution.
16. **Buffer versus decoration:** liquid assets are held to be usable in stress, with appropriate governance.
17. **Contingency source versus contingency plan:** a source without amount, owner, conditions, timing, and tested procedure is only a possibility.
18. **Closing today’s gap versus restoring resilience:** overnight replacement funding can postpone rather than solve a structural mismatch.

1. **Retrieval from Day 009:** A bank owns assets worth more than its liabilities but cannot meet tomorrow’s withdrawals. Why does that describe a liquidity diagnosis rather than prove insolvency, and what two facts would you verify before counting an asset as usable cash?
2. A bank reports six funding categories and a large securities portfolio. Explain how both can provide false diversification. Name one common funding failure mode and four tests an asset must pass before it belongs in the stressed liquidity buffer.
3. **Retrieval across Days 003 and 008:** Why can replacing relationship deposits with expensive short-term wholesale funding simultaneously weaken net interest income and amplify a portfolio stress? Give the causal chain and identify the assumption you would test first.

## Voice memo request

Ask for a 60-to-90-second response containing all three:

- **Explanation:** Explain the difference between nominal funding, stable funding, and usable liquidity, including why a facility limit or valuable asset is not automatically cash.
- **Application:** Apply the framework to the regional-bank scenario: identify the most important double-count, deposit concentration, and operational test you would require before accepting management’s $600 million claim.
- **Uncertainty:** Name the runoff, renewal, collateral, haircut, settlement, inflow, or commitment-draw assumption you trust least and say how you would verify it.
