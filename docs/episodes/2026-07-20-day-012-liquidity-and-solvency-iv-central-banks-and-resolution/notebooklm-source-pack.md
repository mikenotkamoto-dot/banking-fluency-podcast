# Banking Fluency — Day 012

## Title

Liquidity and solvency IV: central banks and resolution

## Place in the course

Day 011 explained how a run can accelerate from withdrawals into forced sales, realized losses, weaker capital, and contagion. This lesson adds the next layer only: what public authorities can do once private liquidity is no longer enough, which problem each intervention solves, and where liquidity support must give way to bank resolution.

## Learning objective

Explain the boundary among central-bank lending, deposit insurance, and resolution; diagnose whether an intervention is providing time, protecting a covered claim, or restructuring a failed institution; trace how an insured U.S. bank moves from stress through closure and FDIC receivership; and identify who remains exposed when authorities preserve critical functions without preserving the failed bank, its managers, or its owners.

## The coherent layer: public intervention must match the problem

A banking crisis can present three different public-policy problems:

1. **A cash-timing problem at a viable institution.** The bank needs liquidity now but may still have enough asset value and capital to repay its obligations over time.
2. **A depositor-protection problem at a failed insured institution.** Covered depositors need reliable access to insured funds even though their bank can no longer continue normally.
3. **A nonviability problem.** Losses, funding collapse, governance failure, or another condition has made the institution unable to continue safely; its assets, liabilities, contracts, and critical operations must be transferred, reorganized, or wound down under legal authority.

The matching tools are different:

- **Central-bank lending** converts eligible collateral into temporary cash. It adds both cash and a repayment obligation.
- **Deposit insurance** protects qualifying deposit claims under applicable coverage rules. It does not repair the failed bank’s capital.
- **Resolution** changes control and legal relationships after failure: authorities may transfer deposits and assets, create a temporary bridge institution, pay insured deposits, sell operations, and administer remaining claims.

This distinction is the core of the lesson:

> Liquidity support can buy time for a viable bank. Deposit insurance can protect covered depositors. Resolution can preserve selected functions while imposing the consequences of failure. None of the three makes underlying losses disappear.

A crisis response becomes confused when the tool and diagnosis are mismatched. Lending against collateral cannot recapitalize a bank whose losses exceed its equity. Deposit insurance protects depositors, not the bank’s shareholders. Resolution does not mean every operation must stop, but continuing deposits and payments does not mean the old institution survived.

**Simplification label:** This pack uses a generic U.S. FDIC-insured commercial-bank frame. It is educational, not legal, accounting, supervisory, monetary-policy, deposit-insurance, insolvency, receivership, or resolution advice. Credit unions have a separate federal insurance and resolution framework administered by the NCUA. State law, charter, institution type, legal entity, account ownership, collateral, regulator, and facts can change the applicable process.

## A decision map before the details

When a bank is under severe stress, ask four questions in order.

### 1. What payment must be met, and by when?

Identify withdrawals, payment-system obligations, collateral calls, maturing funding, operating expenses, and committed credit draws. A bank can fail on an intraday clock even when a monthly balance-sheet report looks adequate.

### 2. What usable collateral or saleable assets can produce cash before that deadline?

Apply the Day 011 tests: ownership, encumbrance, eligibility, defensible value, haircut, documentation, operational readiness, legal-entity access, currency, and settlement time. A central bank can lend only under its governing authority and program terms; a theoretical pool of assets is not automatic access.

### 3. After realistic valuation, can the bank bear its losses and repay the new borrowing?

This is the solvency and viability question. The answer must include existing losses, forced-sale effects, replacement-funding cost, future earnings capacity, capital, and the value remaining after pledged creditors are paid. More borrowing can bridge a timing gap, but it can also postpone recognition while consuming unencumbered collateral.

### 4. If viability cannot be restored, which functions and claims must be protected or continued through resolution?

The analysis shifts from “How do we save this legal entity?” to:

- How do insured depositors receive prompt access?
- Which deposits, assets, contracts, branches, payment functions, and employees transfer to an acquiring or bridge institution?
- Which assets and claims remain in receivership?
- Who absorbs losses under the applicable priority?
- How can authorities avoid unnecessary destruction of value and wider instability?

These questions can run in parallel during fast stress. The bank, supervisors, central bank, chartering authority, deposit insurer, and potential acquirers have different mandates and information. A disciplined response makes those roles explicit.

## Central-bank lending: cash against collateral, not loss absorption

### What the U.S. discount window does

The Federal Reserve’s discount window allows eligible depository institutions and U.S. branches and agencies of foreign banks to borrow from Federal Reserve Banks after completing legal agreements and pledging collateral. The Federal Reserve describes the window as supporting banking-system stability, monetary-policy implementation, liquidity-risk management, and the continued flow of credit.

Its three programs are:

- **Primary credit**, the main program, for institutions in generally sound financial condition;
- **Secondary credit**, for institutions not eligible for primary credit, at a higher rate; and
- **Seasonal credit**, for qualifying smaller institutions with recurring seasonal funding needs.

All discount-window loans must be collateralized to the satisfaction of the lending Reserve Bank. The institution must establish the legal and operational arrangements and pledge collateral before it can borrow. The Reserve Bank values collateral and lends less than the collateral’s face amount through margins or haircuts designed to protect the lender.

The balance-sheet effect of a simplified $100 loan is:

- bank cash rises by $100;
- central-bank borrowing rises by $100;
- eligible collateral becomes pledged or encumbered; and
- equity does not rise merely because the bank borrowed.

If the bank had a $40 capital hole before the loan, the loan does not fill that hole. It provides cash and a liability. If the bank was viable but temporarily illiquid, the cash can prevent destructive asset sales and allow obligations to be met while normal funding returns or an orderly private transaction completes.

### Why a central bank can stop a self-reinforcing liquidity spiral

Suppose a bank holds a portfolio of performing loans expected to repay over several years. Depositors demand cash today. Selling the loans immediately may require a deep discount because buyers need time to review files, model credit, arrange servicing, and fund the purchase.

If the bank can pledge acceptable assets and borrow enough cash before the withdrawal deadline, it may avoid the chain:

> withdrawal → forced sale → realized loss → weaker capital → more concern → more withdrawal

The intervention works by changing the time constraint. It does not assert that every asset is worth par. It creates a secured bridge from slow asset cash flows to immediate payment needs, subject to eligibility, valuation, haircut, term, interest, documentation, and condition requirements.

This is the economic logic behind the phrase **lender of last resort**: an official liquidity backstop can lend when private funding is unavailable or destabilizing, helping prevent a cash shortage from needlessly destroying a viable institution or forcing credit withdrawal across the economy.

### The boundary: lending is not a solvency guarantee

A bank can possess collateral and still be nonviable. Consider three simplified cases.

#### Case A: viable but illiquid

- Assets have a defensible economic value of $1.05 billion.
- Liabilities are $1.00 billion.
- The bank has $50 million of economic equity.
- It needs $120 million today.
- It has sufficient eligible, unencumbered collateral to support the borrowing.

A temporary secured loan may bridge the timing gap. The bank still bears interest cost, must repay, and must restore a sustainable funding position.

#### Case B: solvent on a central estimate, but highly uncertain

- Estimated asset value is $1.03 billion, but a severe plausible range is $900 million to $1.08 billion.
- Liabilities are $1.00 billion.
- Asset files are incomplete and collateral eligibility is uncertain.
- Deposits are leaving faster than valuations can be completed.

The diagnosis cannot rest on management’s central estimate. Authorities need conservative valuation, current collateral data, condition assessment, repayment analysis, and a credible path to viability. Limited liquidity might buy time under applicable rules, but uncertainty is not itself proof of solvency.

#### Case C: insolvent

- Assets are economically worth $900 million.
- Liabilities are $1.00 billion.
- Equity is therefore negative $100 million before further costs.

A $150 million secured loan supplies cash but also adds $150 million of debt. It does not create the missing $100 million of net worth. Pledging the best assets may leave fewer unencumbered assets for other creditors and can increase loss if failure is only delayed.

The central bank therefore is not merely asking, “Is there an asset with a face value?” The relevant institutional questions include eligibility, collateral value after haircut, legal priority, borrower condition, purpose, term, repayment capacity, and whether continued lending is consistent with law and policy.

**Simplification label:** The three cases are invented teaching examples, not Federal Reserve eligibility determinations or accounting measurements. “Solvent,” “viable,” “generally sound,” and “adequate collateral” are not interchangeable legal tests. Actual central-bank credit decisions are made under the Federal Reserve Act, Regulation A, Reserve Bank procedures, supervisory information, and institution-specific facts.

### Readiness is part of access

The Federal Reserve’s readiness guidance emphasizes that institutions should establish borrowing documents, pledge collateral, maintain internal approvals, identify contacts, and conduct test transactions in normal conditions. This is not clerical trivia.

A bank that waits until a run to discover that:

- the board resolution is stale;
- the correct officials lack authority;
- loan schedules do not reconcile;
- a blanket lien already covers the assets;
- collateral files have not been accepted;
- transfer accounts are untested; or
- staff do not know the cutoff and settlement process

may have economic collateral but no timely cash.

Emergency liquidity is therefore partly a governance and operations capability. The institution should know before stress:

1. which legal entity can borrow;
2. which assets can be pledged;
3. their current lendable value after margins;
4. existing liens and encumbrance;
5. documentation and data defects;
6. who can authorize and execute a draw;
7. how quickly funds settle; and
8. how borrowing changes the remaining collateral map.

### Stigma and the collective-action problem

Banks may hesitate to use an official facility because they fear that counterparties, customers, investors, or markets will interpret borrowing as evidence of weakness. If every institution avoids a backstop for reputational reasons, a tool designed to prevent forced sales may be used too late.

The correct control is not to assume stigma away. Management and the board should:

- treat readiness and test borrowing as ordinary contingency preparation;
- compare the full economic and operational cost of each source;
- avoid unsupported public claims;
- plan communications consistent with disclosure and confidentiality rules; and
- escalate before the survival horizon collapses.

Borrowing can be prudent liquidity management or a sign of severe distress; the fact of borrowing alone does not answer which. The diagnosis depends on condition, purpose, collateral, size, duration, alternatives, and repayment path.

## Deposit insurance: protection of covered claims, not preservation of the bank

### What is insured

For an FDIC-insured bank, deposit insurance protects eligible deposits if the bank fails. The standard amount is at least $250,000 **per depositor, per insured bank, for each account ownership category**, subject to detailed rules.

Covered deposit products generally include checking accounts, savings accounts, money market deposit accounts, certificates of deposit, and certain official items issued by the bank. Deposit insurance does not cover stocks, bonds, mutual funds, annuities, life-insurance policies, crypto assets, or other non-deposit investments merely because an insured bank sold or held them.

Coverage is not determined by adding every account number and giving each one a separate $250,000 limit. The FDIC aggregates deposits held by the same depositor in the same ownership category at the same insured bank, then applies the rules for that category. Trust, joint, retirement, government, fiduciary, and other categories require their own facts and records.

### What insurance changes during a run

Insurance changes the covered depositor’s exposure to loss upon bank failure. If the depositor expects prompt access to insured funds, the incentive to race other depositors solely to avoid credit loss is reduced.

Insurance does **not** mean:

- the bank cannot fail;
- every product at the bank is insured;
- every dollar of every depositor is covered;
- funds are insured at a fintech or nonbank merely because the nonbank says it uses a partner bank;
- the FDIC supplies operating cash to management before failure as ordinary funding;
- shareholders or bank debt investors are insured; or
- losses disappear.

The Deposit Insurance Fund is funded principally by assessments on insured institutions and income on its investments. When an insured bank fails, the FDIC can use the fund to satisfy its insurance obligation and support the selected resolution method. The fund then has a claim in the receivership through subrogation to the insured deposits it paid or assumed.

### Access can be restored by transfer rather than by reviving the failed bank

The most visible depositor outcome may be seamless: a customer’s insured account appears at another bank, checks and cards continue, and branches reopen. Legally and economically, however, the old institution may have been closed and placed into receivership.

This distinction matters:

> Continuity of a deposit relationship is not continuity of the failed bank’s ownership, charter, management, or equity.

The FDIC may arrange a purchase-and-assumption transaction in which an acquiring institution assumes deposits and purchases some or all assets. If no suitable acquirer is available, the FDIC can use a deposit payout or another lawful structure. A temporary bridge depository institution can continue selected operations while the FDIC seeks a final resolution.

**Simplification label:** The $250,000 statement is a general educational summary, not an account-specific insurance determination. Coverage depends on current law, the insured status of the bank, depositor identity, ownership category, account records, pass-through requirements where relevant, and other facts. The FDIC—not the bank, app, or this pack—makes the determination.

## Resolution: preserve value and critical functions without pretending failure did not occur

### What triggers the change in regime

An open bank is managed by its board and executives under its charter and supervision. When the appropriate chartering authority closes a failing insured depository institution and appoints the FDIC as receiver, control changes.

The FDIC as receiver takes custody of the failed bank’s records, premises, loans, and other assets. It reconciles the closing books, administers claims, transfers or liquidates assets and liabilities, and uses statutory receivership powers. The receivership process is designed for a failed insured depository institution; it is distinct from keeping an open bank alive through liquidity support.

This produces a clean boundary:

- **Before closure:** management, the board, supervisors, private counterparties, and any eligible official lender work on liquidity, capital, recovery, sale, or other measures under their authorities.
- **At closure:** the chartering authority closes the bank and the FDIC is appointed receiver.
- **After closure:** the receiver executes the resolution, provides for insured deposits, administers the failed estate, and allocates recoveries under law.

The FDIC often prepares before closure by gathering data, valuing assets, marketing the institution confidentially, qualifying bidders, and comparing transaction structures. Preparation can reduce interruption and loss, but preparation does not itself transfer control before the legal closing.

### The least-cost discipline

For an ordinary failed-bank resolution, the FDIC evaluates bids and resolution methods to identify the option least costly to the Deposit Insurance Fund, subject to the governing statutory framework. The comparison can include:

- cash and other consideration received;
- assets transferred or retained;
- liabilities assumed;
- the cost of paying or transferring insured deposits;
- guarantees or loss-sharing obligations;
- estimated asset recoveries;
- administrative and receivership expense; and
- timing and uncertainty.

“Least cost” is not the same as “highest headline bid” or “no disruption at any price.” A bid that assumes more deposits but requires a large FDIC payment may cost more than another structure. A rapid liquidation can destroy value. A bridge may preserve franchise value but incurs operating and execution costs. The FDIC models the net effect on the fund.

### Three core resolution methods

#### 1. Purchase and assumption

An acquiring insured institution assumes some or all deposits and purchases some or all assets. Variants can transfer different asset pools and liabilities. This is often less disruptive for customers because deposit access and banking relationships can continue at the acquirer.

The receiver retains whatever the acquirer does not take and administers those assets and claims. “Another bank bought it” therefore does not necessarily mean every asset, contract, creditor, employee, or legal entity transferred.

#### 2. Deposit payout or insured-deposit transfer

If a suitable transaction is unavailable, the FDIC can pay insured depositors directly or arrange for insured deposits to be transferred to another institution. Uninsured amounts become claims against the receivership and may receive recoveries as assets are collected, subject to applicable priority and the estate’s value.

A payout protects insured deposits; it does not promise full recovery to every claimant.

#### 3. Bridge depository institution

A bridge institution is a new, temporary institution established after closure to receive selected assets and liabilities and continue operations while the FDIC arranges a final transaction. It can preserve access, payment functions, customer relationships, staff capability, and franchise value when an immediate sale is impractical.

A bridge is a resolution tool, not proof that the failed bank recovered. The former institution has entered receivership. The bridge has temporary governance and an exit strategy, such as sale, merger, or another resolution.

### Losses remain and must be allocated

When a bank’s assets cannot satisfy all claims, resolution determines how available value is distributed under legal priority. The FDIC’s 2025 Resolutions Handbook summarizes the order for its least-cost analysis as:

1. administrative claims of the receiver;
2. secured depositors and preferred creditors to the value of their collateral, with deficiencies generally falling into the applicable unsecured class;
3. depositor claims, including the FDIC as subrogee for insured deposits and uninsured depositors;
4. general unsecured creditors;
5. subordinated creditors; and
6. shareholder or member obligations.

Creditors in a class must be paid in full before the next class receives payment. The exact treatment can depend on law and facts, but the broad logic is essential: equity is residual and stands behind creditors. Protecting insured deposits does not rescue shareholders.

The receiver may sell assets over time rather than dump them immediately. Better recoveries can reduce losses to the Deposit Insurance Fund and improve distributions to eligible receivership claimants. Speed matters for depositor access; patience may matter for asset recovery. Resolution must manage both clocks.

**Simplification label:** The resolution-method descriptions and priority list summarize the FDIC’s handbook for instruction. They omit statutory exceptions, secured-claim details, setoff, netting, qualified financial contracts, foreign deposits, pass-through coverage, taxes, administrative expenses, litigation, repudiation, asset-specific liens, and institution-specific transaction terms. They are not a forecast of any claimant’s recovery.

## Resolution is not the same as bailout

“Bailout” is often used so broadly that it obscures who actually benefits and who absorbs loss. Analyze a crisis action claim by claim.

Ask:

- Did the old shareholders retain value, or was their equity eliminated?
- Did management remain, or was it removed?
- Were insured depositors paid under the standing insurance framework?
- Were uninsured depositors protected beyond ordinary coverage?
- Were secured creditors paid from their collateral?
- Did unsecured or subordinated debt absorb loss?
- Did an acquirer purchase assets and assume liabilities?
- Did the Deposit Insurance Fund incur cost?
- Were any fund losses recovered through assessments on insured banks?
- Did the central bank make a collateralized loan that must be repaid?
- Did taxpayers provide capital or bear ultimate loss?
- Were critical services continued through a bridge while the failed entity entered receivership?

These are different distributions of risk. Keeping payroll deposits available can protect customers and payment stability while shareholders lose everything. A secured central-bank loan can provide liquidity without injecting equity. A government capital injection would be a different instrument again.

The internationally agreed Financial Stability Board objective is instructive: effective resolution should make failure feasible without severe systemic disruption or taxpayer loss, preserve vital economic functions, and allow shareholders and unsecured and uninsured creditors to absorb losses in accordance with the claims hierarchy. Resolution tools can include transfer to a buyer, a bridge institution, creditor-financed recapitalization, or orderly liquidation.

That objective is not a promise that every real-world resolution achieves zero public cost or perfect continuity. It is the design principle: preserve the function where necessary, not the private claim regardless of loss.

## Exceptional systemic protection does not erase the boundary

Ordinary deposit insurance covers insured deposits under its rules. Ordinary least-cost resolution does not automatically protect uninsured deposits in full.

In March 2023, after Silicon Valley Bank and Signature Bank were closed, U.S. authorities invoked the statutory systemic-risk process and completed resolutions that protected all depositors. The joint Treasury–Federal Reserve–FDIC statement also said:

- shareholders and certain unsecured debtholders would not be protected;
- senior management had been removed;
- losses to the Deposit Insurance Fund associated with supporting uninsured depositors would be recovered through a special assessment on banks; and
- the Federal Reserve would make additional funding available to eligible depository institutions.

This episode demonstrates that deposit protection, loss allocation, resolution, and system-wide liquidity can be used together but remain separate instruments.

The FDIC’s official 2023 deposit-insurance reform report explains the legal boundary in practical terms: if protecting uninsured deposits is not the least-cost resolution, the FDIC cannot do so without a systemic-risk determination by the Treasury Secretary acting on recommendations from the FDIC and Federal Reserve and after consultation with the President.

The lesson is not “uninsured deposits are always protected.” The lesson is:

1. ordinary coverage has a defined limit and ownership rules;
2. uninsured balances are ordinarily exposed to receivership recovery;
3. broader protection requires a specific exceptional legal process when statutory conditions are met; and
4. even broader depositor protection does not necessarily protect shareholders, management, or all creditors.

Expectations matter. If market participants assume every uninsured deposit will always be protected, run incentives may fall, but market discipline and bank risk-taking can change. If authorities credibly impose loss in one case, exposed depositors at similar banks may run. Policy must weigh stability, moral hazard, legal authority, cost, and consistency rather than use “confidence” as an unlimited mandate.

**Simplification label:** This is a compressed teaching account of the March 2023 actions, not a complete legal analysis or policy endorsement. The systemic-risk exception is exceptional, fact-specific, and procedurally constrained. The cited official statements and statutory materials govern; one episode does not create a universal guarantee for uninsured deposits.

## The intervention ladder—and why it is not a guaranteed sequence

A useful analytical ladder is:

### Level 1: private liquidity and balance-sheet action

The bank uses cash, sells assets, pledges collateral privately, retains deposits, reduces new commitments, raises term funding, raises equity, or seeks a private sale.

### Level 2: ordinary central-bank liquidity

An eligible institution borrows under established terms against satisfactory collateral. The purpose is to meet cash needs and reduce destabilizing fire sales, not to transfer losses to the central bank.

### Level 3: supervisory recovery and transaction measures

Management and authorities intensify monitoring, require corrective action, restrict risk, pursue capital or a sale, and prepare for possible failure. Liquidity, capital, governance, and operational workstreams converge.

### Level 4: closure and resolution

The chartering authority closes the bank, the FDIC becomes receiver, and insured deposits and critical functions are transferred, continued temporarily, or paid while the failed estate is administered.

### Level 5: exceptional system-wide measures

If legally authorized and justified by broader instability, authorities may deploy extraordinary liquidity, guarantees, systemic-risk exceptions, or other measures. Each has separate approval, eligibility, funding, and loss-allocation rules.

The ladder is not a promise that every bank receives every level. A nonviable institution may move quickly to closure. A bank may be unable to produce acceptable collateral. A private transaction may resolve stress without official lending. An institution can borrow and still fail later. Resolution planning should begin before liquidity is exhausted because a last-minute process can destroy franchise value and operational continuity.

## Worked scenario: when does liquidity support become delay?

A $12 billion regional bank experiences a networked run after disclosing losses on long-duration securities.

At 8:00 a.m. management reports:

- $900 million of cash and same-day inflows;
- $2.0 billion of securities at carrying value;
- $2.5 billion of loans described as “pledgeable”;
- $2.2 billion of withdrawal instructions expected by close;
- $300 million of likely credit-line draws;
- $1.4 billion of common equity on the last published balance sheet; and
- primary-credit access “available if needed.”

By 10:00 a.m. review finds:

- $500 million of securities are already pledged;
- selling the remaining securities today would realize $250 million of losses;
- the loan pool has an estimated lendable value of $1.3 billion after exclusions and haircut, but only $500 million has completed collateral documentation;
- another lender has a blanket lien that may affect priority;
- current credit-loss estimates add $600 million of losses not reflected in the published capital number;
- expensive replacement funding would reduce annual pre-provision earnings materially;
- withdrawals are accelerating through common advisers;
- a potential acquirer wants a loss-sharing arrangement; and
- operations can process only a limited volume of collateral files before cutoff.

### Step 1: establish the cash need

Expected same-day outflows are at least:

- $2.2 billion withdrawals; plus
- $300 million credit draws;
- less $900 million cash and same-day inflows;
- leaving a preliminary $1.6 billion gap before other payments and contingency effects.

The arithmetic is illustrative. The operator must reconcile queued payments, returned items, settlement accounts, intraday credit, legal entities, and timing.

### Step 2: establish executable borrowing capacity

Management’s $2.5 billion “pledgeable” loan balance is not capacity. Only $500 million has completed documentation, and even that amount requires confirmation of lien priority, eligibility, valuation, haircut, transfer, approval, and settlement before cutoff.

Securities already pledged cannot be counted again. The remaining securities can produce cash but at a realized loss that reduces capital.

### Step 3: update solvency and viability

Published equity of $1.4 billion is not the final answer. The bank must incorporate:

- $600 million additional credit-loss estimate;
- $250 million potential securities-sale loss;
- emergency-funding cost;
- franchise deterioration;
- operational and transaction expense; and
- uncertainty around remaining asset values.

A rough teaching bridge would leave $550 million before additional effects if both stated losses reduced equity. That is not a regulatory-capital calculation or a verified valuation. It shows why liquidity and solvency must be updated together.

### Step 4: compare two paths

#### Path A: temporary liquidity with credible viability

This path may be defensible if conservative analysis shows:

- positive capital remains under severe but plausible loss estimates;
- sufficient acceptable collateral can be perfected in time;
- the bank can repay without assuming a return of fragile deposits;
- governance can execute a capital, funding, or private transaction plan; and
- continued operation does not deepen losses or prejudice resolution.

The loan buys time. It is not the recovery plan itself.

#### Path B: borrowing merely delays nonviability

Resolution preparation should dominate if:

- losses consume capital under a defensible valuation;
- collateral is insufficient or inaccessible by the deadline;
- repayment depends on implausible deposit return or asset prices;
- continued borrowing encumbers the best assets while losses grow;
- no credible capital or transaction solution exists; or
- operations and governance cannot execute safely.

The relevant objective becomes prompt depositor access, continuity of critical functions, a competitive transaction where possible, and disciplined loss allocation—not keeping the old legal entity open for another day.

### The board packet

A useful board and authority decision view would show:

1. cash needs by hour and day;
2. verified collateral capacity by facility after lien, eligibility, haircut, and cutoff;
3. capital under multiple current asset-value scenarios;
4. the effect of each liquidity action on realized loss, earnings, encumbrance, and repayment;
5. a credible recovery path with dates, owners, and failure triggers;
6. resolution options, operational dependencies, and depositor-access consequences;
7. which claimants are insured, uninsured, secured, unsecured, subordinated, or equity;
8. the cost and execution risk of purchase-and-assumption, payout, or bridge structures; and
9. the precise decision point at which additional liquidity ceases to buy useful time.

The decisive question is:

> Does this next dollar of borrowing bridge a temporary cash mismatch at a viable institution, or does it only delay closure while adding cost and encumbering assets that resolution will need?

No Bitcoin or Galoy application is needed. The authority, diagnosis, and loss-allocation boundaries should be understood on their own institutional terms.

## What must not be confused

1. **Central-bank loan versus capital:** a loan adds cash and debt; equity absorbs loss.
2. **Collateral face value versus borrowing capacity:** eligibility, valuation, haircut, lien, documentation, and time determine proceeds.
3. **Lender of last resort versus lender without limits:** access remains governed by authority, program terms, collateral, condition, purpose, and repayment analysis.
4. **Temporary liquidity versus restored viability:** meeting today’s payments does not prove the bank can survive tomorrow.
5. **Delay versus cure:** time is valuable only if there is a credible recovery, capital, transaction, or orderly-resolution use for it.
6. **Discount-window readiness versus automatic entitlement:** documents, pledged collateral, approvals, and tested operations create readiness; the Reserve Bank still applies its rules.
7. **Insured deposit versus every bank-related product:** insurance covers qualifying deposits, not investments or assets merely offered through a bank.
8. **Account count versus insurance amount:** coverage depends on depositor, insured bank, ownership category, and aggregation rules—not one limit per account number.
9. **Deposit insurance versus bank survival:** the bank can fail while insured depositors retain prompt access.
10. **Depositor continuity versus legal-entity continuity:** accounts can transfer even though the former bank is closed.
11. **Chartering authority versus receiver:** the appropriate chartering authority closes; the FDIC acts as receiver for the failed insured bank.
12. **Resolution versus ordinary bankruptcy:** FDIC receivership is a specialized statutory process with bank-specific powers and objectives.
13. **Purchase and assumption versus purchase of everything:** an acquirer can assume selected liabilities and purchase selected assets; the rest remains with the receiver.
14. **Bridge bank versus revived old bank:** a bridge is a temporary resolution institution after failure.
15. **Insured depositor protection versus shareholder protection:** shareholders are residual claimants and can lose their investment while insured deposits are paid.
16. **Function preservation versus bailout:** keeping payments and deposits available does not reveal who ultimately bears loss; analyze each class.
17. **Least cost versus least visible disruption:** the statutory comparison concerns cost to the Deposit Insurance Fund under the governing framework, not optics alone.
18. **Uninsured deposit versus guaranteed loss:** it is exposed, but actual recovery depends on the resolution transaction, asset recoveries, and priority.
19. **Systemic-risk exception versus standing universal insurance:** exceptional protection requires a specific legal process and does not rewrite ordinary coverage for every future failure.
20. **No taxpayer loss versus no economic cost:** the Deposit Insurance Fund, special assessments, creditors, acquirers, and the industry can bear costs even when the official statement says taxpayers do not.
21. **Fast closure versus unplanned closure:** confidential preparation, data quality, bidder access, and tested continuity arrangements can make a rapid weekend resolution possible.
22. **Liquidity analysis versus resolution analysis:** the first asks how to meet payments; the second asks how to transfer functions and distribute value once the bank cannot continue.
