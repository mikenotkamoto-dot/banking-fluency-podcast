This course is for a learner preparing for a possible marketing / business-development / AI-operator role around Galoy-style Bitcoin banking infrastructure. The first goal is not to learn Bitcoin. ## Primary lesson text

A bank is not mainly a website, a card, a branch, a vault, or a mobile interface. Those are surfaces. The bank itself is a regulated institution built around a balance sheet.

When a customer deposits $1,000, the bank does not simply put that exact money in a labeled box. The customer now has a claim on the bank. From the customer's perspective, it feels like an asset: “I have $1,000 in my account.” From the bank's perspective, it is a liability: “We owe this customer $1,000 on demand or according to the account terms.”

The bank then uses its balance sheet to hold reserves, buy securities, make loans, provide payments access, and earn revenue. A loan is different from a deposit. If the bank lends $10,000 to a borrower, that loan is an asset for the bank because the borrower owes the bank principal and interest. This is the first unlock: deposits are bank liabilities; loans are bank assets; banking is the management of that structure under uncertainty.

Banks make money largely by earning more on assets than they pay on liabilities, plus fees and services. But this simple model creates risks:

- borrowers may not repay;
- depositors may withdraw faster than expected;
- interest rates may move against the bank;
- securities may lose market value;
- operations may fail;
- fraud, cyber, vendor, or compliance failures may damage the institution;
- public confidence may collapse.

This is why banks, credit unions, and similar financial institutions are supervised. Regulators are not only checking paperwork. They are trying to preserve depositor or member confidence, payment-system stability, consumer protection, anti-money-laundering controls, operational resilience, and the safety and soundness of the institution.

For Galoy-style work, this matters because a bank or credit union does not buy “cool Bitcoin software” the way consumers buy an app. Institutional buyers ask:

- Does this create new balance-sheet risk?
- Does it touch deposits, payments, lending, custody, or customer/member assets?
- What controls prove that transactions are accurate?
- Can examiners understand the system?
- How does this integrate with existing core banking, compliance, audit, and reporting systems?
- What happens when something breaks?

Before Bitcoin becomes interesting to a regulated financial institution, it has to be translated into institutional categories: custody, payments, collateral, lending, treasury, settlement, operational controls, compliance obligations, vendor risk, customer protection, and auditability.

That is the point of this course.

## Key concepts to explain

### Bank

A regulated financial institution that accepts deposits, makes loans, provides payments/services, and manages risk under legal and supervisory constraints.

### Credit union

A member-owned financial cooperative. For this course, credit unions matter because Galoy publicly addresses bank and credit-union leaders, and because credit unions have their own regulator and supervisory framework, especially NCUA in the United States.

### Deposit

Money a customer places with a bank or member places with a credit union. Economically, it is a claim against the institution. For the institution, it is usually a liability.

### Loan

Money the institution lends to a borrower. Economically, it is a claim the institution has against the borrower. For the institution, it is usually an asset.

### Balance-sheet business

A business where assets, liabilities, equity/capital, liquidity, and risk transformation are central to the operating model.

### Supervision

Ongoing regulatory oversight of the institution’s safety, soundness, compliance, controls, risk management, and consumer/member protection.

## Institutional buyer lens

A bank or credit-union buyer does not start from “Is Bitcoin interesting?” They start from:

- Will this help us grow revenue or defend relevance?
- Will this create regulatory heat?
- Can our compliance team live with it?
- Can our core systems integrate with it?
- Can we explain it to our board and examiners?
- Can we operate it safely during stress?

Marketing and BD in this market must speak risk, controls, supervision, integration, and institutional workflow — not just innovation.
