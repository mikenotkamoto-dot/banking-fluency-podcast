# Banking Fluency — Day 014

## Title

Institutional forms II: trusts, fintechs, and vendors

## Place in the course

Day 013 established that similar products do not make banks and credit unions interchangeable: start with the exact legal entity, then identify its charter, ownership, powers, insurer, and supervisors. This lesson extends that identity discipline one step outward. A trust company can hold a specialized charter; a fintech can design the customer experience without holding a bank charter; and a vendor can operate critical infrastructure without acquiring the regulated institution's authority or accountability.

## Learning objective

Explain the structural difference among a trust institution, a fintech, and a service vendor; identify which legal entity holds each regulated power, customer obligation, asset, record, and control duty; and show why technology, branding, contracts, or partnerships do not automatically transfer a bank or credit union's charter, insurance, fiduciary authority, or supervisory responsibility.

## The coherent layer: authority follows the legal entity; accountability follows the regulated function

The words **trust**, **fintech**, and **vendor** describe different things:

- **Trust institution** can describe a legally chartered and supervised entity with fiduciary or related powers, but the exact charter, powers, insurance status, and state or federal regime must be verified.
- **Fintech** describes a technology-enabled business model or market category. It is not, by itself, a charter.
- **Vendor** describes a relationship to a customer organization: one entity supplies a service to another. It says nothing by itself about the vendor's own licenses or powers.

One company can occupy more than one category. A chartered trust bank may be technologically sophisticated. A fintech may be a bank vendor, a bank's agent, a lender under state law, a payments company, or merely a software provider. A core processor is a vendor, but another vendor may also be a regulated financial institution in its own right.

The reliable operating rule is:

> Never infer legal authority from the interface or category label. Map the function to the exact legal entity that performs it, the authority that permits it, the customer or asset relationship it creates, and the institution that remains accountable.

This prevents two opposite errors:

1. treating every finance-shaped app as a bank; and
2. treating every nonbank as an unimportant subcontractor even when it performs a critical activity, controls customer access, maintains the operative ledger, or supplies data on which the bank relies.

**Simplification label:** This pack uses a high-level U.S. framework, with national trust banks and U.S. bank–third-party relationships as the primary examples. It is educational, not legal, chartering, fiduciary, custody, deposit-insurance, accounting, licensing, consumer-protection, contracting, insolvency, or supervisory advice. State trust-company laws and money-transmission, lending, securities, commodities, insurance, privacy, and corporate regimes vary. Verify the exact entity, jurisdiction, charter or license, agreement, account records, current law, and official agency records.

## Build a function–entity–authority map

Before evaluating a product or partnership, make a table with one row for every material function.

| Function | Entity actually performing it | Claimed authority | Customer's legal counterparty | Where the asset or liability sits | Accountable control owner |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Open account | ? | Charter, agency agreement, or other authority? | ? | ? | ? |
| Receive customer funds | ? | Deposit-taking, trust, payments, or other authority? | ? | ? | ? |
| Maintain subledger | ? | Contractual service or regulated function? | ? | System of record? | ? |
| Make credit decision | ? | Bank power, lending license, agency, or service? | Named creditor? | Who owns the loan? | ? |
| Hold or administer assets | ? | Fiduciary, custody, bailment, contractual, or other capacity? | ? | On whose balance sheet, if any? | ? |
| Move money | ? | Bank rail access, payment license, agency, or vendor access? | ? | Which settlement account? | ? |
| Perform KYC or monitoring | ? | Delegated task under contract? | Bank's legal duty remains where? | Which records prove performance? | ? |
| Handle complaints and errors | ? | Contract and applicable law | Who owes correction or reimbursement? | ? | ? |

For each row, demand evidence rather than labels:

- legal name and organizational form;
- charter, license, registration, or statutory power;
- account agreement, trust instrument, loan note, custody agreement, and disclosures;
- official regulator or insurer record;
- general ledger and subledger ownership;
- settlement and omnibus-account structure;
- allocation of decisions, approvals, and exception handling;
- access to records and audit evidence;
- complaint, incident, and regulatory-escalation paths; and
- termination, data-return, asset-transfer, and customer-continuity plan.

A polished user journey can conceal a fragmented legal and operational chain. The map makes that chain inspectable.

## Trust institutions: specialized authority, not a synonym for an ordinary deposit bank

### “Trust company” is not one uniform national category

The phrase **trust company** can refer to materially different structures, including:

- a national bank whose operations are limited to those of a trust company and related activities;
- a federal savings association limited to trust activities;
- a state-chartered trust company under a particular state's law;
- a full-service bank exercising separately authorized fiduciary powers through a trust department; or
- a brand or affiliate whose name contains “trust” but whose precise legal authority still must be checked.

Therefore, the correct first question is not “Is it a trust company?” It is:

> Which legal entity holds which fiduciary or related power under which charter and for which client relationship?

The OCC's *Charters* booklet states that the OCC may approve a national bank limited to trust-company operations and related activities. A national trust bank generally does not accept deposits and need not have FDIC insurance, although some are insured. OCC Bulletin 2007-21 similarly describes a national trust bank as a national bank whose business primarily consists of fiduciary and related products and services; it says most do not offer loans or accept deposits and do not have FDIC insurance.

That immediately breaks a common shortcut:

- **National bank charter** does not necessarily mean a full-service deposit-and-loan business.
- **Trust bank** does not necessarily mean FDIC-insured deposits are offered.
- **No FDIC insurance** does not mean the entity lacks a federal charter or OCC supervision.

Deposit insurance protects qualifying deposit liabilities at an insured institution. It is not a general certificate covering every fiduciary, custody, advisory, or safekeeping relationship.

**Simplification label:** “Most national trust banks do not accept deposits” is an OCC description of the typical model, not a universal legal conclusion. Some trust banks are insured. Federal savings associations limited to trust activities are subject to a different insurance rule, and state-chartered trust institutions vary. Confirm the charter and insurance status in official records and the product's legal documents.

### Fiduciary capacity is a legal role

A **fiduciary** acts under a legally recognized capacity in which duties are owed in administering property or exercising entrusted authority. For national banks, 12 USC 92a and 12 CFR part 9 govern the OCC framework for fiduciary powers. The roles can include trustee, executor, administrator, registrar of stocks and bonds, guardian of estates, assignee, receiver, committee of estates of persons of unsound mind, and other capacities authorized under the governing framework.

The role matters because “holding assets” can describe several different legal relationships:

- **Trustee:** holds and administers trust property according to the trust instrument and applicable law, potentially with discretionary duties.
- **Executor or administrator:** administers a deceased person's estate under a will, court appointment, and applicable law.
- **Custodian or safekeeper:** holds or controls assets and performs agreed administrative functions; investment discretion and broader fiduciary duties depend on the mandate and law.
- **Investment manager or adviser:** may make or recommend investment decisions under a separate grant of authority and regulatory framework.
- **Transfer agent, registrar, or corporate trustee:** maintains ownership or instrument records and performs defined administrative duties for securities or debt arrangements.

The nouns are not interchangeable. Possession, recordkeeping, settlement authority, investment discretion, legal title, beneficial ownership, and fiduciary judgment may be split among different entities.

### Trust property is not automatically the institution's balance-sheet asset

Recall the deposit relationship from Day 002: when a customer deposits money in an ordinary bank account, the bank generally records a liability to the customer and can deploy the funding within its powers and risk controls.

A fiduciary or custody relationship is different. Assets administered for clients are not automatically owned by the trust institution or available to pay its general creditors merely because the institution controls records, keys, certificates, or investment decisions. The governing instrument and legal capacity determine title, beneficial ownership, segregation, permissible use, recordkeeping, and claims.

This changes the economic model:

- a commercial bank often earns material net interest income by funding assets with deposits and other liabilities;
- a national trust bank commonly earns fees for administration, custody, investment management, corporate trust, or related services;
- large client assets under administration can create substantial operational, legal, fiduciary, compliance, and cyber risk without appearing as equally large on-balance-sheet assets; and
- capital ratios based only on balance-sheet assets may not capture the full exposure created by off-balance-sheet fiduciary activity.

OCC Bulletin 2007-21 makes this last point directly: off-balance-sheet asset-management activities are not captured fully by ordinary balance-sheet capital-ratio calculations, so national trust-bank management and boards must separately assess capital and liquidity against the size, complexity, and risks of the business.

**Simplification label:** “Off balance sheet” does not mean risk-free, unrecorded, or outside regulation. Trust institutions maintain extensive books, subledgers, controls, reconciliations, and disclosures. Certain cash positions, indemnities, receivables, operating assets, deposits, guarantees, or other exposures can appear on balance sheet. The exact accounting and insolvency treatment depends on the role, documents, assets, and law.

### The trust control stack

A credible trust operation needs controls around the legal duty, not merely secure storage. The stack includes:

1. **Acceptance:** determine whether the institution has authority, expertise, operational capacity, and acceptable conflicts before accepting an account.
2. **Governing instrument:** identify what the trust, will, court order, custody agreement, indenture, or other instrument permits and requires.
3. **Asset onboarding:** establish title, ownership, valuation, restrictions, cost basis, tax data, and custody location.
4. **Segregation and recordkeeping:** distinguish each client's property and entitlements from the institution's own assets and from other clients' interests.
5. **Decision authority:** document who may invest, distribute, vote, lend, pledge, delegate, or approve exceptions.
6. **Cash control:** reconcile receipts, disbursements, pending cash, fees, and settlement accounts; prevent unauthorized use.
7. **Conflicts and self-dealing controls:** identify affiliate products, fee incentives, related parties, and prohibited or conditioned transactions.
8. **Review and administration:** test investments, distributions, beneficiaries, account terms, deadlines, and continuing suitability or prudence where applicable.
9. **Reporting and evidence:** produce statements, tax and regulatory reporting, audit trails, and proof of approvals.
10. **Resignation, transfer, or termination:** preserve records and assets through a controlled transition to a successor fiduciary, custodian, beneficiary, or other entitled party.

A trust platform can automate parts of this stack. It cannot decide by marketing copy that a legal duty no longer exists.

### Trust-company operator test

When someone proposes a “custody” or “trust” product, ask:

- Who is the exact trustee, custodian, adviser, subcustodian, recordkeeper, and beneficial owner?
- Which entity has legal title or control, and in what capacity?
- Which entity has discretion, and what limits that discretion?
- Which charter or license authorizes each role in each jurisdiction?
- Are client assets segregated from the operator's proprietary assets in law, books, and operations?
- Where does client cash sit while awaiting investment or distribution?
- Is that cash a deposit, fiduciary cash, money owed by a nonbank, or another claim?
- What insurance, if any, applies to which claim and failure event?
- Who reconciles the official books against custodians, subcustodians, banks, and market infrastructure?
- Who bears and remediates an unauthorized transfer, valuation error, lost record, or service outage?

The product word “custody” answers none of these by itself.

## Fintechs: a business model, not a charter

### The category error

A **fintech** is generally a company using technology to deliver or support financial products or operations. The label does not reveal whether the company is:

- an insured bank or credit union;
- a state-licensed nonbank lender;
- a money transmitter or payments company;
- a broker-dealer, investment adviser, or other functionally regulated entity;
- an agent or service provider to a bank;
- a program manager controlling parts of a bank-sponsored product;
- a data provider or decision engine;
- a software vendor with no direct customer-funds role; or
- some combination across separate legal entities.

Some chartered banks call themselves fintechs. Many fintechs are nonbanks. Therefore, “fintech versus bank” is often the wrong comparison. The exact legal entities and functions matter more than the company's self-description.

### A partner bank does not turn the fintech into an insured bank

A common arrangement puts a nonbank interface in front of a bank:

1. the fintech markets the product and operates the app;
2. the customer submits information through the fintech;
3. a partner bank may open or hold the deposit account, originate the loan, or access payment rails;
4. the fintech or another vendor may maintain a subledger and handle support;
5. additional processors, networks, identity providers, and cloud vendors perform supporting functions.

This can be a legitimate structure. But it does not merge the parties into one legal person.

The FDIC's official *Banking With Third-Party Apps* guidance states:

- nonbank companies themselves are never FDIC-insured;
- funds sent to a nonbank are not eligible for FDIC insurance until they are deposited at an FDIC-insured bank and other conditions are met;
- possible pass-through coverage depends on requirements including records identifying each owner and amount; and
- FDIC insurance against failure of the insured bank does not protect against the insolvency or bankruptcy of the nonbank company.

Therefore, four distinct failure events must be analyzed:

1. **Insured-bank failure:** qualifying deposits may be protected under FDIC rules.
2. **Nonbank failure:** FDIC insurance does not insure the fintech's solvency or guarantee immediate customer access through its systems.
3. **Operational failure:** bad records, frozen interfaces, reconciliation breaks, processor outages, or failed instructions can interrupt access even if the partner bank remains solvent.
4. **Fraud or misuse:** deposit insurance is not a general reimbursement scheme for every theft, misrepresentation, unauthorized transfer, or contractual breach.

A claim that a product is “FDIC insured” is incomplete unless it identifies the insured bank, the deposit relationship, the path and timing of funds, the ownership records, the coverage category, and the conditions for pass-through treatment.

**Simplification label:** This is a general teaching summary, not an account-specific insurance determination. FDIC coverage depends on the insured bank's failure and applicable statutes, regulations, account ownership, aggregation, records, and pass-through requirements. The FDIC determines coverage. Other laws or private protections may address some nonbank or transaction failures, but they are not FDIC deposit insurance.

### The fintech can control the experience without holding the regulated claim

The entity with the customer relationship in ordinary language may not be the customer's legal counterparty for every function.

For example:

- the fintech brand may control onboarding, but the bank may approve account opening;
- the fintech may calculate a credit score, but the bank may be the named creditor and make or approve the credit decision;
- the fintech may display a balance from its subledger, while the bank's omnibus or custodial account is the legal deposit on the bank's books;
- the fintech may handle first-line complaints, while the bank retains duties under consumer law for the bank product; and
- the fintech may initiate payment messages, while settlement occurs through accounts and rails controlled by regulated institutions.

This division creates **interface risk**: the customer sees one product, but legal rights and operational records are distributed across several entities.

The control response is not to pretend the distribution does not exist. It is to reconcile it continuously:

> customer entitlement → fintech subledger → processor records → bank deposit or loan system → settlement account → general ledger → regulatory and customer reporting

Every arrow is a potential break. Ownership totals, transaction status, fees, reversals, exceptions, dormant items, complaints, and failed payments must agree across systems.

## Vendors: delegated performance does not erase institutional responsibility

### Outsourcing is not transfer of charter

Banks and credit unions use third parties for core processing, cloud hosting, identity verification, lending workflows, payment operations, call centers, collections, compliance tools, fraud detection, model services, data aggregation, and many other functions.

The 2023 Federal Reserve–FDIC–OCC interagency guidance states the principle plainly: a banking organization's use of third parties does not diminish or remove its responsibility to conduct activities safely and soundly and in compliance with applicable law, including consumer-protection and customer-information requirements.

The bank can contract for performance. It cannot contract away:

- the bounds of its charter and powers;
- responsibility for safe and sound operation;
- compliance obligations attached to its product and activity;
- protection and integrity of customer information;
- accurate books and records;
- effective governance and risk management; or
- the need to provide evidence to supervisors.

This does not mean the vendor has no liability. Contracts, tort law, licenses, privacy law, sector rules, and enforcement authority can impose direct duties on the vendor. The point is narrower: the bank does not cease to be accountable for a bank activity merely because a third party performs it.

### The risk should follow the activity, not the procurement label

A contract can be called “software,” “marketing,” “consulting,” or “referral” while the vendor actually influences:

- who becomes a customer;
- what disclosures the customer sees;
- how eligibility or credit is decided;
- where funds are routed;
- what balance is displayed;
- how transactions are screened;
- whether complaints are recognized and escalated;
- which records exist; and
- whether the institution can continue operating during an outage.

Risk classification should follow those facts. A small annual contract can support a critical control. A famous vendor can still be difficult to audit or exit. A subcontractor the bank never selected directly can operate the most important technical dependency.

The interagency guidance uses a risk-based approach: management should tailor oversight to the relationship's risk, the supported activity's criticality, the bank's size and complexity, and the nature of the arrangement. Not every office-supply contract deserves the same review as a core processor. But “fintech” is not automatically high risk, and “ordinary vendor” is not automatically low risk.

### The third-party relationship life cycle

The agencies organize sound practice around a life cycle. Treat it as a continuous control system rather than a procurement checklist.

#### 1. Planning

Before selection, define:

- the business purpose and expected customer outcome;
- the activity, legal authority, and jurisdictions;
- which decisions or operations the third party will perform;
- risk appetite and prohibited designs;
- information, systems, and funds the third party can access;
- staffing and expertise the bank needs to oversee the arrangement;
- concentration and subcontractor dependencies;
- measurable service and control requirements; and
- an exit or continuity strategy.

A bank cannot conduct useful due diligence if it has not defined the actual function.

#### 2. Due diligence and selection

Evaluate facts relevant to the risk, potentially including:

- ownership, management, financial condition, and business experience;
- legal authority, licenses, regulatory history, litigation, and complaints;
- operational capacity, systems, cyber controls, resilience, and incident record;
- compliance-management system and subject-matter competence;
- data governance, privacy, retention, portability, and deletion;
- model methodology, validation, bias, explainability, and change control where relevant;
- subcontractors and geographic dependencies;
- insurance and financial resources;
- audit reports, testing, control evidence, and remediation history; and
- ability to meet the bank's reporting, examination, and termination needs.

Due diligence is not “the vendor has a SOC report.” Evidence must answer the risks of the specific activity.

#### 3. Contract negotiation

The contract should turn control expectations into enforceable rights and duties. Depending on the relationship, topics can include:

- scope, performance standards, and customer treatment;
- compliance with applicable laws and bank policies;
- ownership and use of data, records, intellectual property, and work product;
- audit, testing, access, and regulatory-examination rights;
- confidentiality, security, incident notification, and cooperation;
- pricing, fees, incentives, complaints, errors, and reimbursement;
- subcontractor approval or notice;
- business continuity and recovery objectives;
- indemnification, liability, and insurance;
- change control;
- default, cure, termination, transition assistance, and data return or destruction; and
- survival of obligations after termination.

A contract cannot make an impossible operating model safe. It can only allocate and enforce duties within a model the institution can actually oversee.

#### 4. Ongoing monitoring

Conditions change after signature. Monitoring should detect whether:

- service and control measures remain within limits;
- financial condition or ownership has changed;
- licenses, legal issues, complaints, incidents, or enforcement events have emerged;
- subcontractors or hosting locations have changed;
- systems, models, products, customer segments, or transaction volumes have drifted;
- audit findings are remediated;
- reconciliations and customer records remain accurate;
- concentration and substitutability have worsened; and
- the institution can still execute its contingency and exit plan.

Monitoring intensity should rise with risk and change. Annual questionnaire theater is not oversight of a rapidly evolving critical service.

#### 5. Termination and exit

Exit planning asks what happens when the relationship ends under stress, not just at a convenient renewal date:

- Can customer service continue?
- Can the bank obtain complete, accurate, usable records?
- Can balances and transactions be reconciled before migration?
- Who communicates with customers and regulators?
- Can data, keys, models, configuration, and operational knowledge move to the bank or a replacement?
- What happens to pending payments, disputes, complaints, and legal holds?
- How are access rights revoked and retained data deleted or preserved?
- What if the vendor is bankrupt, hostile, or technically unable to cooperate?

If the institution cannot exit without losing the ability to serve customers or prove its books, the vendor has become part of the institution's operational boundary whether procurement calls it strategic or not.

### Credit unions face the same structural issue

NCUA's active Letter 07-CU-13 explains that federally insured credit unions use third parties for lending, regulatory compliance, data processing, internet banking, cards, and bill pay. It frames planning, due diligence, and controls according to the credit union's risk profile and the vendor relationship, with attention to the service's critical nature, vendor expertise, staffing, economic and regulatory change, and risk mitigation.

The agency framework differs from the bank-agency guidance, but the operating principle is parallel:

> A credit union can use a qualified third party to expand capability; it still must protect members, operations, and the Share Insurance Fund through risk-appropriate governance.

Do not copy a bank's regulator map into a credit union engagement. Do carry forward the discipline of mapping the function, authority, member relationship, control owner, evidence, and exit path.

## Three boundaries that must remain visible

### Boundary 1: chartered institution versus nonbank interface

Ask who holds the chartered power and regulated claim.

- A bank deposit is a liability of the named bank—not of the app merely displaying it.
- A trust account is governed by the exact fiduciary or custody relationship—not by the word “trust” in a brand.
- A loan belongs to the named creditor or lawful assignee—not necessarily the company that designed the application screen.

### Boundary 2: customer asset or claim versus service provider records

Ask which record is legally operative and how records reconcile.

A fintech subledger may be essential for assigning interests in an omnibus bank account. A processor may maintain transaction history. A trust platform may calculate beneficiary positions. Those records can be operationally critical without themselves changing who owns the underlying asset or owes the underlying liability.

The institution needs both:

- **legal clarity:** agreements and authority establish the rights; and
- **operational integrity:** accurate records make those rights executable.

A sound contract with a broken ledger still harms customers. A precise ledger without lawful authority does not cure an unauthorized activity.

### Boundary 3: delegated task versus retained accountability

Ask who performs, who decides, who verifies, and who answers to the customer and supervisor.

A vendor can collect identity data. The institution must still know whether its customer-identification and financial-crime controls work. A model vendor can produce a score. The creditor must still govern the credit decision and applicable fair-lending and consumer duties. A call center can receive complaints. The institution must still identify, aggregate, investigate, remediate, and learn from them.

“Vendor did it” is an incident fact, not a governance defense.

## Worked scenario: one app, four legal and operational layers

A company called Northstar offers a mobile “wealth cash account.” Its marketing says customers receive safekeeping, competitive yield, bill payment, and “bank-level protection.” The proposed structure is:

- **Northstar Technologies LLC**, a nonbank fintech, owns the app, markets the product, performs onboarding, and handles first-line support.
- **Guardian National Trust Bank**, an OCC-chartered national trust bank, provides custody and investment administration. It generally does not take ordinary retail deposits and is not FDIC-insured.
- **Harbor State Bank**, an FDIC-insured state nonmember bank, holds pooled customer cash awaiting investment and provides payment access.
- **LedgerWorks Inc.**, a technology vendor, maintains the customer subledger and reconciliation engine.
- The product invests some customer funds in securities selected under the governing agreements.

A weak analysis says, “There is a national trust bank and an FDIC-insured bank, so Northstar is a regulated, insured bank account.”

A disciplined analysis separates every layer.

### Step 1: identify the legal relationships

Determine from executed documents:

- whether the customer contracts with Northstar, Guardian, Harbor, or each for different functions;
- whether Guardian acts as trustee, custodian, adviser, agent, or in another capacity;
- whether Harbor's account is titled to Guardian, Northstar, a custodial nominee, or another entity;
- who is the beneficial owner of cash and securities;
- who owns the securities and who has investment discretion;
- which entity owes payment, correction, reporting, and complaint duties; and
- which terms control if marketing and legal documents conflict.

### Step 2: test each entity's authority

- Guardian's OCC charter and fiduciary powers must cover the role it actually performs.
- Harbor's state-bank powers and applicable law must cover the deposit and payment services.
- Northstar's activities must be tested against every applicable nonbank licensing and consumer-law requirement; calling it a vendor is not an exemption.
- LedgerWorks' role must be tested by function, data access, criticality, and any direct legal duties—not merely by the label “software provider.”

### Step 3: classify each customer asset or claim

Split the displayed balance into components:

1. cash actually deposited at Harbor;
2. cash in transit to Harbor;
3. securities held through Guardian's custody structure;
4. accrued but unpaid interest, yield, rebates, or fees;
5. pending payments, reversals, or holds; and
6. any amount owed directly by Northstar or another nonbank.

These components do not necessarily receive the same legal, accounting, insurance, or insolvency treatment.

### Step 4: correct the insurance statement

Potential FDIC coverage concerns qualifying deposit funds at Harbor if all requirements are satisfied. It does not automatically cover:

- Guardian merely because it is a national trust bank;
- Northstar or LedgerWorks as companies;
- securities or market losses;
- cash not yet deposited at Harbor;
- failure to meet pass-through recordkeeping or ownership conditions;
- operational inaccessibility caused by the fintech or ledger vendor; or
- every customer balance merely because the app shows one number.

The disclosure must identify Harbor and explain the conditions without implying that Northstar or the whole product is FDIC-insured.

### Step 5: reconcile the control chain

At a minimum, the parties need daily and event-driven proof that:

- the sum of customer cash entitlements equals the appropriate pooled-bank-account position after explained reconciling items;
- securities positions agree across customer records, Guardian's books, subcustodians, and market records;
- cash and security movements are authorized under the governing agreements;
- fees, yield, distributions, holds, and pending transactions are assigned correctly;
- exceptions are aged, owned, escalated, and resolved;
- no customer asset is used for another party's obligation without lawful authority; and
- customer statements and regulatory reports derive from controlled sources.

### Step 6: assign accountability

For each control, name both the performer and the accountable institution. Examples:

- Northstar may collect onboarding data; Harbor and Guardian remain responsible for the controls applicable to their customer and activity relationships.
- LedgerWorks may calculate entitlements; Harbor and Guardian need sufficient access, reconciliation, testing, and contingency capability to trust and replace the calculation.
- Northstar may receive complaints; each institution must receive relevant complaints promptly and ensure investigation and remediation.
- Guardian may delegate subcustody; it must govern the delegation under its role and applicable authority.

### Step 7: test failure modes separately

Run at least these scenarios:

- Harbor fails.
- Guardian fails or resigns.
- Northstar enters bankruptcy.
- LedgerWorks loses records or suffers a prolonged outage.
- The subledger and bank balance diverge.
- Customer cash remains in transit when a party fails.
- A cyber incident compromises authorization data.
- The parties terminate the relationship during unresolved complaints and pending payments.

For each, ask who has the records, who can access the assets, which legal process applies, what customers are told, and how continuity is restored.

### Step 8: make the customer claim precise

A defensible description would separate:

- the nonbank app provider;
- the national trust bank and its exact fiduciary or custody role;
- the FDIC-insured bank holding qualifying deposits;
- the non-deposit investment component;
- the conditions and limits of any pass-through insurance; and
- the operational dependencies that insurance does not cover.

The lesson is not that multi-entity products are inherently defective. It is that their safety depends on making the legal and operational seams explicit and controlled.

No Bitcoin or Galoy application is needed. The institutional boundary is foundational on its own terms.

## What must not be confused

1. **Trust company versus one universal charter:** federal and state structures, powers, and insurance status differ.
2. **National bank versus full-service deposit bank:** a national trust bank can be limited to fiduciary and related activities.
3. **OCC supervision versus FDIC insurance:** a trust bank can be OCC-chartered and supervised without being FDIC-insured.
4. **Fiduciary asset versus deposit:** property administered in a fiduciary capacity is not automatically an ordinary deposit liability.
5. **Assets under administration versus bank assets:** operational control or recordkeeping does not automatically put client property on the institution's proprietary balance sheet.
6. **Off balance sheet versus no risk:** fiduciary, legal, operational, cyber, and compliance exposure can be substantial.
7. **Trustee versus custodian:** legal title, discretion, duties, and administration depend on the actual capacity and agreement.
8. **Custody versus guaranteed value:** safekeeping does not eliminate market, issuer, legal, operational, or counterparty risk.
9. **Fintech versus nonbank in every case:** some technology-oriented companies own chartered institutions; inspect the legal entity.
10. **Fintech label versus authority:** technology does not itself confer deposit-taking, fiduciary, lending, payments, or investment powers.
11. **Partner bank versus fintech insurance:** a nonbank partner is not FDIC-insured because it works with an insured bank.
12. **Funds promised to a bank versus funds deposited at a bank:** timing, ownership, placement, and records matter.
13. **Pass-through possibility versus automatic coverage:** statutory and recordkeeping conditions still apply.
14. **Bank-failure insurance versus nonbank-failure protection:** FDIC insurance does not insure a fintech's solvency or app availability.
15. **Customer interface versus legal counterparty:** the visible brand may not hold the deposit, loan, or custody obligation.
16. **Subledger balance versus bank balance:** customer entitlements and the pooled account must reconcile; one screen is not proof.
17. **Vendor contract versus charter transfer:** a contract can delegate tasks, not create the bank's powers in the vendor.
18. **Outsourcing versus accountability transfer:** the regulated institution remains responsible for safe, lawful operation of its activity.
19. **Low contract value versus low criticality:** a cheap dependency can control a critical process.
20. **Large vendor versus easy oversight:** scale and reputation do not replace activity-specific evidence.
21. **SOC report versus complete due diligence:** one assurance report covers defined systems, controls, periods, and scope—not every risk.
22. **Contract clause versus operational capability:** audit and exit rights are useful only if the institution can exercise them.
23. **Direct vendor versus full dependency chain:** subcontractors and cloud or data providers can carry material risk.
24. **Annual review versus ongoing monitoring:** rapid changes, incidents, complaints, and control drift require timely detection.
25. **Normal termination versus stressed exit:** records, customer service, pending transactions, and access must survive vendor distress.
26. **Same technology versus same regulatory map:** banks, credit unions, trust institutions, and nonbanks can use the same software under different authority and accountability structures.

These sources anchor the lesson. The function–entity–authority map, control stack, scenario, and tests above are educational synthesis and simplification—not agency findings, legal opinions, charter determinations, or insurance rulings.

1. **Office of the Comptroller of the Currency — Comptroller's Licensing Manual, “Charters” (December 2021).** Primary OCC licensing source on full-service and special-purpose national banks and federal savings associations. The trust-bank section explains limited fiduciary operations, fiduciary-powers authority, typical deposit and insurance status, capital and liquidity, third-party reliance, and OCC supervision.  
   https://www.occ.gov/publications-and-resources/publications/comptrollers-licensing-manual/files/licensing-booklet-charters.html

2. **Office of the Comptroller of the Currency — OCC Bulletin 2007-21, “Supervision of National Trust Banks: Revised Guidance: Capital and Liquidity.”** Official description of national trust-bank fiduciary and related services; notes that most do not offer loans or accept deposits and are not FDIC-insured; explains why off-balance-sheet activity, fee income, capital, liquidity, and contingency planning require specialized analysis.  
   https://www.occ.treas.gov/news-issuances/bulletins/2007/bulletin-2007-21.html

3. **Electronic Code of Federal Regulations — 12 CFR Part 9, “Fiduciary Activities of National Banks.”** Current official regulatory text governing national-bank fiduciary activities, including definitions, fiduciary powers, administration, recordkeeping, custody, audit, and related requirements.  
   https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-12/chapter-I/part-9

4. **Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation — “Banking With Third-Party Apps” (May 31, 2024).** Official explanation that nonbank companies are never FDIC-insured; funds sent to a nonbank are not eligible until deposited at an insured bank and other conditions are met; pass-through treatment depends on records and ownership; and FDIC insurance does not protect against nonbank insolvency or bankruptcy.  
   https://www.fdic.gov/consumer-resource-center/2024-06/banking-third-party-apps

5. **Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation — FIL-29-2023, “Interagency Guidance on Third-Party Relationships: Risk Management” (June 6, 2023).** Official FDIC summary and link to the joint Federal Reserve–FDIC–OCC guidance. States that using a third party does not diminish the banking organization's responsibility and identifies planning, due diligence, contract negotiation, ongoing monitoring, and termination as life-cycle stages.  
   https://www.fdic.gov/news/financial-institution-letters/2023/fil23029.html

6. **Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System — SR 23-4, “Interagency Guidance on Third-Party Relationships: Risk Management” (June 7, 2023).** Federal Reserve issuance of the joint guidance; applies the principles to all types of third-party relationships and specifically notes novel structures involving fintech companies and varying interaction with bank customers.  
   https://www.federalreserve.gov/supervisionreg/srletters/SR2304.htm

7. **Office of the Comptroller of the Currency — OCC Bulletin 2023-17, “Third-Party Relationships: Interagency Guidance on Risk Management” (June 6, 2023).** OCC issuance summarizing risk-proportionate third-party governance and the life cycle for OCC-supervised banks, including bank–fintech arrangements.  
   https://www.occ.treas.gov/news-issuances/bulletins/2023/bulletin-2023-17.html

8. **National Credit Union Administration — Letter 07-CU-13, “Evaluating Third Party Relationships” (December 2007; active).** Official NCUA guidance for federally insured credit unions on planning, due diligence, controls, service criticality, vendor expertise, changing conditions, and risk mitigation in third-party relationships.  
   https://ncua.gov/regulation-supervision/letters-credit-unions-other-guidance/evaluating-third-party-relationships
